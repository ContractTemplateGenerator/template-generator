OFAC Sanctions Screening for Trading Platforms

📅 Updated Dec 2025 ⏱ 18 min read ⚠ Critical Compliance

OFAC Overview & Authority

The Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) is a division of the U.S. Department of the Treasury that administers and enforces economic and trade sanctions based on U.S. foreign policy and national security goals. For trading platforms and financial institutions, OFAC compliance is not optional—it's a federal legal requirement with severe civil and criminal penalties for violations.

OFAC operates under presidential national emergency powers and specific legislation to impose controls on transactions and freeze assets under U.S. jurisdiction. Sanctions can be country-based (comprehensive sanctions against entire countries) or list-based (targeting specific individuals, entities, vessels, and aircraft).

⚠ Strict Liability Standard

OFAC violations are subject to strict liability. This means you can be penalized even if the violation was unintentional or you had no knowledge that a transaction involved a sanctioned party. Lack of awareness is not a defense. This makes robust screening and due diligence essential.

OFAC's Legal Authority

OFAC derives its authority from several key statutes:

  • Trading with the Enemy Act of 1917 - Authority during wartime and national emergencies
  • International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) - Primary authority for most modern sanctions programs
  • USA PATRIOT Act - Enhanced counter-terrorism financial controls
  • Foreign Narcotics Kingpin Designation Act - Sanctions against international drug traffickers
  • Countering America's Adversaries Through Sanctions Act (CAATSA) - Russia, Iran, North Korea sanctions

💡 Who Must Comply?

OFAC regulations apply to all U.S. persons—including citizens, permanent residents, entities organized under U.S. law, and any person within the United States—as well as foreign entities owned or controlled by U.S. persons. If your trading platform serves U.S. customers or operates in U.S. jurisdiction, you must comply with OFAC sanctions.

Sanctions Programs & Lists

OFAC maintains multiple sanctions programs and publishes several lists that financial institutions must screen against. Understanding the differences between these lists and programs is critical for effective compliance.

Types of Sanctions Programs

Program Type Description Examples
Comprehensive Sanctions Broad prohibitions on virtually all transactions with a target country Cuba, Iran, North Korea, Syria, Russia (partial)
List-Based Sanctions Target specific individuals, entities, and vessels regardless of location SDN List, Sectoral Sanctions Identifications (SSI) List
Sectoral Sanctions Prohibit specific types of transactions with designated entities in certain sectors Russia energy, financial, and defense sectors
Secondary Sanctions Target non-U.S. persons for activities with sanctioned jurisdictions Iran-related secondary sanctions, Russia-related measures

OFAC Lists Comparison

List Name Full Name Entries Prohibition
SDN List Specially Designated Nationals and Blocked Persons List ~11,000+ All transactions prohibited; assets must be blocked
SSI List Sectoral Sanctions Identifications List ~300+ Limited prohibitions based on directive (e.g., debt financing over 90 days)
FSE List Foreign Sanctions Evaders List ~90 All transactions prohibited
Non-SDN Menu-Based Sanctions Non-SDN entities subject to menu-based sanctions Varies Certain sanctions apply (varies by program)
CAPTA List Non-SDN Chinese Military-Industrial Complex Companies ~60 Prohibition on securities transactions by U.S. persons

⚠ The 50% Rule

OFAC's "50% Rule" states that entities owned 50% or more, directly or indirectly, by one or more SDNs are themselves considered blocked, even if not explicitly listed. You must screen beneficial ownership structures, not just direct names.

Screening Requirements for Financial Institutions

While OFAC does not prescribe specific screening procedures, it expects financial institutions to implement a risk-based OFAC compliance program appropriate to their size, sophistication, products, services, customers, and geographic locations.

Core Screening Obligations

  • Customer Onboarding Screening - Screen all new customers against OFAC lists before establishing a relationship
  • Transaction Screening - Screen payment parties, beneficiaries, and intermediaries in real-time or near-real-time
  • Ongoing/Batch Screening - Periodically re-screen existing customers as OFAC lists are updated
  • Trade Finance Screening - Screen parties in letters of credit, trade documents, and shipping information
  • Wire Transfer Screening - Screen originators, beneficiaries, and intermediary banks in wire messages
  • Securities Screening - Screen issuers, counterparties, and beneficial owners in securities transactions

💡 Trading Platform Specific Requirements

For trading platforms, screening must cover: (1) account holders at onboarding, (2) beneficial owners of entity accounts, (3) counterparties in trades, (4) payment originators and beneficiaries, (5) custodians and service providers, and (6) issuers of traded securities. Automated screening integrated into your platform workflow is essential.

When to Screen

Account Opening
  • Before establishing relationship
  • Screen individual and entity names
  • Check beneficial owners (25%+ ownership)
  • Verify no SDN matches
Transaction Processing
  • Real-time screening of payments
  • Screen all parties in wire transfers
  • Check trade counterparties
  • Review payment instructions
Periodic Review
  • Daily or weekly batch screening
  • Re-screen when OFAC lists update
  • Annual customer re-screening
  • Ongoing monitoring for changes
Triggering Events
  • Customer name or address change
  • New beneficial owner disclosure
  • Unusual transaction patterns
  • New sanctions program announced

Name Matching & Fuzzy Logic

Effective OFAC screening requires sophisticated name-matching technology because names can be spelled differently, transliterated from other alphabets, abbreviated, or contain minor errors. A simple exact-match search is insufficient.

Matching Techniques

Technique Description Use Case
Exact Match Character-for-character identical Initial filter; insufficient alone
Fuzzy Matching Allows for variations, misspellings, and typos using algorithms like Levenshtein distance Primary screening method; detects close variants
Phonetic Matching Matches names that sound similar (Soundex, Metaphone algorithms) Names from different alphabets or transliterations
Nickname/AKA Matching Database of known aliases and alternate spellings Individuals using multiple names
Weak Alias Matching Broader matching for low-quality aliases listed by OFAC When OFAC includes uncertain or variant names

Common Screening Challenges

  • Transliteration Issues - Arabic, Cyrillic, Chinese names can be spelled multiple ways in Latin alphabet
  • Name Order Variations - Family name first vs. given name first varies by culture
  • Partial Names - Single names, initials only, or incomplete data
  • Corporate Name Variations - Abbreviations (Corp, Ltd, Inc), punctuation differences
  • Address Matching - Street abbreviations, city name changes, transliteration issues
  • Date of Birth Variations - Different date formats, incomplete dates (year only)

⚠ False Positive Management

Aggressive fuzzy matching generates high false positive rates—often 95%+ of initial matches are false positives. You need trained personnel and clear procedures to efficiently review and clear false matches while ensuring true matches are escalated and addressed. Document your decision-making process.

Screening Software Considerations

Trading platforms should implement or integrate with commercial OFAC screening software that provides:

  • Real-Time Screening - API-based integration for account opening and transaction processing
  • Automatic List Updates - Daily updates when OFAC publishes new sanctions
  • Configurable Matching Thresholds - Balance between false positives and false negatives
  • Case Management Workflow - Track alerts, investigations, and resolutions
  • Audit Trail - Comprehensive logs of all screening and decisions
  • Reporting Capabilities - Metrics on screening volume, alerts, and resolution times

Geographic Blocking Requirements

In addition to list-based screening, OFAC enforces comprehensive country-based sanctions that prohibit most or all transactions with certain jurisdictions.

Current Comprehensive Sanctions Programs

Country/Region Scope Trading Platform Implications
Cuba Broad restrictions on trade, investment, and travel Block Cuban nationals and residents; prohibit securities transactions
Iran Comprehensive sanctions on nearly all transactions Block all Iranian persons and entities; no trading services
North Korea Comprehensive ban on virtually all economic activity Complete prohibition on accounts and transactions
Syria Broad sanctions on petroleum, financial services, investment Block Syrian government and related parties; prohibit new investment
Crimea/Sevastopol Investment and importation ban Block persons operating in region; no securities from the region
Russia (Partial) Sectoral and targeted sanctions; not comprehensive but extensive Screen against SDN/SSI; prohibit certain debt/equity transactions

💡 IP Address Blocking vs. Nationality

Geographic blocking must consider both physical location (detectable via IP address, billing address) and nationality/residence. A person physically in the U.S. but who is an Iranian national may still be restricted. Conversely, a U.S. citizen accessing your platform from Iran may trigger concerns. Implement controls for both dimensions.

Geographic Screening Implementation

  • IP Geolocation - Block connections from sanctioned countries at account creation and login
  • Address Verification - Screen customer addresses against sanctioned regions
  • Nationality Checks - Collect and verify customer citizenship and residency
  • Payment Routing - Block payments to/from banks in sanctioned countries
  • VPN/Proxy Detection - Identify attempts to circumvent geographic restrictions
  • Sanctions-Compliant Terms - Include geographic restrictions in user agreements

Rejected Transaction Reporting

When your screening identifies a potential OFAC match, you must take immediate action. The type of action depends on whether the match involves blocking (SDN) or rejection (non-SDN sanctioned party).

Rejection vs. Blocking

Action When Required Procedure
Rejection Transaction involves a sanctioned country but not an SDN Reject/refuse the transaction; do not process; no blocking report required (but maintain records)
Blocking Transaction involves property or interests in property of an SDN Block assets, prohibit transaction, and file blocking report within 10 business days

Rejected Transaction Documentation

Even when a blocking report is not required (rejected transactions), you must maintain records:

  • Date of rejection
  • Name and identifying information of the sanctioned party
  • Nature of the transaction that was rejected
  • Value and currency
  • Reason for rejection (which sanctions program)
  • Internal investigation and decision-making documentation

⚠ No "Tipping Off"

Do not inform the customer or any involved party that the transaction was rejected due to OFAC sanctions, as this could constitute "tipping off" and potentially aid sanctions evasion. Provide a generic reason such as "unable to process" or "compliance review required."

Blocked Assets Reporting (Within 10 Days)

When you identify that you are in possession or control of any property or property interests of an SDN, you must block (freeze) those assets and report the blocking to OFAC within 10 business days.

What Must Be Blocked

  • Account Balances - Funds held in accounts owned by or for SDNs
  • Securities Positions - Stocks, bonds, or other securities owned by SDNs
  • Pending Transactions - Payments to or from SDNs in process
  • Property Interests - Any other assets or interests in property (broadly defined)

⚠ Immediate Action Required

Blocking must occur immediately upon identification of an SDN match. Do not wait for the 10-day reporting deadline. The account/assets must be frozen instantly, and the report is due within 10 business days after blocking.

Blocked Assets Reporting Timeline

Day 0 (Immediate)
Block the assets - Freeze account, halt transactions, prevent any dealing
Within 10 business days
File blocking report - Submit initial report to OFAC using approved format
Annually (by Sept 30)
Annual report of blocked property - Report all blocked property held as of June 30
Ongoing
Maintain blocked status - Assets remain blocked until OFAC authorizes release

Blocking Report Contents

Initial blocking reports to OFAC must include:

  • Identity of the blocked person (name, address, other identifying information)
  • Date of blocking action
  • Description of the property blocked (account numbers, securities positions, amounts)
  • Estimated or actual value of blocked property
  • Your contact information
  • Supporting documentation

Blocked Assets Compliance Checklist

  • Implement automated alerts when SDN match is identified
  • Freeze all account activity immediately (withdrawals, trades, transfers)
  • Segregate blocked funds in internal accounting
  • File initial blocking report with OFAC within 10 business days
  • Do not release or unblock without OFAC license or guidance
  • Maintain detailed records of all blocked property
  • Submit annual reports by September 30 each year
  • Respond promptly to any OFAC inquiries regarding blocked property

License Application Process

In certain situations, you or your customer may need to request a license from OFAC to engage in a transaction that would otherwise be prohibited. OFAC issues two types of licenses: general licenses (pre-authorized categories) and specific licenses (individual applications).

General Licenses vs. Specific Licenses

License Type Description Application Required?
General License Pre-authorized categories of transactions published in sanctions regulations No application needed; can rely on published authorization if transaction fits
Specific License Individual authorization for a specific transaction or set of transactions Yes; must submit detailed application and await approval

Common Specific License Scenarios

  • Unblocking of Mistakenly Blocked Funds - When you determine a block was in error but need OFAC confirmation
  • Closing Blocked Accounts - Authorization to liquidate and transfer blocked assets
  • Humanitarian Transactions - Limited transactions for humanitarian purposes
  • Legal Services - Payment for legal representation of blocked persons
  • Wind-Down Activities - Limited authority to complete pre-existing contracts after new sanctions imposed

Specific License Application Process

OFAC License Application Workflow

1Determine if General License Applies

Review published general licenses in relevant sanctions program; if none applies, proceed with specific license application

2Gather Required Information

Collect detailed information about the transaction, parties involved, justification, and supporting documentation

3Submit Application via OFAC Portal

Use OFAC's online licensing portal or submit by mail; include all required attachments and supporting documents

4OFAC Review (90-180 days typical)

OFAC reviews application; may request additional information; review times vary widely

5Receive Decision

OFAC issues written approval, denial, or return without action; if approved, comply with all conditions

💡 No Presumption of Approval

Submitting a license application does not authorize you to proceed with the transaction while awaiting OFAC's decision. The transaction remains prohibited until and unless OFAC grants a specific license. Plan for potentially lengthy review periods.

OFAC Compliance Program Framework

OFAC published a Framework for OFAC Compliance Commitments outlining five essential components of an effective sanctions compliance program. This framework is used by OFAC to assess your compliance efforts and can be a mitigating factor in enforcement actions.

Five Pillars of OFAC Compliance

1. Management Commitment
  • Tone from the top
  • Adequate resources allocated
  • Clear compliance policies
  • Board/senior management oversight
  • Compliance integrated into culture
2. Risk Assessment
  • Identify sanctions risks
  • Assess customer base
  • Evaluate products/services
  • Geographic risk analysis
  • Periodic reassessment
3. Internal Controls
  • Screening procedures
  • Escalation protocols
  • Recordkeeping systems
  • Payment filters
  • Manual review processes
4. Testing & Auditing
  • Independent testing
  • Periodic audits
  • Control effectiveness review
  • Issue remediation tracking
  • Continuous improvement
5. Training
  • Role-specific training
  • Initial onboarding
  • Annual refresher training
  • Updates on sanctions changes
  • Training documentation

Risk-Based Approach

OFAC expects your compliance program to be tailored to your risk profile. Higher-risk institutions (e.g., international wire transfer businesses, virtual currency exchangers, trade finance providers) require more sophisticated controls than lower-risk businesses.

Risk Factors to Consider

Risk Factor Higher Risk Lower Risk
Customer Base International customers, high-risk jurisdictions, complex entities Domestic individuals, well-established companies
Products/Services International wire transfers, trade finance, correspondent banking Domestic securities, closed-loop systems
Geographic Reach Operations in high-risk countries, cross-border transactions U.S.-only operations, no international exposure
Delivery Channels Online/app-based with global access, limited identity verification In-person relationship, robust KYC
Transaction Volume High volume, automated processing Low volume, manual review feasible

✅ Document Your Risk Assessment

Conduct and document a formal OFAC risk assessment annually. Identify your specific risk factors, explain how your controls address those risks, and demonstrate that your program is appropriately calibrated. This documentation is critical during OFAC examinations or investigations.

Penalties & Enforcement Actions

OFAC has broad enforcement authority and routinely imposes significant civil penalties for sanctions violations. Criminal penalties are also possible for willful violations.

Civil Penalty Framework

Violation Category Maximum Penalty (Per Violation) Notes
IEEPA Violations (most programs) Greater of $356,579 or twice the transaction amount Adjusted annually for inflation
Trading with the Enemy Act $105,083 per violation Cuba program primarily
Foreign Narcotics Kingpin $1,783,063 per violation Drug trafficking-related sanctions

⚠ Criminal Penalties

Willful violations can result in criminal penalties: up to $1 million in fines and 20 years imprisonment for corporate officers. "Willful" includes reckless disregard or willful blindness to sanctions requirements—not just intentional violations.

Factors Affecting Penalty Amount

OFAC's Economic Sanctions Enforcement Guidelines outline factors considered in penalty determinations:

Aggravating Factors (Increase Penalties)

  • Willful or reckless violation
  • Awareness of conduct at management level
  • Harm to sanctions program objectives
  • Individual has received a penalty notice or finding of violation in the past five years
  • Large transaction amount or number of violations
  • Concealment or pattern of conduct

Mitigating Factors (Reduce Penalties)

  • Voluntary self-disclosure to OFAC
  • Cooperation with OFAC investigation
  • Remedial response (improved compliance program)
  • Investment in compliance prior to violation
  • No prior OFAC violations
  • Small transaction amount
  • One-time, non-egregious violation

Recent Enforcement Case Studies

Case Study 1: BitPay, Inc. (2023)

Violation: Cryptocurrency payment processor allowed over 2,100 transactions involving persons in sanctioned jurisdictions (Crimea, Cuba, North Korea, Iran, Sudan, Syria) totaling over $129,000.

Issue: BitPay's sanctions screening program failed to block transactions based on IP addresses in sanctioned regions. The company relied on customer self-certification of location without adequate verification.

Penalty: $507,375 settlement

Case Study 2: E*Trade Securities (2020)

Violation: Online brokerage processed 486 transactions totaling approximately $1.3 million on behalf of Syrians and individuals located in Crimea, violating Syria and Ukraine-related sanctions.

Issue: E*Trade failed to screen customer addresses and relied solely on customer self-certification. Inadequate OFAC compliance program for online platform with global accessibility.

Penalty: $400,000 settlement

Case Study 3: PayPal (2015)

Violation: Payment processor allowed 486 transactions involving persons in Cuba, Sudan, and Iran, totaling $7,091 between 2009-2013.

Issue: Despite having OFAC compliance procedures, PayPal's screening failed to identify sanctioned jurisdictions in certain account and transaction information fields.

Penalty: $7,658,300 settlement (over 1,000x transaction value)

⚠ Key Lesson from Enforcement Actions

Online platforms and fintech companies are frequent OFAC enforcement targets because their global accessibility increases sanctions risk. Self-certification of customer location is insufficient—implement IP geolocation, address verification, and robust identity verification. Document your risk assessment and control design decisions.

Voluntary Self-Disclosure Benefits

If you discover a potential OFAC violation, voluntary self-disclosure to OFAC can significantly reduce penalties. OFAC's guidelines provide that voluntary self-disclosure is a strong mitigating factor, and in some cases OFAC issues a "No Action" or "Cautionary" letter with no monetary penalty.

Implementation Checklist

Use this comprehensive checklist to implement or enhance your OFAC sanctions screening program.

OFAC Compliance Program Checklist

  • Designate a Sanctions Compliance Officer with authority and resources
  • Conduct written OFAC risk assessment for your business
  • Implement automated screening software integrated into workflows
  • Screen all customers at onboarding against SDN and other OFAC lists
  • Screen all transactions in real-time or near-real-time
  • Configure fuzzy logic and name-matching algorithms appropriately
  • Establish procedures for investigating and resolving screening alerts
  • Implement IP geolocation and geographic blocking for sanctioned countries
  • Collect and verify customer address and nationality information
  • Subscribe to OFAC list updates and re-screen customers when lists change
  • Develop written procedures for blocking assets and reporting to OFAC
  • Create alert escalation procedures for potential SDN matches
  • Train all relevant personnel on OFAC requirements annually
  • Maintain comprehensive audit trail of all screening and decisions
  • Conduct independent testing or audit of OFAC controls annually
  • Monitor OFAC guidance and sanctions program updates
  • Include OFAC compliance terms in customer agreements
  • Develop procedures for license applications when needed
  • Establish recordkeeping systems for rejected and blocked transactions
  • Prepare incident response plan for potential violations

✅ Next Steps

Start by conducting an OFAC risk assessment for your trading platform. Based on your risk profile, select appropriate screening technology, develop written procedures, and train your team. OFAC compliance is an ongoing commitment—not a one-time project. Regular testing, training, and updates are essential.
