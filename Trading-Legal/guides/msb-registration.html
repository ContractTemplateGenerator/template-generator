Money Services Business Registration Guide

Updated Dec 2025 28 min read FinCEN Compliance

What is a Money Services Business?

A Money Services Business (MSB) is a person or entity that engages in certain financial services activities as a business. Under the Bank Secrecy Act (BSA), administered by the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN), MSBs are subject to federal registration and comprehensive anti-money laundering (AML) compliance requirements.

If your trading platform, fintech application, or financial services business falls into any MSB category, you have three critical obligations: (1) register with FinCEN, (2) implement a written AML program, and (3) comply with reporting and recordkeeping requirements.

Federal Criminal Offense

Operating an unregistered MSB is a federal crime under 18 U.S.C. 1960, punishable by up to 5 years imprisonment and substantial fines. Civil penalties can reach $25,000 per violation per day. State prosecutors can also bring criminal charges for unlicensed money transmission.

Why MSB Classification Matters

MSB classification triggers several immediate legal obligations:

  • Federal registration: Must register with FinCEN within 180 days of commencing operations
  • AML program: Written anti-money laundering policies, procedures, and controls
  • Reporting: Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs) and Currency Transaction Reports (CTRs)
  • State licensing: Money transmitter licenses in 48+ jurisdictions (separate from federal registration)
  • Ongoing compliance: Training, audits, recordkeeping, and biennial renewal

Federal vs. State Requirements

FinCEN registration is federal and required for all MSBs operating in the United States. State money transmitter licensing is separate and required by individual states. You need both federal registration AND applicable state licenses. This guide focuses on federal FinCEN requirements; see our State Money Transmitter Guide for state-specific requirements.

MSB Categories & Definitions

FinCEN defines six categories of MSBs. Your business may fall into one or more categories. Understanding which categories apply is essential for proper registration and compliance.

MSB Category Definition Common Examples
Money Transmitter Accepts currency, funds, or value that substitutes for currency from one person and transmits it to another location or person by any means Payment processors, remittance services, peer-to-peer payment apps, crypto exchanges
Currency Dealer or Exchanger Exchanges currency of one or more countries for currency of another country (including virtual currency) Forex platforms, crypto-to-fiat exchanges, OTC crypto desks
Check Casher Accepts checks or monetary instruments in exchange for currency in amounts greater than $1,000 per person per day Check cashing stores, retail check cashing services
Issuer or Seller of Traveler's Checks or Money Orders Sells traveler's checks or money orders in amounts greater than $1,000 per person per day Money order issuers, retailers selling prepaid instruments
Stored Value Provider Issues or redeems stored value in amounts greater than $1,000 per person per day Gift card programs, prepaid card issuers, mobile wallet providers
Money Services Business Agent Acts as an agent of another MSB Retail locations offering Western Union, authorized dealers for money transmitters

Crypto Assets and Virtual Currency

FinCEN has clarified that persons dealing in "convertible virtual currency" (CVC) - cryptocurrency that can be exchanged for fiat currency - are MSBs. Specifically:

  • Exchangers: Persons who accept and transmit convertible virtual currency or buy/sell convertible virtual currency for any reason are money transmitters
  • Administrators: Persons who issue (put into circulation) virtual currency and have authority to redeem it are money transmitters
  • Hosted wallet providers: Services that provide wallet services and have independent control over virtual currency are money transmitters
  • DeFi protocols: Decentralized finance protocols with identifiable administrators or exchangers may have MSB obligations

FinCEN Virtual Currency Guidance

FinCEN's 2019 guidance clarifies that most cryptocurrency exchange services, wallet providers with control over funds, and certain DeFi operators are money transmitters. Simply using blockchain technology does not exempt you from MSB requirements. Consult counsel if your platform touches crypto assets in any way.

Who Is NOT an MSB

Certain entities and activities are excluded from MSB classification:

  • Banks and credit unions: Federally or state-chartered depository institutions
  • Securities broker-dealers: Registered with SEC for securities activities (but may still be MSB for non-securities activities)
  • Futures commission merchants: Registered with CFTC
  • Payment processors for merchants: Acting solely as the merchant's agent to receive payments from customers
  • Agents of MSBs: If properly authorized and disclosed (the principal MSB remains responsible)
  • Pure software providers: Providing software alone without transmitting funds (rare; most are money transmitters)

MSB Category Decision Tree

Use this decision tree to determine if your business is an MSB and which categories apply. Answer each question honestly based on your actual business activities.

Am I Operating as a Money Services Business?

1. Do you accept money, funds, or value from one person and transmit it to another person or location?
If YES: You are likely a Money Transmitter. This includes payment processing, remittances, P2P transfers, and cryptocurrency exchanges.
2. Do you exchange one currency for another (including crypto to fiat or crypto to crypto)?
If YES: You are likely a Currency Dealer or Exchanger. This includes forex platforms and crypto exchanges.
3. Do you cash checks or other monetary instruments for customers in amounts over $1,000 per person per day?
If YES: You are a Check Casher. Registration required if you exceed the $1,000 threshold.
4. Do you sell money orders, traveler's checks, or other prepaid instruments in amounts over $1,000 per person per day?
If YES: You are an Issuer or Seller of Traveler's Checks or Money Orders.
5. Do you issue or redeem stored value (gift cards, prepaid cards, e-wallets) in amounts over $1,000 per person per day?
If YES: You are a Stored Value Provider. This includes prepaid card programs and digital wallet services.
6. Do you act as an agent or authorized dealer for another MSB?
If YES: You are an MSB Agent. The principal MSB handles FinCEN registration, but you must be listed as an agent location.
If you answered YES to any question above, you must register as an MSB with FinCEN within 180 days of beginning operations.

Get a Professional Assessment

MSB classification is fact-specific and consequences for getting it wrong are severe. If you answered "maybe" or "I'm not sure" to any question, consult with an AML/BSA attorney who can review your actual funds flow, terms of service, and business model. Don't self-diagnose complex regulatory status.

FinCEN Registration Requirements

Every MSB operating in the United States must register with FinCEN unless specifically exempted. Registration must be completed within 180 days of the date you begin operating as an MSB.

Who Must Register

Registration is required if you:

  • Are engaged in MSB activities as a business within the United States
  • Have an agent, agency, branch, or office in the United States
  • Are engaged in business wholly or in substantial part within the United States

Who Does NOT Need to Register

The following are exempt from MSB registration (but not necessarily from AML requirements):

  • Banks and credit unions
  • SEC-registered securities broker-dealers (for securities activities)
  • CFTC-registered futures commission merchants and introducing brokers
  • Persons doing business entirely outside the United States
  • Agents of registered MSBs (the principal MSB registers on their behalf)

Registration Process

MSB registration with FinCEN is straightforward compared to state licensing:

1
Access BSA E-Filing System
Day 1
Navigate to FinCEN's BSA E-Filing System at https://bsaefiling.fincen.treas.gov/main.html. Create an account if you don't have one. No application fee is charged.
2
Complete Registration of Money Services Business (RMSB) Form
1-3 Days
Complete FinCEN Form 107 (Registration of Money Services Business). You'll need: business legal name, DBA names, EIN, business address, owner/control person information, MSB activity types, branch/agent locations.
3
Submit Registration
Same Day
Submit the completed form electronically through the BSA E-Filing System. There is no filing fee. You will receive a confirmation number immediately.
4
Receive Registration Confirmation
1-2 Weeks
FinCEN will process your registration and assign you an MSB Registration Number. Maintain this confirmation for your records. You are now a registered MSB.
5
Biennial Renewal Required
Every 2 Years
MSB registration must be renewed every two years by December 31 of the renewal year. Mark your calendar and set reminders. Failure to renew is a violation.

Key Information Required

The RMSB form (FinCEN Form 107) requires:

  • Business identification: Legal name, DBA names, EIN, formation date, business structure
  • Business address: Principal place of business (P.O. boxes not acceptable)
  • Ownership information: Names, addresses, SSN/EIN, and dates of birth for owners and control persons
  • MSB activities: Check boxes for all applicable MSB categories
  • Agent locations: List of branches and authorized agents (if applicable)
  • Contact information: Designated contact person for the MSB

Timing Is Critical

You must register within 180 days of beginning MSB operations. "Beginning operations" means the date you first engage in MSB activities, not when you launch your website or form your company. If you're already operating and haven't registered, file immediately. Late registration is better than no registration, but expect scrutiny.

Material Changes and Amendments

You must file an amended registration within 180 days of any material change to the information in your initial registration. Material changes include:

  • Change in business name or DBA
  • Change in principal business address
  • Change in ownership or control persons
  • Addition or deletion of MSB activity types
  • Change in agent locations (adding or closing branches)

State MSB Licensing Requirements

In addition to federal FinCEN registration, MSBs typically need state money transmitter licenses. This is separate from and in addition to FinCEN registration. State licensing is far more expensive and time-consuming than federal registration.

State-by-State Overview

48 states plus D.C. and Puerto Rico have money transmitter licensing requirements. Only Montana and South Carolina do not have general money transmitter licensing laws (though they may have other applicable regulations).

State Requirement Number of States Key Details
License Required 48 + DC + PR Must obtain license before operating in that state
No General MTL 2 (MT, SC) Montana and South Carolina have no general money transmitter law
Surety Bond Required Most states Bonds range from $25,000 to $2,000,000+ depending on volume and state
Net Worth Requirement Most states Minimum net worth ranges from $25,000 to $500,000
NMLS System 48+ states Most states use the Nationwide Multistate Licensing System for applications

Typical State License Requirements

While each state has unique requirements, common elements include:

  • Application fee: $500 - $5,000 per state (non-refundable)
  • Investigation fee: Additional fees for background checks
  • Surety bond: $25,000 - $2,000,000+ depending on state and transaction volume
  • Minimum net worth: $25,000 - $500,000 in liquid assets
  • Audited financials: CPA-audited financial statements
  • Background checks: Criminal and credit checks on owners, officers, directors
  • Fingerprints: FBI fingerprinting for control persons
  • Business plan: Detailed description of business model and operations
  • AML program: Written anti-money laundering policies and procedures
  • Compliance policies: Operational and risk management policies

Timeline for State Licensing

State money transmitter licensing is a lengthy process. Expect 3-18 months per state depending on:

  • State processing times (New York and California can take 12-18+ months)
  • Completeness of your application
  • Complexity of your business model
  • Number of follow-up questions from regulators
  • Background check results

NMLS Streamlines Multi-State Applications

The Nationwide Multistate Licensing System (NMLS) is used by most states for money transmitter licensing. While NMLS doesn't create a single nationwide license, it does provide a centralized platform for managing applications across multiple states, reducing duplicative data entry and paperwork.

Alternatives to State-by-State Licensing

Given the enormous cost, many startups pursue alternatives:

  • Bank partnership: Partner with a licensed bank that holds licenses; you operate as their technology provider
  • Sponsor arrangement: Work with a licensed money transmitter who acts as your sponsor
  • Gradual rollout: Launch in a few states first (e.g., states with lower barriers), prove the model, then expand
  • Regulatory sandbox: Some states (AZ, UT, WY) offer fintech sandboxes allowing limited testing without full licensing
  • Business model restructuring: Restructure to avoid holding or transmitting customer funds

Consider a Sponsor or Bank Partner

For early-stage companies, partnering with a licensed bank or money transmitter can provide a compliant path to market while you build traction and raise capital for full licensing. Many successful fintech companies started with sponsor arrangements before obtaining their own licenses at scale.

Annual Renewal & Reporting

MSB compliance is not a one-time registration. You have ongoing renewal and reporting obligations to maintain your registered status and comply with AML laws.

Biennial FinCEN Renewal

FinCEN MSB registration must be renewed every two years by December 31 of the renewal year. The renewal process:

  • Log into BSA E-Filing System
  • Complete a new FinCEN Form 107 with current information
  • Update any changes to business activities, ownership, or locations
  • Submit electronically (no fee)
  • Receive new confirmation number

Don't Miss Renewal Deadline

Failure to renew your FinCEN MSB registration is a violation of the Bank Secrecy Act. Set calendar reminders well in advance of your renewal deadline. If you miss the deadline, file the renewal immediately and consult counsel about potential enforcement risk.

State License Renewals

State money transmitter licenses must be renewed annually in most states. Requirements vary by state but typically include:

  • Annual renewal application through NMLS
  • Updated audited financial statements
  • Proof of continued surety bond coverage
  • Demonstration of minimum net worth requirements
  • Annual fees ($200 - $2,000+ per state)
  • Call reports (quarterly or annual financial reporting)

Ongoing Reporting Obligations

Registered MSBs have several ongoing reporting requirements under the BSA:

Report Type When Required Deadline Form
Suspicious Activity Report (SAR) Transactions of $2,000+ involving known or suspected criminal activity 30 days from detection (60 days if no suspect identified) FinCEN Form 111
Currency Transaction Report (CTR) Cash transactions over $10,000 by same person in one day 15 calendar days FinCEN Form 112
Report of Foreign Bank and Financial Accounts (FBAR) If MSB has signature authority over foreign accounts exceeding $10,000 April 15 (auto extension to October 15) FinCEN Form 114
Report of Cash Payments Over $10,000 (Form 8300) Cash payments over $10,000 received in trade or business 15 days IRS Form 8300

Recordkeeping Requirements

MSBs must maintain comprehensive records for at least 5 years:

  • Transaction records: All transactions over $3,000 (funds transfers over $3,000 require additional data)
  • Customer identification: CIP/KYC documentation for all customers
  • Agent records: Contracts and information for all authorized agents
  • SAR/CTR filings: Copies of all filed reports and supporting documentation
  • AML program: Written policies, training records, audit reports
  • Correspondence: Communications with regulators

Exam Readiness

State and federal regulators can examine your MSB at any time. Maintain organized records and ensure your AML program is documented and followed. Many enforcement actions arise from inadequate recordkeeping, not underlying violations.

Agent Registration Requirements

If your MSB operates through agents (also called authorized delegates, retailers, or branch locations), you have additional registration and oversight obligations.

What Is an MSB Agent?

An MSB agent is a person or entity authorized to engage in MSB activities on behalf of the principal MSB. Examples include:

  • Retail stores offering Western Union or MoneyGram services
  • Independent contractors selling your money orders or stored value products
  • Branch offices or subsidiaries conducting money transmission
  • Payment network participants operating under your license

Agent Listing Requirements

The principal MSB (not the agent) must:

  • Maintain a list of all agents
  • File the agent list with FinCEN as part of MSB registration
  • Update the agent list when adding or terminating agents (within 180 days of change)
  • Make the agent list available to regulators upon request

State Agent Registration

Many states require agents to be individually registered or approved:

  • Agent registration: Filing agent information with state regulator
  • Background checks: Some states require background checks on agent owners
  • Notice requirements: Advance notice before agent begins operating
  • Fees: Per-agent registration fees (typically $50-$500 per agent)

Agent Oversight and Compliance

The principal MSB remains responsible for agent compliance. You must:

  • Conduct due diligence before appointing agents
  • Provide AML training to agents
  • Monitor agent transactions for suspicious activity
  • Audit agents periodically (annually or more frequently)
  • Maintain agency agreements with compliance provisions
  • Terminate agents who violate policies or laws

Agents Don't File Separate FinCEN Registration

MSB agents do not file their own FinCEN Form 107. The principal MSB registers and lists all agent locations in its registration. However, some states do require separate agent registration with state regulators.

Common Registration Errors

Avoid these frequent mistakes that can delay registration or trigger enforcement action:

1. Late Registration

Error: Filing FinCEN registration after 180 days from beginning operations.

Consequence: Operating as an unregistered MSB is a federal crime. Late registration may trigger investigation.

Solution: Register immediately when you begin MSB activities. If you're already late, file now and consult counsel.

2. Incorrect MSB Category Selection

Error: Checking wrong MSB category boxes or missing applicable categories.

Consequence: Incomplete registration; potential violations of category-specific requirements.

Solution: Carefully analyze your actual business activities. When in doubt, consult AML counsel to classify properly.

3. Using P.O. Box for Principal Business Address

Error: Listing a P.O. box or mail forwarding service as your principal place of business.

Consequence: FinCEN requires a physical address. Registration may be rejected or flagged.

Solution: Use your actual physical business location. If you operate remotely, use your registered agent address or principal's home address.

4. Incomplete Ownership Information

Error: Missing SSNs, dates of birth, or addresses for owners and control persons.

Consequence: Incomplete registration; possible rejection.

Solution: Gather complete information for all individuals with 25%+ ownership or control. This includes dates of birth and full SSNs.

5. Failure to Update Agent Lists

Error: Adding or removing agents without updating FinCEN registration.

Consequence: Inaccurate registration; potential state licensing violations.

Solution: File amended registration within 180 days whenever you add or remove agent locations.

6. Not Renewing Biennial Registration

Error: Missing the biennial renewal deadline (December 31 of renewal year).

Consequence: Operating with expired registration; BSA violation.

Solution: Set calendar reminders for renewal 60-90 days in advance. File renewal even if slightly late.

7. Confusing FinCEN Registration with State Licensing

Error: Believing FinCEN registration authorizes you to operate (it doesn't).

Consequence: Operating without required state licenses; state enforcement action.

Solution: Understand that FinCEN registration is federal compliance only. You still need state money transmitter licenses in each state where you operate.

8. No AML Program

Error: Registering with FinCEN but not implementing a written AML program.

Consequence: BSA violation; potential civil and criminal penalties.

Solution: Develop written AML policies before or immediately after registration. Designation of AML officer, training, and independent testing are required.

Penalties for Non-Registration

The consequences for failing to register as an MSB or comply with AML requirements are severe and can include criminal prosecution, civil penalties, and business disruption.

Criminal Penalties

Operating an unregistered MSB is a federal crime under 18 U.S.C. 1960:

  • Imprisonment: Up to 5 years in federal prison
  • Criminal fines: Up to $250,000 for individuals, $500,000 for organizations
  • Asset forfeiture: Government can seize funds and property involved in violations

Real Enforcement Risk

Federal prosecutors actively pursue unregistered MSB cases, particularly in cryptocurrency and fintech. Several crypto exchange operators have been sentenced to prison for operating without MSB registration and state licenses. This is not a theoretical risk.

Civil Penalties

FinCEN can impose civil money penalties for BSA violations:

  • Per violation: Up to $25,000 per violation per day
  • Willful violations: Greater of $125,000 or value of transaction (up to $1.1 million) per violation
  • Recordkeeping violations: $500 - $100,000 per violation
  • Failure to register: Assessed penalties can reach millions for ongoing operations

State Penalties

States can also pursue enforcement for unlicensed money transmission:

  • Cease and desist orders: Forced shutdown of operations
  • Civil fines: $1,000 - $10,000+ per day of unlicensed operation
  • Criminal prosecution: State criminal charges (misdemeanor or felony)
  • Consumer restitution: Orders to return customer funds
  • Permanent ban: Prohibition from operating MSB in the state

Collateral Consequences

Beyond direct penalties, non-compliance creates additional problems:

  • Banking relationships: Banks will close accounts upon discovering unlicensed MSB activity
  • Payment processor termination: Stripe, PayPal, etc. prohibit unlicensed money transmission
  • Investor concerns: VC firms will not invest in non-compliant companies
  • Customer lawsuits: Customers can sue for operating illegally
  • Reputational damage: Public enforcement actions destroy trust
  • Acquisition barriers: Non-compliance kills M&A opportunities

Recent Enforcement Examples

FinCEN and state regulators have brought numerous enforcement actions against unregistered MSBs:

  • Cryptocurrency exchange operators sentenced to prison for unlicensed money transmission
  • Peer-to-peer payment platforms fined millions for operating without licenses
  • Money remittance services shut down and assessed multi-million dollar penalties
  • Gift card and stored value providers forced to cease operations in multiple states

Voluntary Self-Disclosure

If you discover you should have registered but haven't, consult counsel immediately about voluntary self-disclosure. Proactive remediation is significantly better than waiting for regulatory discovery. FinCEN and state regulators may exercise leniency for good-faith self-reporting.

Exemptions & Exclusions

Certain entities and activities are exempt from MSB registration or specific requirements. Understanding exemptions is crucial to avoid over-compliance or incorrect registration.

Entities Exempt from MSB Registration

The following are not required to register as MSBs (though they may have other regulatory obligations):

Exempt Entity Reason for Exemption Alternative Regulation
Banks and Credit Unions Federally or state-chartered depository institutions Regulated by FDIC, OCC, Fed, NCUA; subject to BSA but not MSB registration
SEC-Registered Broker-Dealers For securities-related activities only SEC oversight; FINRA AML rules; still MSB if conducting non-securities money transmission
CFTC-Registered FCMs Futures commission merchants and introducing brokers for commodities activities CFTC/NFA oversight; subject to CFTC AML requirements
USPS United States Postal Service for money order sales Federal government agency
Governmental Agencies Federal, state, and local government entities Public sector; not engaged in business for profit

Activity-Specific Exemptions

Certain activities do not constitute money transmission even though they might appear similar:

Payment Processor Exemption

Entities that only provide payment processing services for merchants are generally not money transmitters if:

  • They act solely as the merchant's agent to receive payments
  • They do not take title to the funds
  • The service is provided to the payee (merchant), not the payor (customer)

Example: A payment gateway that processes credit card payments for an e-commerce site on behalf of the merchant.

Integral to Non-Financial Business Exemption

Funds transmission that is integral to providing a non-financial good or service may not be money transmission if:

  • The transmission is a necessary component of the underlying service
  • The primary business is not financial services

Example: A telecom company that allows customers to send airtime to other users.

Stored Value Exemption (Limited)

Certain closed-loop stored value products with very limited acceptance may be exempt:

  • Usable only for goods/services from the issuer
  • Not convertible to cash or transferable between users
  • Single-merchant gift cards (not network-branded)

Example: A coffee shop gift card usable only at that coffee shop's locations.

Thresholds and De Minimis Exceptions

Some MSB categories have thresholds below which registration is not required:

  • Check cashers: Only if cashing checks or monetary instruments over $1,000 per person per day
  • Money order/traveler's check sellers: Only if selling over $1,000 per person per day
  • Stored value providers: Only if issuing/redeeming over $1,000 per person per day

Important: Money transmitters and currency exchangers have NO threshold. Even $1 in transmission activity can trigger registration.

Exemptions Are Narrow

MSB exemptions are narrowly construed. Do not assume you are exempt without a detailed legal analysis. The default is registration unless you clearly fall within a specific exemption. When in doubt, register or consult counsel.
