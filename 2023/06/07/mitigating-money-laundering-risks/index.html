Introduction

Money laundering is a process through which criminals attempt to make the proceeds of their illegal activities appear legitimate. The money laundering process has three distinct steps: placement, layering, and integration.

Placement is the first step in money laundering, where criminals attempt to introduce their illegal proceeds into the financial system. This is done by making deposits into banks, purchasing monetary instruments such as travelers’ checks, or investing in high-value assets such as real estate. The goal of placement is to get the illegal funds into the financial system in a way that will not raise suspicion.

The second step in money laundering is layering. This is where the origin of the funds is obscured through a series of complex transactions. The layering process may involve making multiple transfers between different accounts, converting the funds into different currencies, or investing in offshore companies. The purpose of layering is to make it difficult for law enforcement agencies to trace the origin of the funds.

The final step in money laundering is integration. This is where the previously laundered money is integrated into the economy through the banking system to make it appear as if the funds were earned legally. This may involve making legitimate business transactions or investing in legitimate businesses. The goal of integration is to make the funds appear as if they are coming from a legitimate source.

Money laundering is a major concern for governments around the world because it undermines the stability of financial systems, contributes to the growth of organized crime, and undermines the rule of law. The proceeds of money laundering activities are often used to fund other illegal activities, such as drug trafficking, human trafficking, and terrorism. In addition, money laundering can also lead to the erosion of public trust in financial institutions, as the public becomes aware of the extent to which the financial system is being used to launder illegal funds.

To combat money laundering, governments around the world have implemented a range of measures, including anti-money laundering laws and regulations, financial intelligence units, and cooperation between law enforcement agencies. Anti-money laundering laws require financial institutions to monitor their customers’ transactions and report any suspicious activities to the authorities. Financial intelligence units are responsible for analyzing financial data to identify potential money laundering activities. Cooperation between law enforcement agencies is critical in detecting and investigating money laundering activities, as it allows for the sharing of information and the coordination of enforcement efforts.

Despite these measures, money laundering remains a significant problem, and criminals continue to find new and innovative ways to launder their illegal proceeds. The fight against money laundering is a constant battle, as criminals adapt to new measures and technologies to evade detection. To stay ahead of the criminals, law enforcement agencies must remain vigilant and continue to develop new and effective measures to detect and prevent money laundering.

Shell Corporations vs Special Purpose Entities

Shell corporations and special purpose entities (SPEs) are both types of corporate entities that serve different purposes. While shell corporations are often used to obscure the origin of funds in a transaction, SPEs are used for specific purposes such as operating a project or allowing a group of accredited investors to purchase securities in a company.

A shell corporation is a company that exists only on paper and has no office or employees. It is typically used as a holding company for investments or to conduct business transactions without revealing the identities of the individuals behind the company. In some cases, shell corporations are used to launder money or evade taxes. The primary purpose of a shell corporation is to provide anonymity and to separate the owners of the company from the activities of the company.

In contrast, a special purpose entity is a company that is created for a specific purpose, such as operating a project or allowing a group of accredited investors to purchase securities in a company. SPEs are often used in complex financial transactions, such as securitizations and structured finance deals, where the underlying assets are separated from the entity that owns them. SPEs provide a way to transfer risk and manage complex financial transactions while limiting the exposure of the owners of the entity.

There are several techniques for determining the difference between a shell corporation and an SPE. One way is to examine the corporate writings and governance documents of the entity. For example, the articles of incorporation and bylaws of an SPE will often include specific information about the purpose of the entity and the role of the owners. In contrast, a shell corporation will often have vague or generic articles of incorporation and bylaws that do not provide specific information about the purpose of the company.

Another technique for telling the difference between a shell corporation and an SPE is to conduct due diligence on the owners of the entity. In the case of an SPE, the owners are typically well-known individuals or organizations that have a specific purpose for owning the entity. In the case of a shell corporation, the owners are often difficult to identify, and the ownership structure may be complex and obscure.

The operating history of the entity is also a useful tool for determining the difference between a shell corporation and an SPE. An SPE will typically have a well-documented operating history that shows how the entity has been used in the past. In contrast, a shell corporation may have a limited operating history or no operating history at all.

Finally, the underlying contractual documents of the entity can also provide insight into the difference between a shell corporation and an SPE. In the case of an SPE, the underlying contractual documents will often be specific and detailed, outlining the purpose and structure of the entity. In contrast, the underlying contractual documents of a shell corporation may be vague or missing altogether.

In conclusion, shell corporations and special purpose entities serve different purposes and can be differentiated by examining the corporate writings and governance documents, conducting due diligence on the owners, examining the operating history, and reviewing the underlying contractual documents. Understanding the difference between these two types of corporate entities is important for anyone involved in financial transactions or investment activities.

Applicable Laws

Money laundering is a serious crime that undermines the integrity of financial systems and threatens the stability of national and international economies. To combat this problem, governments have enacted a number of anti-money laundering (AML) statutes and regulations. These laws and regulations aim to prevent, detect and prosecute money laundering activities, by imposing obligations on financial institutions, intermediaries, and other regulated entities to implement AML measures and report suspicious transactions to the authorities.

The Bank Secrecy Act of 1970 was one of the first pieces of legislation to address money laundering in the US. It requires banks and other financial institutions to cooperate with law enforcement and regulators by providing information about their transactions and customers. The USA PATRIOT Act of 2001 was enacted in response to the 9/11 terrorist attacks and strengthened the existing anti-money laundering framework. It expanded the definition of financial institutions to include new types of businesses, such as precious gem dealers, and introduced new reporting requirements to detect and prevent the financing of terrorism.

The Federal Reserve Regulations, also known as the CFR, apply to all U.S. banks that are members of the Federal Reserve. The CFR sets out the minimum standards for AML programs that banks must establish, maintain and update in order to comply with the Bank Secrecy Act and other AML laws.

The Basel III protocols are an agreement between the central banks of the world, including the US Federal Reserve, to promote financial stability and reduce the risk of money laundering and terrorism financing. Basel III introduces new standards for banks and other financial institutions, including enhanced customer due diligence, suspicious activity reporting and risk management measures.

The U.S. Office of Foreign Asset Control (OFAC) Sanctions List is maintained by the U.S. Treasury Department and lists foreign individuals, groups, nations and entities that have been sanctioned by the U.S. government. The OFAC website makes this list public, and financial institutions are required to check it regularly in order to prevent transactions with sanctioned entities.

In conclusion, the AML statutes and regulations are designed to protect the financial system from money laundering and other illicit activities. They require financial institutions and intermediaries to implement measures such as customer due diligence, suspicious activity reporting and risk management, and to cooperate with law enforcement and regulators in the fight against money laundering. These measures help to reduce the risk of money laundering, preserve the integrity of financial systems, and protect the global economy from the negative impacts of criminal activities.

Know Your Customer (“KYC”)

Know Your Customer (KYC) is an anti-money laundering (AML) policy that requires financial institutions, including banks and law firms, to verify the identity of their clients and understand their financial activities in order to detect and prevent money laundering. KYC is critical to maintaining the integrity of the financial system and ensuring that it is not used for illegal purposes.

The best way to avoid money laundering issues is to obtain as much information as possible from the client. This information is typically collected through a Client Information Sheet (CIS), which should include a color copy of the client’s passport, vital information such as name, address, date of birth, and social security number, and a statement of how their money was made.

For corporate entities, KYC also requires information on all parties who own more than 5% of the company’s equity. This includes a color copy of their passport and other vital information, as well as a statement on how their funds were initially made.

When conducting KYC, there are three main questions that need to be answered in order to ensure that the client is not involved in money laundering activities. These are: who are the parties involved in the transaction, how were the funds being used in this transaction initially made, and what is the true purpose of the use of funds.

In order to answer these questions, financial institutions and law firms must conduct due diligence and perform ongoing monitoring of their clients’ activities. This includes keeping records of their transactions and reviewing any changes in their financial status or circumstances.

Having an effective KYC program is important for several reasons. First, it helps to prevent money laundering, which can have serious consequences for financial institutions and their clients. Second, it helps to ensure that the financial system is not used to support illegal activities, such as terrorism financing or organized crime. Finally, it helps to protect the reputation of the financial institution and to maintain public trust in the financial system.

In conclusion, Know Your Customer (KYC) is a critical component of any anti-money laundering (AML) policy. By obtaining information from clients, conducting due diligence, and monitoring their activities, financial institutions and law firms can ensure that they are not involved in illegal activities and that they are complying with AML regulations. This helps to protect the integrity of the financial system, prevent money laundering, and maintain public trust in the financial sector.

Risk Mitigation Tools

Risk mitigation is an essential aspect of Anti-Money Laundering (AML) compliance. In the financial and legal industries, it is crucial to maintain the integrity of transactions and ensure that the funds involved are legally acquired. The following are some of the commonly used risk mitigation tools:

  1. AML minded representations & warranties in contracts: Incorporating AML clauses in contracts is a crucial step in mitigating risks associated with money laundering. These clauses can help prevent money laundering by requiring clients to declare the origin of their funds and prohibiting the use of funds for illegal purposes.
  2. Signed and sworn affidavits: Requiring clients to sign and swear under oath that the funds being used in the transaction are legally acquired and will be used for lawful purposes can provide some level of protection against money laundering.
  3. Client Information Sheet (CIS): Maintaining a CIS with detailed information about clients and their sources of funds is an important risk mitigation tool. This sheet should include a color copy of the client’s passport, vital information, and a statement regarding the origin of their funds.
  4. Trackable methods of accepting funds: Only accepting funds in trackable ways such as wire transfer, certified check, and Automated Clearing House (ACH) helps reduce the risk of money laundering. This is because these methods leave a clear trail of transactions and can be easily traced if required.
  5. Top banks in non-OFAC sanctioned nations: Only accepting funds directly from top banks in the United States and non-OFAC sanctioned nations can reduce the risk of money laundering. This is because these banks have strong AML protocols in place and can provide reliable information about their clients.
  6. Sending funds to top banks: Only sending funds to U.S. Federal Reserve member banks or top banks in non-OFAC sanctioned nations can mitigate the risk of money laundering. This is because these banks have strong AML protocols in place and can provide reliable information about their clients.
  7. Using private investigators: Hiring private investigators can help verify the information provided by clients and ensure that the funds involved in a transaction are legally acquired.
  8. Good record keeping: Maintaining accurate and comprehensive records is the strongest technique to mitigate the risk of money laundering. This includes keeping a record of all transactions, client information, and any other relevant information.

In conclusion, implementing a combination of these risk mitigation tools can help ensure that transactions are compliant with AML regulations and that the funds involved are legally acquired. It is important to keep in mind that risk mitigation is an ongoing process, and it is necessary to regularly review and update these tools to maintain the highest level of protection against money laundering.

Sample AML Escrow Contract Provision

“The parties involved in this agreement (the Principals), guarantee that any funds being deposited into the escrow account under this agreement are not the result of illegal activities and are intended for a legitimate purpose in the transaction, specified as __________________. Additionally, the Principals affirm that the source of these funds is not subject to sanctions by the United States Office of Foreign Asset Control or any local equivalent. The Principals agree to provide the Escrow Agent with a fully completed Client Information Sheet and any additional necessary information to back up their guarantees outlined in this agreement.”

The importance of having clients sign an Anti-Money Laundering (AML) provision is to protect both the client and the service provider from potential involvement in money laundering activities. By having clients sign an AML provision, they are representing and warranting that the funds being used in the transaction are of legal origin, not obtained through corrupt means, and are intended for lawful purposes. The AML provision also requires the clients to provide a Client Information Sheet (CIS) and any additional information that may be necessary to support their representations and warranties.

Having clients sign an AML provision is crucial as it protects the service provider from any legal and reputational risks associated with money laundering activities. In the event of an investigation, the service provider can demonstrate their compliance with AML regulations by presenting the signed AML provision and CIS. This reduces the likelihood of being held liable for money laundering activities or facing penalties.

Moreover, having clients sign an AML provision enhances the transparency of the transaction and helps ensure that the funds used in the transaction are of lawful origin. It helps prevent the abuse of financial systems for illegal activities such as terrorist financing, drug trafficking, and tax evasion.

In conclusion, having clients sign an AML provision is a critical step in protecting the service provider from potential involvement in money laundering activities and enhances the transparency of the transaction. It helps ensure that the transaction is in compliance with AML regulations and supports a secure and lawful financial system.

AML Transactional Checklist

 Client Information Sheet (“CIS”) – Have you obtained a complete CIS for each party involved in the transaction?

 Representations & Warranties – Does the underlying contract include representations and warranties that protect your client and give you the ability to retrieve more information before moving funds?

 OFAC Sanctions – Has it been confirmed that the parties themselves and the nations they are from are not subject to sanctions by the Office of Foreign Asset Control (OFAC)?

 Origin and Purpose of Funds – Have you received written representations as to the actual origin and intended use of the funds in the transaction?

 Banking Coordinates – Do you have the banking information (e.g. routing number, account number) for the flow of funds? Have you verified that none of the banks involved in the transaction are problematic?

 Trackable Method of Fund Transmission – Are the funds being transmitted through a traceable method such as a certified check or wire transfer?

 Documentation – Do you have a proper record-keeping system in place to document the transaction and support your due diligence efforts?

 Third-Party Due Diligence – Have you considered engaging a private investigator or conducting additional due diligence on the parties involved in the transaction to mitigate any risks?
