Introduction

Due diligence is a critical step in the mergers and acquisitions (M&A) process, and it’s essential for companies to conduct thorough due diligence in order to maximize the chances of a successful and seamless integration. Due diligence involves reviewing and analyzing the target company’s financial, legal, operational, and strategic information in order to identify any potential risks or issues that may impact the M&A deal.

In this blog post, we will explore the different types of due diligence that may be conducted in M&A transactions, as well as tips and techniques for conducting effective due diligence. We will also discuss the importance of preparing a comprehensive due diligence report and strategies for managing and mitigating risk in M&A deals.

There are several different types of due diligence that may be conducted in M&A transactions, including financial, legal, operational, and strategic due diligence. It’s important for companies to conduct a comprehensive due diligence review in order to identify any potential risks or issues that may impact the M&A deal.

FINANCIAL DD

Conducting thorough financial due diligence is critically important for evaluating the historical performance and current financial health of the target company. Buyers will closely analyze financial statements, growth trends, profitability, working capital, fixed assets, taxes, and more.

Review Audited Financial Statements

Audited financial statements hold much greater weight than unaudited reports. The audit provides an independent verification of the company’s financial position. Request to see audited financial statements for the past 3 years if available. Compare the auditor’s opinion letters across periods to ensure no adverse changes.

Analyze the footnotes and management discussion and analysis sections for red flags. Identify any modified opinions – a qualification, disclaimer, or adverse opinion indicates problems. Understand all explanations for changes in accounting policies or significant estimates.

Scrutinize Revenue Streams

Examine revenue trends over time, segmenting by product line, geography, sales channel, or other relevant categories. Look for unusual spikes or dips that could point to unsustainable growth or pending declines.

Watch for concentration risk – if a large % of revenue comes from just a few customers or products, loss of any could significantly impact the business. Check if revenue recognition policies are in line with accounting standards. Improper acceleration of revenue could inflate financials prior to sale.

Dissect Gross Margins

Gross margin ratios indicate how profitable sales are before subtracting operating costs. Review gross margins by product segment. Low or declining margins suggest potential issues like rising input costs that are not being passed to customers, risk of competing on price, or excess inventory leading to write-downs.

Verify margins align to industry benchmarks. Check for variability by customer segment – larger customers may demand bigger discounts. Understand where economies of scale could be achieved to expand margins.

Dive Into Operating Expenses

Granularly examine operating expense accounts over time. Bar any non-recurring or discretionary expenses that may not continue post-acquisition, like transaction fees or owner perks.

Watch for abnormal swings in specific accounts that could signal problems. Large jumps in bad debt expense indicates rising collections issues. Spikes in legal fees could suggest forthcoming lawsuits or settlements.Adapt your diligence focus to the company’s business model and metrics.

Assess Working Capital Needs

Study working capital trends over the past year. Inventory, accounts receivable, and accounts payable balances will indicate how much cash is tied up supporting operations.

Compare key working capital ratios like days sales outstanding (DSO) and days payable outstanding (DPO) vs. industry averages. Changes in working capital requirements will impact future cash flow.

Validate Fixed Asset Register

Review the fixed asset ledger, schedules, and depreciation policies. Physical inventory counts of property, plant, and equipment may be warranted for manufacturing businesses.

Test useful lives and depreciation methods applied. Are assets being depreciated too slowly or rapidly? Ensure accounting matches the physical assets and their condition.

Verify Sales Tax Compliance

Scrutinize sales tax compliance filings and nexus analysis. For ecommerce sellers, understand taxable presence and responsibilities across states.

Unpaid taxes and penalties can create massive liabilities later on. Review any communications with state tax authorities. Consider obtaining a sales tax audit or opinions from an accounting firm.

Sample Other Areas to Inspect

  • Capitalization table and previous investment history
  • Payroll records, employee benefits and HR policies
  • Accounts payable trends and payments terms
  • Inventory management procedures and controls
  • Customer concentration risks
  • Contingent liabilities footnoted

Watch For Financial Red Flags

Be on the lookout for accounting maneuvers that deliberately inflate financial performance. Warning signs include:

  • Frequent changes in revenue recognition policies
  • Suffering gross margins while revenues rise
  • Black box expense accounts with unclear purposes
  • EBITDA rising much faster than cash flow
  • LIBOR abuse inflating interest expenses
  • Unusual spikes in cap ex or operating expenses
  • Delays in providing recent financial data

Conducting thorough financial due diligence takes time but is hugely important for making an informed acquisition decision. Raise and investigate any concerns early in the process before reaching the closing table.

LEGAL DD

Conducting diligent legal review helps assess regulatory, contractual, intellectual property, liability, and other legal risks related to the acquisition. Developing a complete understanding of the company’s legal standing is critical.

Review Formation Documents

Examine articles of incorporation, certificates of formation, and other documents establishing the corporate entity. Verify proper business licenses are held. Check for amendments or name changes.

Review governing documents like shareholder agreements and operating agreements. Do any terms impact the proposed deal, like rights of first refusal? Understand classes of stock issued and rights attached.

Confirm Compliance

Determine licenses, permits, and regulatory approvals required for business operations. For regulated industries like healthcare, conduct deeper compliance review.

Check for past citations or ongoing disputes. Obtain evidence the company is current on all registrations and filings. Review compliance audits or opinions from legal counsel.

Scrutinize Key Contracts

Review all material contracts including leases, vendor agreements, loan documents, and customer contracts. Watch for change of control clauses triggered by the sale.

Identify contracts requiring consent to assign to the buyer. Review termination clauses – are there risks of key contracts being cancelled post-close? Analyze liability limitations and indemnification terms.

Verify Intellectual Property

Confirm the company’s rights to vital intellectual property like trademarks, patents, copyrights, and domain names. Request registration documents and ownership confirmations.

Carefully review licensing agreements for software, data, or other IP. Are ongoing royalty payments required? Do license terms limit usage or transferability? Clarify expiration dates.

Examine Insurance Policies

Obtain certificates of insurance for all active policies. Check policy limits on liability coverage including general, errors and omissions, directors and officers, cyber, and others.

Look for exclusions or carve-outs that leave the company exposed. Review claim history – a pattern of claims could signal deeper issues. Ensure policies can be transferred or replaced post-close.

Identify Litigation Exposure

Run litigation docket searches for past or current lawsuits involving target company. Analyze if claims have merit and potential liability. Even frivolous suits cost time and money to defend against.

Review settlements, demands, arbitrations, or other legal disputes over past 3-5 years. Obtain legal expenses records. Have settlement funds been properly recorded?

Review Privacy & Security

Examine website terms of use, privacy policies, and consumer disclosures/disclaimers. Do these adequately limit liability while following regulations?

Assess security protections for customer data and sensitive information. Request third-party vulnerability audits. Have there been any data breaches?

Examples of Due Diligence Failures:

  • During HP’s acquisition of Autonomy, HP failed to thoroughly vet Autonomy’s contracts with resellers. This allowed Autonomy to fraudulently book revenue from resellers even when actual end customer sales did not exist.
  • When purchasing a technology company, the buyer did not closely review employment agreements. Several key engineers had terms that awarded large bonuses upon sale of the company – costing the buyer millions extra.
  • A private equity firm acquired a retail chain without sufficiently auditing store leases. Many allowed termination if sales thresholds weren’t met, forcing mass store closures post-close.

Watch For Deal-Killing Terms

Carefully review contracts for any terms that could derail the deal or present major obstacles, such as:

  • Consents required for acquisition or transfer
  • restrictive change of control clauses
  • Liens on assets being transferred
  • Debts tied to personal owner guarantees
  • Severe non-compete or non-solicitation restrictions
  • Unfavorable IP ownership/licensing terms

Problematic contract clauses can blow up deals late stage if not addressed early. Surface these early to determine if renegotiation is feasible.

OPERATIONAL DD

Evaluating the day-to-day operations of a business provides critical insights into how it actually functions. Operational due diligence aims to understand processes, systems, suppliers, personnel, and infrastructure.

Customer Acquisition Process

Study the entire customer acquisition process, from initial traffic source through the conversion funnel. This helps assess the health of the sales process and efficiency of marketing spend.

Analyze trends in traffic channels – is growth reliant on a single source like Facebook ads or Google SEO? Review metrics like bounce rates, conversion rates, cost per lead, and customer lifetime value. Calculate CAC by channel. How profitable is each source?

Website Analytics Insights

Analyze website analytics data over time to identify trends in key growth and engagement metrics. Track users, sessions, page views, bounce rate, pages/session, average session duration etc.

Monitor email signup conversion rates, newsletter open rates, and click through rates. Falling engagement points to problems ahead. Review funnel drop-off points and workflows.

Customer Lifetime Value Analysis

Study lifetime value (LTV) of customers acquired over the past 2-3 years. Group into cohorts by acquisition period to see trends.

Evaluate average order value, purchase frequency, churn rate, gross margin %, and customer lifespan. Lower LTV for recent cohorts can signal emerging issues with user quality or engagement.

Shipping and Fulfillment

Understand order processing steps, warehouses used, and shipping methods offered. Review historical service levels like order accuracy, on-time delivery, and customer satisfaction.

Analyze costs per shipment and trends in delivery times. Check for integrations between order systems, inventory, and shipping software. Review returns rate and process.

Supplier and Vendor Analysis

Map out key supplier relationships and risk of concentrations or bottlenecks. Do a small number of vendors provide critical products or services? Examine contract terms with key partners.

Analyze costs by supplier category over time. Falling or unstable pricing can impact financial projections. Review accounts payable trends for major vendors. Consider supply chain financing options.

Personnel and Payroll Practices

Examine employee org chart and job descriptions. Identify mission-critical positions with high skill requirements or turnover risks. Understand payroll processes and benefit plans offered.

Check for upcoming union contract negotiations, expected labor disputes, or wage pressures in the market. Review employee churn rate. Obtain compensation studies to inform post-close planning.

Facilities and Infrastructure

Assess owned or leased real estate and facilities – production, warehouses, offices, stores etc. Tour physical locations during due diligence, if possible.

Catalog equipment, machinery, IT assets, furniture and other infrastructure elements. Understand age and condition. Review plans for maintenance, upgrades or replacement.

MARKETING DD

Conducting diligence on marketing capabilities and assets provides critical insights into customer acquisition processes, brand health, and growth potential. Key areas to examine include:

Traffic Sources Analysis

Analyze where website traffic is generated from – organic search, paid search, social media, referrals, direct etc. Assess the diversity of channels and dependency on any single source.

Review search rankings for core keywords over time. Falling positions indicate SEO issues. Audit search ad spend on Google/Bing. Analyze cost per click trends and conversion rates.

Website and Funnel Analytics

Inspect Google Analytics data covering key metrics such as users, bounce rate, pages per session, goal completions, and conversion rates. Monitor trends over past 2-3 years.

Study funnel performance for lead gen or sales processes. Analyze drop-off rates between steps. Review A/B tests on site. Assess opportunities to improve conversions.

Advertising Spend and Performance

Compile data on advertising costs over time across search, social, display, and other channels. Compare to revenue and sales conversion trends to gauge effectiveness.

Review ROI and customer LTV for each source. Assess strengths of current targeting and creative approaches. Identify channels with strong performance to increase budgets.

Email and SMS Marketing

Inspect email marketing assets including lists, segmentation, templates, and automation workflows. Review open, clickthrough, and conversion rates over time.

Audit SMS campaigns for mobile conversion performance. Ensure opt-in process follows regulations. Identify opportunities to increase engagement.

Social Media and Influencers

Catalog branded social media accounts and followers. Check for access credentials and assets like creative files. Assess posting strategy and engagement levels.

Analyze influencer partnerships and performance. Review contracts and compensation structures. Identify new partnership opportunities.

Online Reviews and Reputation

Audit online reputation on review sites like Yelp and Google. Monitor sentiment over time, issues raised, and response rate. Assess reputation management processes.

Research complaints on Better Business Bureau, social media, forums and other sources. Identify recurring issues to address.

Competitor Analysis

Profile key competitors and benchmarks for pricing, features, website performance, social media, reviews, etc. Perform SWOT analysis on competitive positioning.

Identify gaps in product lineup, website conversion, brand awareness and other areas to focus on post-acquisition. Monitor competitor advertising activity.

TECHNOLOGY DD

Thorough technology due diligence is critical for evaluating infrastructure, systems, software, and development capabilities. This helps ensure no major gaps or risks are inherited that can undermine the investment.

Audit Ecommerce Platform

Review ownership documentation for the ecommerce platform and any custom plugins or extensions. Verify no open source software is present without compliance.

Examine hosting environment and security provisions. Load test for peak traffic. Inspect SEO configuration and performance. Review site architecture for scalability.

Analyze integrations between the platform, order/inventory management, CRM, email, and other systems. Overly complex or brittle connections increase risk.

Scrutinize Software and Apps

Catalog all software, tools, and mobile apps used in the business. Confirm ownership or proper licensing documentation. Look for end-of-life or unsupported services.

Inspect software contracts to understand transferability, termination clauses, and recurring fees. Review data ownership rights and security protections.

If proprietary apps are present, verify the company owns the IP and source code. Old codebases increase technical debt without documentation and tests.

Map Technical Infrastructure

Document IT infrastructure like servers, networks, data centers, computers, and devices. Assess age, capacity, and condition. Review disaster recovery provisions and backups.

Check for upcoming replacement or refresh cycles. Understand processes for managing hardware, upgrades, security patching, and break-fix support.

Analyze Tech Team and Skills

Profile engineering and IT teams – size, roles, experience, and capabilities. Assess bench strength in key technology areas and risks of attrition after acquisition.

Review reliance on contractors or outsourced providers. Understand roadmaps, resourcing needs, and risks of technical debt. Validate team can maintain existing systems while delivering improvements.

Best Practices for Tech Due Diligence:

  • Inspect the codebase for undocumented hacks or architectural issues
  • Performance test sites and apps under heavy load to find limits
  • Review disaster recovery provisions and security audits
  • Analyze technical team skills and roadmaps
  • Confirm ownership of IP, source code, and account logins
  • Discuss inherited tech debt and timelines to address
  • Watch for fragile integrations between systems and data
  • Validate scalability of systems and servers for growth

Watch For Integration Roadblocks

Scrutinize integrations between systems and data flows for potential roadblocks, including:

  • Hardcoded links vs API connections
  • Monolithic vs modular architecture
  • Test coverage gaps
  • Unsupported languages or frameworks
  • Customizations without documentation
  • Fragile cron jobs or scripts

These pitfalls can derail deals if not uncovered early. Drive detailed discussions on architecture and workflows.

Due Diligence Process Best Practices

Following a methodical due diligence process is key to effectively evaluating an acquisition target across all areas: financial, legal, operational, marketing, and technology. Here are some tips for running an efficient due diligence process:

Set a Timeline

Establish a detailed timeline upfront with the seller to drive the diligence process forward. Set expectations for document production turnarounds, management interview scheduling, data room access, and other key events.

Schedule blocks for your team to conduct review and analysis between deliverables. Factor in time for synthesizing findings and follow-up requests. Build in buffers before critical milestones like issuing the diligence report.

Organize a Virtual Data Room

Structure a user-friendly virtual data room to facilitate due diligence. Index documents into logical folders by diligence area to simplify finding information.

Automate access controls and permissions by user. Track document views and downloads. Use Q&A features for follow-up requests and open items visible to all parties. Virtual data rooms streamline collaboration significantly.

Leverage Legal and Finance Advisors

Engage specialized legal counsel and accounting firms to assist with due diligence review. Lawyers help examine regulatory issues, IP, contracts, litigation, and other legal diligence.

Accountants provide deep financial modeling, tax assessment, valuations, and industry benchmarking. Their experience spotlights risks and insights a generalist might miss.

Plan for Renegotiation

Assume issues will arise during due diligence that require renegotiation. Models your team builds may show lower valuations than initially discussed. Be prepared to justify your position with data.

Have fallback options if key risks can’t be mitigated, like lowering the price or closing contingencies. Think through dealbreakers upfront before you get too far down the path.

Handle Problems Professionally

Bring up problems assertively but diplomatically. Avoid accusatory language and suggest solutions. Present issues factually with evidence from documents or data.

If fraud is detected, involve lawyers immediately. For less severe items, give the seller a chance to explain and address deficiencies. Offer flexibility while upholding your key requirements.

Regular Updates to Stakeholders

Keep your executives and lenders updated throughout diligence with regular syncs. Discuss big picture deal status, key risks emerging, and any renegotiation needed.

Provide access to the data room and written updates. Lack of visibility risks losing stakeholder support for the deal. Be transparent about issues arising.

Create Detailed Due Diligence Reports

Summarize findings from each diligence area in a formal report. Quantify identified risks and impacts. Make recommendations to address concerns. Suggest closing requirements where needed.

Reports provide critical backing during negotiations to enforce your position. They also help risk mitigation planning during integration post-close.

Crafting the Due Diligence Report

The due diligence report summarizes findings, highlights key risks, and provides data-driven recommendations. It serves as a tool for negotiation and guides risk mitigation planning post-close.

Executive Summary

The executive summary provides a high-level overview of the target company, deal highlights, valuation analysis, and summary of major risks identified. It gives stakeholders a snapshot of the transaction rationale and areas needing attention.

Key elements to cover in the executive summary include:

  • Brief company overview and acquisition rationale
  • Deal terms – price, structure, timing
  • Growth drivers and market opportunity
  • Financial summary – revenue, profitability, cash flow
  • Valuation discussion
  • Top risks remaining and mitigation recommendations
  • Proposed closing requirements and contingencies

Keep the summary tightly drafted around key findings needing to be addressed given deal stakes and timeline.

Detailed Diligence Sections

In-depth sections are dedicated to each diligence area – financial, legal, operational, marketing, and technology. Findings are presented through charts, graphs, document excerpts, data analysis, and other visuals.

Each section should cover:

  • Scope of diligence performed
  • Key insights uncovered
  • Process strengths
  • Areas of concern
  • Potential risks quantified
  • Recommended actions to address risks and concerns

Use graphics to spotlight trends, present analyses, highlight risks, and display benchmarks. Maintain an objective tone using evidence to support positions.

Key Risks Summary

Synthesize the most major risks, misrepresentations, compliance gaps, technology flaws, or other findings that require attention. These often have the largest potential impact on the business.

For each top risk, detail:

  • Description of finding
  • Quantitative potential impact
  • Severity level – high/moderate/low
  • Proposed mitigation strategies such as repricing, closing contingencies, holdbacks, indemnities, or remediation requirements.

Rank or categorize risks by severity. Present data-backed risk profiling.

Closing Considerations

Provide recommendations on closing requirements to address identified risks requiring mitigation. This may include:

  • Lowering, repricing, or structuring the deal to share risks
  • Negotiating closing contingencies to alleviate major concerns
  • Holdbacks or escrows to cover potential issues arising
  • Special indemnities providing recourse for problems uncovered post-close
  • Remediation requirements or plans as a condition of closing
  • Updates to reps & warranties to protect against misrepresentations
  • Breakup fee if risks warrant walking away

Detail post-closing actions required to address risks flagged, such as audits, legal compliance changes, or system upgrades. These should inform integration roadmaps.

Appendix

Include supporting documents and tools in the appendix, such as:

  • Due diligence checklist
  • Sample customer contract abstract
  • Market growth data
  • Competitor benchmarking
  • Full financial model and projections
  • Contract/liability matrix
  • Legal memorandums
  • Valuation models
  • Technical architecture diagrams

This provides stakeholders underlying diligence information for further analysis.

The due diligence report distills exhaustive research into an insightful snapshot of risks and recommendations as negotiations heat up. It provides vital support for driving fair deal terms in the interest of shareholders or investors.

SAMPLE DD REPORT – ABC ECOMMERCE

Executive Summary

ABC Ecommerce is a profitable and rapidly growing ecommerce retailer selling home goods and accessories online. Key highlights:

  • Average revenue growth of 45% over last 3 years
  • Projected revenue of $18M and EBITDA of $2.5M for current year
  • Loyal customer base with excellent reviews and repeat purchase rate
  • Proprietary technology stack and mobile apps supporting operations
  • Experienced executive team with low turnover

This acquisition presents an opportunity to enter a high-growth category with an established brand. However, diligence identified areas needing attention:

  • Eroding CAC efficiency on key Facebook traffic source
  • High customer and supplier concentration
  • Underinvestment in warehouse automation and inventory management
  • Technical debt accumulation in legacy ecommerce platform

To address these risks, we recommend revising the offer 15% lower along with closing contingencies around platform migration planning and minimum working capital levels. Ongoing scrutiny of advertising efficiency will be critical post-close.

Financial Due Diligence

  • Audited financials reviewed for past 3 years
  • Revenue by product line and channel stable aside from reduced wholesale deals
  • Gross margins averaged 43% but trending down last 18 months
  • Fulfillment and payroll are fastest growing operating expenses
  • Minimal accounts receivable and payable balances
  • Customer concentration: Top 5 represent 38% of revenue
  • Supplier concentration: Top 3 make up 65% of inventory

Legal Due Diligence

  • Business properly formed and in good standing
  • Intellectual property confirmed as owned by company
  • Advertising and supplier contracts do not have change of control restrictions
  • Facilities leases can be transferred with notice
  • Identified litigation claims likely frivolous per legal team
  • Privacy policy and security provisions appear sufficient

Operational Due Diligence

  • CAC increased 35% last year due to declining Facebook performance
  • Customer LTV is $185 with 60% repeat rate and 15 month life
  • Shipping costs are rising and SLA missed for 23% of orders
  • Inventory accuracy rate is 83% leading to backorders
  • Burn risk moderate given specialized warehouse knowledge

Marketing Due Diligence

  • Facebook represents 72% of traffic – concerning dependency
  • Google Shopping and display ads see strong ROIs
  • Email list 180k subscribers with 28% open rate
  • Instagram following grew 40% last year to 330k
  • Reviews are 4.8 across major sites

Technology Due Diligence

  • Ecommerce platform built in 2010 on obsolete stack
  • Mobile app only covers 20% of orders due to minimal investment
  • Server hardware approaching end of life in 2 years
  • Lack of tests for customized platform code bases

Key Risks Summary

  • Rising CAC – Facebook advertising costs up 47% with lower conversions
  • Warehouse Deficiencies – Inventory and shipping KPIs degrade while demand grows
  • Legacy Platform – Dated stack will hamper agility and limit functionality
  • Top Customer Reliance – Loss of 2 largest customers would cut revenue 20%+

Closing Considerations

  • Lower Valuation – Reduce offer by 15% given risks identified
  • Transition Planning – Require warehouse continuity plan for knowledge transfer
  • Minimum Working Capital – Ensure sufficient inventory and operating funds to buffer transition
  • Platform Investment – Commit to modernized ecommerce platform within 18 months post-close
  • Retention Packages – Prepare offers for key warehouse managers and developers

Managing Risk

In addition to identifying and analyzing risks and issues during the due diligence process, it’s important for companies to take steps to manage and mitigate these risks in order to maximize the chances of a successful M&A deal. There are several strategies that companies can use to manage and mitigate risk in M&A transactions, including:

  1. Indemnification Provisions: Indemnification provisions are legal clauses that allow the buyer to seek compensation from the seller in the event that certain risks or issues arise after the M&A deal is completed. For example, a typical indemnification provision may state: “Seller shall indemnify and hold harmless Buyer and its affiliates, and their respective officers, directors, employees, and agents, from and against any and all claims, damages, losses, and expenses (including reasonable attorneys’ fees) arising out of or in connection with any breach of any representation, warranty, covenant, or agreement made by Seller in this Agreement.”
  2. Representation and Warranty Insurance: Representation and warranty insurance is a type of insurance that covers the buyer against any losses resulting from breaches of representations and warranties made by the seller in the M&A agreement. For example, a typical representation and warranty insurance provision may state: “Buyer may purchase representation and warranty insurance with respect to any or all of the representations and warranties made by Seller in this Agreement. In the event that any such representation or warranty is breached, the insurer shall pay the amount of the loss, up to the limits of the policy, directly to Buyer.”
  3. Escrow: Escrow is a financial arrangement where a third party holds funds or assets in trust until certain conditions are met. In the context of M&A transactions, escrow can be used to hold a portion of the purchase price in trust until certain conditions, such as the completion of the due diligence process, are met. For example, a typical escrow provision may state: “An escrow agent shall be appointed to hold the Escrow Amount in trust until the Closing Date. If the Closing does not occur on or before the Closing Date, the Escrow Agent shall return the Escrow Amount to Buyer.”

By using these and other risk management techniques, companies can effectively manage and mitigate risk in M&A transactions and increase the chances of a successful and seamless integration.

FAQs

What legal issues should you watch out for?

Key legal due diligence areas include:

  • Reviewing formation documents and compliance status
  • Validating ownership of intellectual property assets
  • Checking for any required consents to transfer material contracts
  • Reviewing pending or past litigation for exposures
  • Ensuring privacy policies and terms of use limit liability
  • Confirming insurance policies provide adequate coverage
  • Identifying potential regulatory gaps or licensing issues
  • Scanning for problematic clauses in contracts like change of control triggers

What technology factors are important?

Critical technology diligence aspects:

  • Reviewing core platforms like ecommerce and CRM ownership
  • Auditing infrastructure – servers, security, data centers
  • Evaluating developer team skills and risks
  • Identifying end-of-life systems or technical debt
  • Testing software integrations and data flows
  • Reviewing disaster recovery provisions
  • Confirming ownership of IP, source code and account logins
  • Assessing architecture scalability and flexibility

What determines the success of due diligence?

Due diligence success depends on:

  • Allocating sufficient time and resources
  • Developing comprehensive diligence checklists
  • Gaining full access to people, data and documents
  • Having experienced advisors conduct review
  • Identifying “red flags” early for further investigation
  • Maintaining objectivity and professional skepticism
  • Having contingency plans if major risks found
  • Creating detailed report summarizing findings

How do you handle problems discovered during due diligence?

  • Raise issues professionally with supporting evidence
  • Allow seller chance to explain gaps and propose solutions
  • Be prepared to renegotiate price or closing conditions
  • Consider walking away if unresolved dealbreakers found
  • Use report findings to negotiate reps and warranties
  • Develop post-close remediation plans where needed
  • Build closing requirements to mitigate risks

What qualifications should your advisors have?

Experienced M&A legal counsel and accountants with expertise in:

  • Negotiating and drafting acquisition agreements
  • Conducting detailed financial statement analysis
  • Modeling growth, margins and cash flows
  • Performing valuation analysis
  • Running due diligence processes and checklists
  • Auditing complex operations and technologies
  • Identifying industry-specific risks and regulatory issues

How long should the due diligence process take?

Typical due diligence timescale:

  • Small business with simple structure: 4-8 weeks
  • Mid-size established business: 8-12 weeks
  • Large complex global business: 12-16+ weeks

Factor in time for document production, reviews, site visits, findings analysis and negotiations. Avoid rushing complex diligence, but seek efficiencies to contain costs.

What are the consequences of inadequate due diligence?

Risks of substandard due diligence:

  • Overpaying for the acquisition
  • Unexpected liabilities or integration costs
  • Lost value from customer/revenue loss
  • Legal disputes down the road
  • Reputational damage if issues come to light
  • Opportunity costs from failed merger
  • Senior management turnover
  • Shareholder lawsuits if public company
