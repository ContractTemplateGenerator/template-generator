Filing and Amending a Form D Notice

Published: July 12, 2023 • M&A

Form D is a notice that is required to be filed with the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) by companies and funds that have sold securities without registration under the Securities Act of 1933. This is typically done in an offering based on a claim of exemption under Rule 504, 505, or 506 of Regulation D or Section 4(6) of that statute. The notice must be filed within 15 days after the first sale of securities in the offering based on the exemption. The date of the first sale is considered to be the date on which the first investor is irrevocably contractually committed to invest.

Paper Form D Filings

Companies and funds can file a Form D notice with the SEC on paper. There are two versions of the form available: Form D and Temporary Form D. Each version requires slightly different information, allowing filers to choose the one that best suits their needs. For example, Temporary Form D requires the filing company or fund to provide information on any beneficial owners of 10% or more of its equity securities, while Form D does not.

Copies of both paper versions of the blank Form D are available from the SEC on the Internet at this link or by telephone at (202) 551-4041. The paper forms contain all the instructions you need to complete and file a Form D notice or amendment on paper, including information on where to send the filing and how many copies to include. The filing needs to be signed by a duly authorized person, which could include the filer’s attorney, if duly authorized.

Online Form D Filings

For online filings, a company or fund must have its own filer identification number (Central Index Key or CIK number) and a login password (CIK Confirmation Code or CCC number) to access the SEC’s online filing system, EDGAR.

Step 1: Determine whether the SEC has assigned a CIK number to your company

A CIK number is a unique ten-digit number assigned by the SEC to companies that file with the SEC. If your company has previously filed a Form D, if your company is already an EDGAR filer, or if your company has already taken the necessary steps to obtain one, then a CIK number has already been assigned to you.

If your company has not previously filed a Form D or any other filing with the SEC, then you must apply for a CIK number. You can find your CIK number by entering your company name on the EDGAR Company Search screen at this link.

Step 2: Obtain EDGAR access codes and a CIK number (if not previously assigned)

If your company currently makes electronic filings, you already have and use EDGAR access codes. If your company has not filed a Form D or any other filing with the SEC and does not have EDGAR access codes, you must apply to obtain them. To apply, you must complete and submit an application for EDGAR access online through Form ID.

If you have a CIK number because you have previously filed a Form D on paper, you must apply to obtain access codes. To apply, you provide the SEC with your CIK and create a passphrase. Use the selection for “Convert Paper Only Filer to Electronic Filer” at this link.

Step 3: Create your EDGAR access codes

When the SEC processes your Form ID and approves your request for a CIK number and EDGAR access codes, the SEC will send a message to the email address you provided in the application process with further instructions. In that email, the SEC will provide you with your CIK number and information on how to generate your access codes in EDGAR. You must have your passphrase and CIK number to complete this process.

Step 4: Filing an electronic Form D on EDGAR

To file an electronic Form D on EDGAR, visit this link and use the access codes you generated to log into EDGAR. When you are logged into the system, choose “Form D” under “Make a Filing”. The SEC provides item-by-item instructions and helpful tips on how to complete the Form D on EDGAR. They also provide links to definitions of key terms used throughout the form.

After you complete all of the required Form D information, the SEC recommends that you print the form and carefully review the information you provided before you submit it as your official Form D filing. Once you submit your filing, the SEC will provide you with an email message notifying you of the status of your submission.

Amendments to Form D Filings

A Form D filer may file an amendment to a previously filed Form D notice, either on paper or online, by indicating in the space provided on the form that the filing is an amendment rather than a new filing. A Form D filer should abide by the following guidance in determining whether it should file an amendment to a previously filed Form D notice:

  • A filer may file an amendment to a previously filed notice at any time.
  • A filer must file an amendment to a previously filed notice for an offering to correct a material mistake of fact or error in the previously filed notice, as soon as practicable after discovery of the mistake or error.
  • A filer must file an amendment to reflect a change in the information provided in the previously filed notice, except as provided below, as soon as practicable after the change.
  • Beginning March 16, 2009, a filer must file an amendment annually, on or before the first anniversary of the most recent previously filed notice, if the offering is continuing at that time.

For more information, you may direct additional questions on the process to SEC personnel by telephoning (202) 551-8900. You may obtain answers to interpretive legal questions on Form D information requirements by emailing the SEC’s Office of Small Business Policy at smallbusiness@sec.gov

Frequently Asked Questions

Can multiple amendments be made to a Form D filing?

Yes, multiple amendments can be made to a Form D filing. There is no limit on the number of amendments that can be made. Each amendment should be clearly indicated as such and should provide updated information as necessary.

Amendments to a Form D filing may be necessary for a variety of reasons. For example, if there is a material change in the information provided in the original filing, an amendment should be filed to update the information. Similarly, if a mistake or error is discovered in the original filing, an amendment should be filed to correct the mistake or error.

It’s important to note that each amendment to a Form D filing is a separate filing that must comply with all of the Form D filing requirements. This includes the requirement to file the amendment within the required time frame.

What are the implications of not filing Form D within the 15-day window?

The Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) mandates that Form D should be filed within 15 days of the first sale of securities in the offering. This is not merely a guideline but a requirement. If a company fails to file Form D within this stipulated time frame, it could face serious repercussions.

The SEC may take enforcement action against the issuer for failing to comply with the filing requirements. This could result in penalties, which could include fines or other sanctions. The severity of these penalties can vary depending on the circumstances, including the length of the delay, the reasons for the delay, and whether the issuer has previously failed to comply with SEC filing requirements.

Moreover, the failure to file Form D could potentially jeopardize the issuer’s reliance on the Regulation D exemption for the offering. This could lead to a violation of the securities registration requirements, which could have serious legal and financial implications for the issuer. It could also damage the issuer’s reputation and credibility with investors and the market at large.

Therefore, it is crucial for issuers to ensure that they file Form D within the 15-day window after the first sale of securities in the offering. If there are any doubts or difficulties in meeting this requirement, issuers should seek legal advice to understand their obligations and options.

What should I do if I discover a mistake in a previously filed Form D?

Discovering a mistake in a previously filed Form D can be a cause for concern, but the SEC provides a clear process for correcting such mistakes. If you discover a material mistake of fact or error in a previously filed Form D, you must file an amendment to correct it as soon as practicable.

When filing the amendment, you should clearly indicate that it is an amendment rather than a new filing. This can be done by checking the appropriate box on the form. In the amendment, you should provide an explanation of the mistake or error and the correct information. It’s important to be as clear and detailed as possible in explaining the mistake and the correction to ensure that the SEC and any readers of the filing understand the nature of the correction.

The SEC considers the accuracy of information in Form D filings to be crucial for maintaining transparency and protecting investors. Therefore, it’s essential to ensure that all information provided in Form D filings is accurate and complete. If a mistake or error is discovered after a filing has been made, it should be corrected promptly to maintain the integrity of the company’s disclosures to the SEC and the public.

How does the Central Index Key (CIK) factor into the Form D filing process?

The Central Index Key (CIK) is a unique identifier assigned by the SEC to each company that files with the SEC. The CIK is used to track all of the company’s filings with the SEC. When filing Form D online through the EDGAR system, the company must use its CIK to log in. The CIK is crucial for ensuring that the Form D filing is correctly associated with the company in the SEC’s records.

If a company does not already have a CIK because it has not previously filed with the SEC, it must apply for a CIK as part of the process of preparing to file Form D. This involves completing an online application form and providing certain information about the company. The SEC will then assign a CIK to the company, which the company must use for all future filings with the SEC.

The CIK is a critical part of the SEC’s system for tracking and managing filings by companies. It helps to ensure that all filings are correctly associated with the right company and that the public can easily find and access a company’s filings.

What is the role of the EDGAR access codes in the Form D filing process?

The EDGAR access codes are a set of password-like codes that a company must use to log into the EDGAR system for online filing of Form D. The access codes consist of the CIK, the CIK Confirmation Code (CCC), and the password. The company creates the password when it applies for EDGAR access, and the CCC is generated by the EDGAR system and provided to the company.

The EDGAR access codes play a crucial role in ensuring the security of the company’s filings with the SEC. They help to prevent unauthorized access to the company’s EDGAR account and unauthorized filings on behalf of the company. This is particularly important given the sensitive nature of the information contained in many SEC filings, including Form D.

The process of obtaining EDGAR access codes involves submitting an application to the SEC, which includes providing certain information about the company and agreeing to comply with the rules and regulations governing the use of the EDGAR system. Once the application is approved, the SEC provides the company with its CIK and information on how to generate the CCC and create the password.

It’s important for companies to manage their EDGAR access codes carefully. They should ensure that the codes are stored securely, that they are only used for authorized purposes, and that they are updated regularly to maintain their effectiveness. If a company loses its access codes or believes they may have been compromised, it should contact the SEC as soon as possible for assistance.

In conclusion, the EDGAR access codes are a key component of the process of filing Form D and other documents with the SEC. They help to ensure the security and integrity of the filing process, and companies should take care to manage them appropriately.

What is the significance of the Regulation D exemption for securities offerings?

Regulation D is a crucial part of the securities law framework in the United States. It provides exemptions that allow companies to offer and sell their securities without having to register the securities with the SEC. This can be a significant advantage for smaller companies and startups that may not have the resources to go through the full registration process.

The exemptions under Regulation D are based on the premise that smaller, private offerings do not require the same level of regulatory oversight as larger, public offerings. They are designed to facilitate capital formation for smaller companies while still providing adequate protection for investors.

However, it’s important to note that even though a company may be exempt from the registration requirements under Regulation D, it is still subject to the anti-fraud provisions of the securities laws. This means that the company must provide accurate and complete information to potential investors and must not engage in any deceptive or manipulative practices in connection with the offering.

Can an issuer withdraw a Form D filing?

Yes, an issuer can withdraw a Form D filing if it determines that the filing was made in error or is no longer necessary. To withdraw a Form D filing, the issuer must file a Form RW with the SEC. The Form RW must include the file number of the Form D filing that is being withdrawn, and it must be signed by a person authorized to sign on behalf of the issuer.

It’s important to note that withdrawing a Form D filing does not erase the filing from the SEC’s records. The filing will still be visible on the SEC’s EDGAR system, but it will be marked as “withdrawn.”

Before deciding to withdraw a Form D filing, issuers should carefully consider the potential implications. For example, if the issuer is still conducting the securities offering, withdrawing the Form D filing could potentially jeopardize the issuer’s reliance on the Regulation D exemption for the offering. Therefore, issuers should seek legal advice before deciding to withdraw a Form D filing.

Can I solicit or advertise my offering while relying on the Regulation D exemption?

The rules regarding solicitation and advertising for offerings conducted under the Regulation D exemption depend on the specific exemption being utilized.

Under Regulation D Rule 506(b), issuers are generally prohibited from engaging in general solicitation or advertising to market their offering. This means that issuers cannot use public advertisements, mass emails, or similar methods to promote the offering. Instead, the offering must be made through private communications with individuals who have a pre-existing relationship with the issuer.

On the other hand, Regulation D Rule 506(c) allows for general solicitation and advertising, but with certain requirements. Issuers relying on Rule 506(c) must take reasonable steps to verify that all investors are accredited investors. This may involve obtaining documentation or other information to substantiate an investor’s accredited status.

It’s important to carefully review the specific rules and requirements of the exemption being utilized to determine the scope of permissible solicitation and advertising activities. Consulting with legal professionals experienced in securities regulations can provide valuable guidance and ensure compliance with the applicable rules.

Is Form D filing required for every securities offering?

Form D filing is not required for every securities offering. The requirement to file Form D arises when an issuer is relying on an exemption under Regulation D for the offering.

Regulation D provides exemptions from the registration requirements of the Securities Act of 1933, allowing issuers to offer and sell securities without undergoing a full registration process. The exemptions provided by Regulation D include Rule 504, Rule 506(b), and Rule 506(c).

While a Form D filing is not mandatory for every securities offering, issuers relying on the Regulation D exemptions must file Form D with the SEC. Form D serves as a notice of the securities offering and provides essential information to the SEC and potential investors.

Issuers should carefully review the requirements of the specific exemption they are relying on to determine if a Form D filing is necessary. Failing to file Form D when required can have serious consequences, including potential violations of securities laws and loss of the exemption.

Can I raise an unlimited amount of capital under Regulation D?

Under Regulation D, issuers can raise capital without registering the securities offering with the SEC, but there are limitations on the amount of capital that can be raised depending on the exemption being used.

For offerings conducted under Regulation D, there are three common exemptions: Rule 504, Rule 506(b), and Rule 506(c).

  • Rule 504: This exemption allows issuers to raise up to $5 million of capital within a 12-month period.
  • Rule 506(b): This exemption does not have a specific dollar limit, but it restricts the offering to a maximum of 35 non-accredited investors and an unlimited number of accredited investors.
  • Rule 506(c): This exemption also does not have a specific dollar limit and allows for general solicitation and advertising, but all investors must be verified as accredited investors.

It’s important to note that while there are no specific dollar limits for Rule 506(b) and Rule 506(c), issuers must still comply with the anti-fraud provisions of the securities laws. This means that all information provided to investors must be accurate and not misleading.
