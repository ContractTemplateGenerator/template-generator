Amendment Clause FAQ

Answers to common questions about modifying NDAs - written requirements, oral amendments, who has authority to change terms, and practical procedures for updating agreements.

Clause Language Negotiation Guide FAQ

📝 Amendment Basics

  • An amendment clause (also called a "modification clause") establishes the rules for changing the NDA after it's signed. It specifies what's required for a change to be valid and binding.

    A typical amendment clause says:

    • Amendments must be in writing
    • Amendments must be signed by both parties
    • Oral agreements don't modify the NDA
    • Sometimes: Amendments must reference the original agreement

    Why this matters:

    • Prevents misunderstandings: Without clear rules, parties might disagree about what was actually changed
    • Creates evidence: Written amendments create a paper trail
    • Protects against unauthorized changes: Prevents lower-level employees from making binding modifications
    • Supports enforceability: Courts can clearly see what terms apply

    Without an amendment clause: General contract law would apply, which may allow oral modifications in some jurisdictions. This creates uncertainty that both parties should want to avoid.

  • If your NDA requires written amendments: Yes, writing is required for a valid amendment.

    Standard clause language:

    "This Agreement may not be amended, modified, or supplemented except by a written instrument signed by authorized representatives of both parties."

    What "in writing" means:

    • Traditional paper documents with wet signatures
    • Electronic signatures (DocuSign, Adobe Sign, etc.) usually qualify
    • Email may qualify in some jurisdictions if it contains essential terms and clear intent
    • Text messages are risky - courts are split on whether they satisfy "writing" requirements

    Can an oral agreement override a "writing required" clause?

    This is a complex legal question with different answers in different jurisdictions:

    • Traditional view: No - the written requirement is self-enforcing
    • Modern trend: Maybe - if parties clearly intended an oral modification and acted on it (course of performance)
    • UCC context: Written requirements can be waived by conduct

    Best practice: Always put amendments in writing, even if you think an oral agreement might be enforceable. Why create risk?

  • It depends on your NDA's language and your jurisdiction, but email amendments are risky.

    When email might work:

    • Your NDA says "written instrument" without requiring formal signatures
    • Both parties clearly express agreement in the email exchange
    • Your jurisdiction recognizes emails as "writings" for contract purposes
    • The email is from someone with authority to bind the organization

    When email probably won't work:

    • Your NDA says "signed by authorized representatives" - emails lack signatures
    • Your NDA requires amendments to be executed "in the same manner as this Agreement"
    • The email exchange is ambiguous about what's actually agreed
    • The person emailing doesn't have signing authority

    Risks of email amendments:

    • Disputes about which email in a chain is "the" agreement
    • Unclear whether proposals were actually accepted
    • Questions about authority of the person sending the email
    • Difficulty proving the email wasn't altered

    Better approach: Use email to negotiate, then document the agreed changes in a formal written amendment signed by both parties. Electronic signature platforms make this easy and fast.

👤 Authority and Approval

  • Only people with actual authority to bind your organization should sign amendments.

    People who typically have authority:

    • Officers: CEO, CFO, General Counsel, COO
    • Delegated signatories: People given formal signing authority by the board or officers
    • Those who signed the original NDA: Usually have implied authority for amendments

    People who typically do NOT have authority:

    • Project managers and team leads (unless specifically delegated)
    • Sales representatives
    • Individual contributors
    • Outside consultants

    What if the wrong person signs?

    • The amendment may be voidable (the company can disavow it)
    • But if the company acts as though the amendment is valid, it may become binding through ratification
    • The other party might claim "apparent authority" if they reasonably believed the signer could bind the company

    Best practices:

    • Maintain a clear signing authority policy
    • Verify the other party's signer has authority
    • Request a certificate of authority for significant amendments
    • Match signer level to amendment significance

  • Yes, and for significant amendments you should.

    Ways to verify authority:

    • Check title: Officers (CEO, CFO, etc.) typically have inherent authority
    • Request a certificate: A secretary's certificate confirming the signer's authority
    • Check public filings: SEC filings list officers; state records show authorized agents
    • Review organizational documents: Bylaws may specify who can sign contracts
    • Ask for board/committee approval: For major amendments, request proof of approval

    When to be more careful:

    • The signer's title doesn't clearly indicate signing authority
    • The amendment is materially changing important terms
    • The other company is large with complex approval requirements
    • You've never dealt with this person before
    • The amendment seems one-sided or unusual

    When informal verification is OK:

    • Minor administrative amendments
    • Same person who signed the original NDA
    • Clear officer title (CEO, General Counsel)
    • You have an established relationship with the company

    If you discover the signer lacked authority after signing: Raise the issue promptly. The amendment might be voidable, or the company might ratify it. Don't rely on an amendment without resolving authority questions.

  • You can establish a tiered approval system, either in the original NDA or through a separate delegation.

    Example tiered approach:

    • Administrative changes: Project managers can approve (e.g., updating contact info, changing notice addresses)
    • Minor modifications: Department heads can approve (e.g., extending term, adding representatives)
    • Material changes: Officers only (e.g., expanding scope, changing confidentiality periods)

    How to implement:

    • In the NDA: "Amendments regarding [X] may be approved by the project lead identified in Exhibit A"
    • Separate delegation: Written authority letter specifying what the person can approve
    • Power of Attorney: Formal legal document for broader authority

    Benefits:

    • Faster approval for routine changes
    • Reduces burden on senior executives
    • Keeps decision-making at appropriate levels

    Risks to manage:

    • Clear boundaries on what's "administrative" vs. "material"
    • Training for delegated approvers
    • Documentation of delegations
    • Regular review to ensure delegations remain appropriate

⚠ Common Amendment Scenarios

  • Create a simple written amendment before the NDA expires.

    Sample term extension amendment:

    "This Amendment to the Non-Disclosure Agreement dated [Original Date] between [Party A] and [Party B] is entered into as of [Amendment Date]. The parties agree to extend the Term of the Agreement from [Current End Date] to [New End Date]. All other terms and conditions of the Agreement remain unchanged and in full force and effect. [Signature blocks]"

    Timing considerations:

    • Before expiration: Ideal - clearly extends the existing agreement
    • After expiration: More problematic - you may need to reinstate the agreement and address the gap period

    If the NDA already expired:

    • Consider whether survival provisions still protect confidential information
    • You might need a new NDA rather than an amendment
    • Address whether the extension is retroactive to cover the gap

    Automatic renewal clauses: Some NDAs include automatic renewal provisions ("This Agreement shall automatically renew for successive one-year periods unless..."). Check if your NDA has this before drafting an amendment.

  • Memorialize the verbal agreement in writing as soon as possible.

    Steps to formalize a verbal agreement:

    • Document immediately: Send an email summarizing what was agreed, asking the other party to confirm
    • Prepare a formal amendment: Draft a proper amendment document reflecting the agreed change
    • Execute promptly: Get both parties to sign before memories fade or positions change
    • Address effective date: Decide if the change is retroactive to when you verbally agreed or effective when signed

    What to include in the amendment:

    • Reference to the original NDA (date, parties)
    • Clear statement of what's being changed
    • Effective date of the change
    • Confirmation that other terms remain unchanged
    • Authorized signatures

    Why formalization matters:

    • Your NDA likely requires written amendments
    • Verbal agreements are hard to prove
    • Memories differ about what was actually agreed
    • Personnel changes may make the verbal agreement impossible to verify later

    What if the other party won't sign? If they agreed verbally but won't formalize it, you have a problem. Their refusal might indicate they didn't really agree, or they've changed their mind. You may need to continue under the original terms.

  • Sometimes, but it's often simpler to use a joinder agreement or sign a new NDA.

    Options for adding parties:

    1. Amendment adding a party:

    • All existing parties plus the new party sign the amendment
    • The new party agrees to be bound by all NDA terms
    • May require modifying terms that assume two-party structure

    2. Joinder agreement:

    • A separate document where the new party agrees to join the existing NDA
    • Existing parties acknowledge and consent to the joinder
    • Common for multi-party arrangements

    3. Separate NDA:

    • The new party signs a fresh NDA with one or more existing parties
    • Simplest approach if the new party has a different relationship
    • Avoids complicating the existing agreement

    Considerations when adding parties:

    • Does the new party need access to all existing confidential information?
    • Should the new party have the same rights and obligations as original parties?
    • How do indemnification and liability provisions apply to the new party?
    • Do governing law and venue choices still make sense?

    Recommendation: Unless the original NDA was designed for multiple parties, a separate NDA with the new party is usually cleaner.

    Expanding scope (adding more information types):

    • Specify exactly what new categories of information are covered
    • Clarify whether new information has the same protections as original scope
    • Consider whether any special handling is needed (higher standard of care, different marking)

    Sample expansion language:

    "The definition of Confidential Information is hereby amended to include [new category]. Such information shall be subject to all protections of the Agreement, including [specific provisions]."

    Narrowing scope (removing information types):

    • Be explicit about what's no longer confidential
    • Address information already disclosed under the old scope
    • Consider whether return/destruction applies to carved-out information

    Changing the purpose:

    If you're changing scope because the business purpose changed (e.g., from evaluating a partnership to evaluating an acquisition), consider amending the Purpose clause as well. The two are interconnected.

    Best practice: When significantly changing scope, attach a revised definition as an exhibit rather than trying to describe changes inline. This makes it clear what the current scope is.

📄 Amendment Mechanics

  • These terms are often used interchangeably, but there are nuances.

    Amendment:

    • Changes existing terms of the agreement
    • Modifies, deletes, or replaces language
    • Most formal approach
    • Example: Changing the survival period from 3 years to 5 years

    Addendum:

    • Adds new terms without changing existing ones
    • Supplements the original agreement
    • Example: Adding a new category of confidential information

    Side Letter:

    • Informal agreement on specific points
    • Often addresses practical implementation rather than legal terms
    • May or may not be binding depending on language
    • Example: Clarifying how destruction certificates will be handled

    Practical advice:

    • For any change to legal terms, use "Amendment"
    • Use "Addendum" if you're truly only adding, not changing
    • Avoid "Side Letter" for important changes - it sounds non-binding
    • What matters most is clear, precise language - not the title

  • A complete amendment should include these elements:

    1. Title and reference:

    • "Amendment No. 1 to Non-Disclosure Agreement"
    • Reference the original agreement date
    • Identify all parties

    2. Recitals (background):

    • Brief description of the original agreement
    • Reason for the amendment (optional but helpful)
    • "WHEREAS, the parties wish to amend certain terms..."

    3. The actual changes:

    • Identify each section being changed by number/title
    • Quote the language being deleted or replaced
    • State the new language clearly
    • Use "deleted in its entirety," "replaced with," "amended to add"

    4. Effective date:

    • When does the amendment take effect?
    • Is it retroactive to a prior date?

    5. Confirmation of remaining terms:

    • "Except as expressly modified hereby, the Agreement remains in full force and effect"
    • "In the event of conflict, this Amendment shall control"

    6. Signature blocks:

    • Authorized representatives of all parties
    • Printed names and titles
    • Date of signature

  • Good amendment tracking prevents confusion and disputes.

    Basic tracking approach:

    • Number amendments sequentially (Amendment No. 1, Amendment No. 2, etc.)
    • Keep all amendments with the original NDA
    • Maintain a summary sheet showing key terms as currently amended
    • Store originals or certified copies securely

    For heavily amended agreements:

    • Create an "Amended and Restated" version that incorporates all changes into a single clean document
    • This becomes the new controlling document
    • Reduces confusion about which version of each clause applies

    Amendment tracking table:

    Consider maintaining a simple table:

    • Amendment number and date
    • Sections modified
    • Brief description of change
    • Effective date

    Common mistakes:

    • Not numbering amendments (leads to "which one came first?" disputes)
    • Failing to reference the original agreement clearly
    • Inconsistent defined terms across amendments
    • Losing track of amendments when personnel change

    Best practice: If you have more than 2-3 amendments, consider creating an amended and restated NDA that consolidates everything.

  • Yes, in most cases electronic signatures are valid for NDA amendments.

    Legal framework:

    • E-SIGN Act (US federal): Electronic signatures are generally valid
    • UETA (most US states): State-level validation of e-signatures
    • eIDAS (EU): Recognizes electronic signatures across Europe

    What e-signature platforms provide:

    • Identity verification of signers
    • Audit trail showing when and how documents were signed
    • Tamper-evident seals on completed documents
    • Secure storage of executed documents

    When to use wet signatures instead:

    • If the original NDA was executed with wet signatures and requires the same for amendments
    • If the counterparty's country doesn't recognize e-signatures
    • If the amendment involves particularly sensitive changes
    • If your organization's policy requires wet signatures above a certain dollar value

    Best practices for e-signatures:

    • Use a reputable platform (DocuSign, Adobe Sign, HelloSign)
    • Include clear signing instructions
    • Verify signers' email addresses
    • Download and retain copies of fully executed documents
