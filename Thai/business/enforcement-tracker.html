Enforcement Overview

Thailand has launched its most aggressive enforcement campaign to date against illegal foreign nominee structures. Beginning in earnest in 2024, authorities have dramatically increased investigations, raids, and prosecutions targeting foreigners who use Thai nominees to circumvent the Foreign Business Act (FBA) and Land Code restrictions. According to Khaosod English, legal action has been taken against 857 entities with total business damages amounting to 15.288 billion baht ($468 million).

The crackdown involves coordinated efforts between multiple agencies including the Department of Special Investigation (DSI), Central Investigation Bureau (CIB), Department of Business Development (DBD), Anti-Money Laundering Office (AMLO), and provincial police forces. As reported by AustCham Thailand, this represents Thailand's most comprehensive enforcement wave in history.

Active Enforcement Warning

Authorities are now using AI-powered detection systems to identify suspicious company structures. According to Nation Thailand, the Intelligence Business Analytics System (IBAS) became fully operational in August 2025. Cross-referencing between DBD records, tax returns, work permits, and bank accounts is happening in real-time. If your company structure relies on passive Thai shareholders who cannot demonstrate genuine investment and involvement, you face significant criminal exposure.

Additional Consequences

  • Company Dissolution: Courts routinely order nominee companies to be dissolved
  • Asset Seizure: AMLO can freeze and seize assets linked to nominee structures
  • Land Forfeiture: Illegally held land must be disposed within 180 days to 1 year; no compensation under proposed amendments
  • Immigration Consequences: Deportation, blacklisting, visa revocation
  • Director Liability: Directors and authorized representatives face personal criminal liability under FBA Section 41

Red Flags That Trigger Investigations

Authorities are using sophisticated detection methods including AI-powered analysis and real-time cross-referencing of multiple databases. The following patterns trigger investigation:

Inadequate Capital Sources

Thai shareholders cannot prove legitimate funds for share subscriptions. No verifiable income source for claimed investments.

Passive Thai Shareholders

Same Thai individuals appearing in multiple unrelated companies. Shareholders unable to explain business operations.

Dividend Anomalies

No proportionate dividend income to Thai shareholders. Profits not matching equity positions.

Suspicious Payment Patterns

Disguised management fees to foreigners. Consulting fees that leak profits back to foreign parties.

Foreign Control Signals

Foreigners signing contracts, authorizing payments, setting policy. Email trails showing foreign decision-making.

Filing Inconsistencies

Discrepancies between DBD records and tax returns. Mismatches in payroll data and work permits.

Sudden Ownership Changes

Ownership transfers before applying for FBL. Restructuring in anticipation of enforcement.

Registration Pattern Spikes

Rapid increase in company registrations. Shell companies with no genuine business activity.

DSI Investigation Methodology

The DSI examines four key streams when investigating nominee structures:

  • Funding Trail: Whether Thai shareholders have verifiable, independent sources for share subscriptions
  • Control Signals: Who authorizes payments, signs contracts, sets policy (email trails, board minutes, mandates)
  • Benefit Flow: Whether profits are distributed to Thais proportionate to equity, or leak back to foreign side via fees
  • Filing Consistency: Cross-referencing DBD records, tax returns, payroll data, immigration/work-permit files, UBO/AML disclosures

Proposed Legal Reforms (2024-2025)

Thailand is strengthening its legal framework to close loopholes and increase penalties for nominee arrangements:

Anti-Money Laundering Act Amendments

  • Status: Cabinet approved February 2025, pending parliamentary approval (DFDL analysis)
  • Nominee as Predicate Offense: Acting as or assisting nominee structure = money laundering
  • Expanded AMLO Powers: Asset seizure from both direct offenders and facilitators (Formichella & Sritawat)
  • 15-Year Prescription Period: Allows prosecution of older violations

Proposed Nominee Transactions Act

  • Criminalize indirect control of companies
  • Allow confiscation of land without compensation
  • Clearer definitions of "nominee" and "nominee transaction"

Foreign Business Act Amendments

  • Redefine "foreigner" to include those controlling companies through Thai representatives
  • Close loopholes allowing indirect foreign ownership

Detailed Case Studies: Who Gets Prosecuted?

The following real cases illustrate who is being targeted and help you assess whether your situation may be at risk:

Case 1: Russian Real Estate Network (Phuket, May 2024)

Business Type: Real Estate Companies, Tour Operators

Profile: Russian nationals operating real estate and tour companies through Thai nominees. One woman, Ms. Iana (45), was found to be a director/shareholder in 272+ companies. Thai nominee Ms. Trithip admitted to being paid THB 30,000-50,000 per company to lend her name.

Detection Method: Investigation of phone scam accidentally uncovered shell company network. Cross-referencing revealed same Thai individuals in dozens of unrelated companies.

Outcome: 231 arrests, THB 1.5 billion in assets seized including land, bank accounts, passports, and company stamps.

Sources: Bangkok Post, Khaosod English, Thai Examiner

Case 2: British Bar Owner (Koh Samui, November 2025)

Business Type: Beer Bar / Hospitality

Profile: 60-year-old British woman, Maxine, operating a Chaweng beer bar after her son Ashton was deported. Two Thai women were listed as shareholders but admitted they were just paid employees, not investors.

Detection Method: Financial review found THB 17.3 million transferred from company accounts to her personal Bangkok Bank account. Another THB 5.6 million in cash deposits routed between November 2024 and March 2025.

Key Red Flag: Thai "shareholders" had no investment in the business and could not explain operations - classic nominee indicators.

Sources: Khaosod English, Thai Examiner

Case 3: French Businesswoman's Estate (Koh Samui, 2024)

Business Type: Luxury Villa Rentals / Real Estate

Profile: Catherine Delacote (59), French businesswoman who had operated in Thailand since 2007. Owned 5 luxury rental villas worth approximately THB 50 million through two companies: GVN.E. Company Limited and Maxicat Company Limited.

Key Finding: Investigation revealed Thai nominees were used to circumvent land ownership laws. Her housekeeper, who was to inherit the properties, was denied the estate because the companies were illegal.

Important Note: Enforcement continues posthumously. Criminal charges were filed against the companies and two Thai nominees even after Delacote's death in April 2024.

Sources: Khaosod English, Silk Legal, Bangkok Post

Case 4: Chinese Durian Farm Operators (Eastern Thailand, 2024-2025)

Business Type: Agriculture / Land-Based Operations

Profile: Chinese investors using Thai companies to purchase agricultural and forest land for durian cultivation. In Chachoengsao, 1,500 rai of forest land was seized - 450 rai had already been converted to durian plantations.

Scale: In one Chanthaburi case, a 400-rai plot in a national reserved forest was discovered. Chinese investors reportedly control 90% of Thai durian export hubs.

Detection Method: Joint investigation by National Anti-Corruption Commission and irrigation offices. Local tax payments used as a pretext to claim land occupancy rights.

Sources: Bangkok Post, Dialogue Earth

Case 5: Phuket Law Firm Network (September 2024)

Business Type: Professional Services Facilitating Nominees

Profile: A Phuket law and accounting firm that orchestrated nominee arrangements for approximately 60 companies. The firm provided Thai shareholders and structured companies to circumvent the Foreign Business Act.

Outcome: 23 defendants convicted. Initial 10-year sentences reduced to 5 years (suspended for 2 years) due to confessions. THB 200,000 fines per defendant. All implicated companies ordered dissolved.

Warning: This case shows that professional facilitators are equally liable as foreign clients using nominee structures.

Sources: Phuket News, Nagashima Ohno & Tsunematsu, The Thaiger

Does This Apply to Your Situation?

Based on enforcement patterns, you may be at elevated risk if:

  • Real Estate via Company: You hold Thai land or villas through a Thai company where Thai shareholders cannot prove independent funding for their shares
  • Tourism Businesses: You operate tour companies, hotels, restaurants, or bars with Thai "shareholders" who are actually employees or family connections with no genuine investment
  • You Make Decisions: You sign contracts, authorize payments, set pricing, or manage operations - even if Thais hold majority shares
  • Profits Flow to You: Company profits are paid to you as "consulting fees," "management fees," or other mechanisms rather than dividends proportionate to equity
  • Same Shareholders Appear Elsewhere: Your Thai shareholders also appear in other unrelated companies (pattern detected by DBD cross-referencing)

For legal pathways that provide genuine compliance, see BOI Promotion Pathways and Treaty of Amity (US Citizens).

Case 6: Pattaya / Chon Buri Real Estate Enforcement (Dec 2024 – May 2025)

Pattern: Pattaya/Bang Lamung real estate enforcement shows the practical overlap between (i) illegal “front-end” brokerage work (unlicensed foreign agents), (ii) back-end nominee structures (Thai-fronted entities), and (iii) labor/immigration enforcement at build sites and sales offices.

Illustrative actions: Tourist Police arrests for illegal property brokerage (Dec 2024); construction-site raids tied to “pool-villa” projects alleged to operate as illegal hotels (Mar 2025); and subsequent nominee-focused economic crime enforcement tied to a large-scale condo project spanning Chon Buri + Rayong (May 2025).

Legal hooks featured in public reporting: Alien Work/immigration offenses for “doing the work” (brokering/sales, construction oversight), plus Foreign Business Act + Land Code exposure where nominee ownership/control is alleged.

Why it matters for risk analysis: Pattaya enforcement is not limited to paper-shareholding structure reviews; it is frequently operational (who is pitching/selling, who is supervising construction, where the money flows), and authorities publicly emphasize evidence like customer lists, bank books, land deeds, and Hong Kong-linked transfers.

Case 7: Bangkok Metro Gambling & Condo-Based Operations (Nov 2024–Nov 2025)

Business Type: Gambling dens, condo‑based ops, cross‑border online gambling

Profile: Multi‑national groups operating in Bangkok districts (e.g., Wang Thonglang, Din Daeng, Ramkhamhaeng/Thonglor corridors), often using apartments/condos as operating bases.

Detection Method: Tips + undercover work + search warrants; raids frequently seize cash, devices, and operational records.

Outcome: Arrests of both foreign nationals and Thai facilitators; public scrutiny of local‑station oversight when high‑profile dens are discovered.

Why It Matters: If a foreign‑linked operation is physically based in Bangkok (even if “online”), a single complaint can trigger a search warrant; landlords, Thai directors, and staff can get pulled in fast.

Sources: Chinese‑only gambling den bust (Nov 2024) • 52‑person poker raid (Mar 2025) • Condo raid → online gambling (Nov 2025)

Case 8: Chiang Mai Northern Shift: Online Gambling + Call‑Center Infrastructure (Nov–Dec 2024)

Business Type: Online gambling platforms, call‑center/cyber scam networks

Profile: Operations using luxury condos and a resort property in Hang Dong to house staff and equipment; foreign leadership with Thai/local support roles.

Detection Method: Digital/telecom signals (NBTC monitoring) + cyber‑police investigations; coordinated raids across multiple properties.

Outcome: Arrests of foreign nationals and local facilitators; seizure of computers, telecom devices, and suspected infrastructure enabling scam calls/gambling platforms.

Why It Matters: Chiang Mai is not “low‑risk” just because it’s not a tourist beach zone—recent raids show sophisticated networks relocating inland when border/telecom pressure increases.

Sources: Online gambling base raids (Nov 2024) • Resort‑based scam operation (Dec 2024)

Case 9: Koh Phangan: Unlicensed Lodging + Illegal Work (Dec 2025)

Business Type: Bungalow/hospitality operations; foreign management

Profile: Foreign operators allegedly running unlicensed bungalows and performing management work without proper authorization, with foreign staff involvement.

Detection Method: Local enforcement triggered by inspections and documentation gaps (license, work authorization, foreign employment).

Outcome: Charges reported for unlicensed operation + illegal work/foreign employment offenses.

Why It Matters: On the islands, enforcement often targets “informal” hospitality and villa/bungalow businesses; licensing + work authorization is the fastest‑moving tripwire.

Sources: Russian bungalow case (Dec 2025)
