Short answer: Yes, you need one.

I know it seems silly ‚Äî a contract with yourself ‚Äî but here's why it matters:

1. Liability Protection

The whole point of an LLC is separating your personal assets from business liabilities. But that protection (the "corporate veil") can be pierced if a court decides you're not treating the LLC as a separate entity. Having an operating agreement is evidence that you're taking the LLC seriously as a separate legal entity.

2. Bank Requirements

When you open a business bank account, many banks will ask for your operating agreement. Without one, they may not let you open an account in the LLC's name.

3. State Requirements

Some states (New York, Missouri, Maine, California, Delaware) actually require an operating agreement by law. Florida doesn't mandate one, but strongly recommends it.

4. Define Your Rules

Even as a single member, the operating agreement establishes important things:

  • How profits will be distributed
  • Your capital contributions
  • What happens if you become incapacitated
  • How to add new members if you want to later
  • How to dissolve the LLC

Without an agreement, you default to your state's LLC statute, which may not match your intentions.

Bottom line: Spend 30 minutes creating one. It doesn't need to be complicated. There are templates available. This is one of those things that costs almost nothing but can save you a lot of headaches later.

Adding to Sarah's excellent points ‚Äî I've seen single-member LLCs get their veil pierced specifically because they lacked documentation showing the LLC was operated as a separate entity.

Case in point: Wachovia Securities, LLC v. Banco Panamericano, Inc. ‚Äî court looked at whether the LLC had an operating agreement as one factor in determining if it was legitimately separate from its owner.

The agreement doesn't need to be fancy. Key provisions for a single-member LLC:

  1. Statement that you're the sole member with 100% interest
  2. Your initial capital contribution (even if it's just $100)
  3. That you have full authority to act on behalf of the LLC
  4. How the LLC will be taxed (default is disregarded entity)
  5. What happens on death or incapacity (who takes over?)

That's really it. One to two pages is fine.

Pro tip while you're at it: set up a few other things to keep your LLC legit:

  • Separate bank account ‚Äî never mix personal and business funds
  • Get an EIN ‚Äî even if you don't technically need one, it's free and looks more professional
  • Keep meeting minutes ‚Äî even if it's just "Member approved continuing operations for 2024"
  • Sign contracts as the LLC ‚Äî "John Doe, Member of ABC LLC" not just "John Doe"

All of this helps maintain the separation between you and the LLC. If you ever get sued and someone tries to pierce the veil, having this paper trail shows you treated it as a real business.
