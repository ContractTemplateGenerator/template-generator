Let me break this down because there's a lot of misunderstanding about AB5.

What AB5 Actually Does:

AB5 codified the "ABC test" from the Dynamex case. Under this test, a worker is presumed to be an employee unless the hiring entity proves ALL THREE:

(A) The worker is free from the control and direction of the hiring entity in performing the work.
(B) The worker performs work outside the usual course of the hiring entity's business.
(C) The worker is engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, or business of the same nature as the work performed.

For your dev agency, part B is your problem.

If you're a dev agency and you hire developers to do dev work... that's literally your "usual course of business." Under a strict reading of B, they would fail the test even if they meet A and C.

However ‚Äî and this is important:

AB5 has a "business-to-business" (B2B) exemption under Labor Code Section 2750.3(e). If your contractors meet certain criteria, they can be evaluated under the older, more flexible "Borello" test instead of the ABC test.

The B2B exemption requirements:

  1. The contractor has their own business entity (LLC, corp, etc.)
  2. They have their own business location (can be home office)
  3. They set their own hours and control work performance
  4. They have the ability to negotiate their own rates
  5. The contractor provides their own tools (computers, software)
  6. The contractor maintains a business license if required
  7. The contractor has the ability to work for multiple clients
  8. The contract is in writing
  9. The contractor provides their own workers' comp if they have employees
  10. The contractor provides their own benefits
  11. The contractor is customarily engaged in an independently established business
  12. They can set their own hours and location

If your contractors meet ALL of these, they qualify for the B2B exemption and are evaluated under Borello, not ABC.

The Borello test (from S.G. Borello & Sons, Inc. v. Department of Industrial Relations, 1989) is a multi-factor test that looks at the totality of circumstances. Key factors include:

  • Whether the worker can be discharged at will
  • Whether the work is part of the regular business of the principal
  • Whether the principal or worker supplies tools
  • Worker's investment in equipment
  • Whether the service requires special skill
  • The kind of occupation and whether it's usually done under direction
  • Worker's opportunity for profit or loss
  • Length of time for which services are to be performed
  • Payment by time vs. by the job
  • Whether parties believe they're creating an employment relationship

No single factor is determinative. It's a balancing test.

For your senior devs who have their own LLCs, multiple clients, specialized skills, set their own rates, and genuinely operate as independent businesses ‚Äî they would likely pass Borello.

Key things to ensure:

  • Written contracts that explicitly state independent contractor status
  • Contracts that describe project scope, not ongoing employment
  • No control over HOW they do the work (just WHAT the deliverables are)
  • Document that they have other clients
  • They invoice you (not the other way around)

Been through this exact situation. Run a design agency in LA, work with about 12 contractors.

What we did:

  1. Had an employment lawyer review our contractor relationships (cost about $800 for the consultation)
  2. Revised all our contracts to better document the B2B relationship
  3. Added a "certification" clause where contractors affirm they have other clients and operate independently
  4. Now require all contractors to have an LLC or similar entity
  5. Changed from hourly to project-based billing for some relationships

Two of our contractors didn't actually meet the criteria (one worked exclusively for us, another didn't have their own entity). We converted those two to part-time W-2. Everyone else stayed 1099.

The key is genuinely treating them as independent businesses, not just calling them contractors while treating them like employees.

One more thing to consider: even if you're compliant with AB5, you're not necessarily compliant with the IRS's classification rules (which use a different test) or with other states where your contractors might be located.

Remote work has made this extra complicated. If your California agency works with a contractor in Texas, California rules might not even apply to that relationship. But if your agency has contractors in say, Massachusetts, their rules are even stricter than California's in some ways.

Multi-state compliance is a whole other headache. Something to discuss with that employment lawyer.
