JessicaM_EmploymentLaw Attorney Jun 14, 2025

This is almost certainly misclassification under both IRS rules and the Department of Labor standards. The key test is behavioral control, financial control, and relationship type.

Based on what you described:

  • Behavioral control: They control when and where you work = employee factor
  • Required to use their equipment: employee factor
  • Set hours (9-5): employee factor
  • Can't work for others: employee factor
  • Must attend meetings: employee factor

Everything points to employee status. The fact they're calling you a contractor doesn't make it legal.

DanK_TaxCPA Attorney Jun 14, 2025

If you sign, here's what you're taking on:

  • Self-employment tax: 15.3% on your first ~$170k (both employer and employee portions of SS/Medicare). That's $26k+ per year.
  • No unemployment insurance: If they let you go, no UI benefits.
  • No workers comp: Get injured at their office? You're on your own.
  • No benefits: No health insurance, 401k match, paid time off, etc.
  • Quarterly estimated taxes: You pay IRS every quarter or face penalties.

That $85/hr looks more like $60/hr after taxes and missed benefits. Compare that to your W-2 offers.

JM
JessicaM_EmploymentLaw Attorney Jun 15, 2025

Also you lose all employee protections:

  • No minimum wage protection
  • No overtime pay (even though they're requiring set hours)
  • No protection under Title VII (discrimination/harassment)
  • No FMLA (family/medical leave)
  • No protection against wrongful termination

If the IRS or DOL audits them and determines you were misclassified, you could file Form SS-8 to get reclassified. Then you'd be entitled to back benefits, but it would likely end your relationship with the company.

JM
JessicaM_EmploymentLaw Attorney Jun 15, 2025

To make it a legitimate contractor relationship, you'd need to negotiate:

  1. Control your own hours: Work when and where you want, as long as deliverables are met
  2. Use your own equipment: Your laptop, software, workspace
  3. Project-based work: Specific deliverables with defined scope, not ongoing employment
  4. Ability to work for others: No exclusivity clause
  5. Set your own methods: They define what needs to be done, you decide how

But honestly? Startups want employees. They want someone they can manage and direct. Sounds like they want an employee but don't want to pay the costs. I'd push for W-2.
