This question comes up constantly, so let me give you the real breakdown:

Delaware's Actual Advantages:

  • Court of Chancery ‚Äî Specialized business court, judges (not juries) who understand corporate law. This matters if you ever have shareholder disputes, M&A litigation, etc.
  • Predictable case law ‚Äî 100+ years of corporate precedent. Lawyers know exactly how Delaware courts will interpret provisions.
  • Investor expectation ‚Äî VCs expect Delaware C-corps. It's the standard.
  • Flexibility ‚Äî Delaware corporate law gives you maximum flexibility in structuring governance.

Wyoming's Actual Advantages:

  • No state income tax ‚Äî Doesn't matter much for corporations since you pay tax where you operate, but nice for LLCs.
  • No franchise tax ‚Äî Delaware's franchise tax can be $200-$200K+ depending on structure. Wyoming is $50/year flat.
  • Privacy ‚Äî No public disclosure of members/managers for LLCs.
  • Cheap formation ‚Äî $100 vs Delaware's $90 (similar, but annual costs differ significantly).

My recommendation for YOUR situation:

You said: B2B SaaS, bootstrapped, no VC plans, small team.

Honestly? Form an LLC in Colorado.

You're already there. You'll have to register as a foreign entity in Colorado anyway if you form in Delaware or Wyoming. That means paying fees and filing requirements in TWO states instead of one.

If you're not raising VC money, there's little benefit to Delaware. The Court of Chancery advantage only matters if you end up in corporate litigation ‚Äî unlikely for a small bootstrapped SaaS.

Form the Colorado LLC now. If you later decide to raise VC, you can convert to a Delaware C-corp at that point. The conversion is straightforward and your investors will likely require it anyway.

100% agree with Kim. I made the mistake of incorporating in Delaware "just in case I raise money later." Four years in, still bootstrapped, and I've paid probably $3,000 in Delaware franchise taxes for... nothing. Could have saved that money.

Counterpoint: I'm an angel investor and I've passed on a few deals simply because they were Wyoming LLCs and the founder wanted to keep it that way.

It's not that Wyoming is "bad" ‚Äî it's that it creates friction. Every term sheet assumes Delaware C-corp. Conversion takes time and legal fees. Some investors just won't bother.

If there's even a 20% chance you might want to raise outside capital someday, just start with Delaware. The franchise tax is annoying but it's not that much in the early years (minimum is like $175/year for small companies).

Conversion typically costs $2,000-5,000 in legal fees if you use a lawyer (recommended), plus filing fees. The process takes a few weeks.

Key steps:

  1. Form the new Delaware C-corp
  2. Exchange LLC membership interests for stock
  3. Transfer all assets/contracts to new corp
  4. Dissolve the LLC (or keep it as subsidiary if needed)

It's not that bad, but there are tax implications to consider ‚Äî the conversion can trigger a taxable event depending on how it's structured. You'll want a CPA involved.

That said, if you're raising a proper seed round, investors often pay for or reimburse this legal work anyway. It's standard.

Quick note on Wyoming privacy: it's great, but don't rely on it for asset protection. Courts can still pierce the veil if you're using the LLC improperly.

Also, if you're using Wyoming for "privacy" but you have a California nexus (employees, customers, whatever), California will still require you to register and disclose information there. The privacy is less meaningful than people think.
