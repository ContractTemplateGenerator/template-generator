RealEstateLawyer_CA Attorney Jan 16, 2026

California real estate attorney here. I'm sorry but you're in a very difficult position.

The bad news:

In California, stays of 30 days or more typically establish a tenancy. Some jurisdictions (including San Francisco) consider tenancy established after just 14 days. Even though your booking was 28 days, the fact that he's claiming residency and has evidence (mail, ID) means he probably has established tenant rights.

This means:

  • You CANNOT change the locks or physically remove him (that's illegal "self-help eviction")
  • You CANNOT shut off utilities
  • You must follow formal eviction procedures
  • In San Francisco, evictions can take 6-12 months or longer

What you should do immediately:

  1. Hire a landlord-tenant attorney in SF TODAY. This is not a DIY situation.
  2. Serve him with a 3-day notice to pay rent or quit (he owes rent for Jan 13 onward)
  3. Document everything - all communications, the original Airbnb booking, his refusal to leave
  4. File an unlawful detainer lawsuit as soon as the 3-day notice period expires

DO NOT take any self-help actions. If you illegally evict him, he can sue you for damages and you'll be in an even worse position.

I've heard of cases where these people stay rent-free for 8+ months while the eviction grinds through the courts. And San Francisco has some of the most tenant-friendly (landlord-hostile) laws in the country.

Some things I've learned from other hosts who went through this:

  • Offer cash for keys - literally pay them to leave voluntarily (cheaper than eviction)
  • Some hosts offer $2,000-$5,000 for immediate departure
  • Get everything in writing if they agree
  • Have them sign a release and vacate immediately while you're there

It's infuriating but sometimes paying them off is the least-bad option.

RealEstateLawyer_CA Attorney Jan 16, 2026

Do NOT serve any notices yourself until you talk to an attorney. San Francisco has very specific requirements for eviction notices and if you do it wrong, you have to start over (adding weeks to the timeline).

Also, if he's a professional squatter with a prior case, he knows the law better than most landlords. He will exploit any procedural mistakes you make.

Cash for keys: This is worth trying but be very careful. Common mistakes:

  • Don't hand over money until they're physically out and you have the keys
  • Get a written agreement that they're voluntarily vacating and releasing all claims
  • Have them sign in front of a witness or notary
  • Do the exchange during the day with someone else present

Your other guest: Cancel and fully refund them immediately. Explain there's a property issue. If you try to force the squatter out and fail, you'll be on the hook for that guest's alternative accommodations AND Airbnb may penalize your account.

Media: Be careful. Publicly shaming him could backfire if he sues you for defamation or harassment. Focus on the legal process.

TenantLaw_SF Attorney Jan 17, 2026

I practice tenant defense in SF, so I'm coming at this from the other side. Few things to know about San Francisco specifically:

SF Rent Ordinance is extremely pro-tenant. If he established tenancy (which it sounds like he has), you're subject to:

  • Just cause eviction requirements - you can only evict for specific approved reasons
  • Rent control - you can't raise rent more than the annual allowable increase
  • Tenant harassment protections - any aggressive tactics can result in penalties
  • Relocation assistance requirements in some eviction scenarios

Your best argument is that this was a short-term rental and he overstayed. But because he's been there 30+ days now, he may have tenant protections.

The "owner move-in" eviction won't work here because you were using it as a short-term rental. You'll probably need to go with "breach of lease" (unauthorized holdover after booking ended).

Timeline in SF courts right now: 6-9 months minimum for uncontested eviction, 12-18 months if he fights it.

Check your homeowner's insurance and any separate Airbnb host insurance you have. Some policies include "host guarantee" coverage that covers property damage and legal fees for situations like this.

Airbnb's Host Guarantee is basically useless for this (as you discovered), but if you bought third-party short-term rental insurance, they may cover:

Also document any damage or unauthorized changes to the property. Take photos, videos, written notes with timestamps.
