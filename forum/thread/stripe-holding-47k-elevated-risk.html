Mike, I've handled several of these Stripe disputes. Let me give you the lay of the land.

First, understand your agreement: When you signed up for Stripe, you agreed to their Services Agreement which includes Section 13.3 ‚Äî a mandatory arbitration clause. This is important because it defines your dispute resolution path.

Your options, ranked by effectiveness in my experience:

1. Formal Demand Letter (Start Here)

Before anything else, send a formal demand letter citing Section 13.3(a) which requires 30 days notice before initiating arbitration. This letter should:

  • State the specific amount being held
  • Document the timeline and Stripe's failure to provide specifics
  • Cite potential legal claims (breach of contract, conversion, violation of CA B&P Code ¬ß 17200)
  • Set a deadline for response (14 days is reasonable)
  • State your intent to file for arbitration if unresolved

This alone resolves about 40% of the cases I've seen. Stripe's legal team actually reads these and often escalates internally.

2. CFPB Complaint (Do This Simultaneously)

File at consumerfinance.gov. Stripe is required to respond within 15 days. The CFPB complaint often triggers an internal review that the support emails don't.

3. AAA Arbitration (If No Resolution)

Per the Stripe agreement, you file with the American Arbitration Association. Important: Under the agreement, Stripe pays the arbitration fees for claims under $75,000. The filing fee is around $200 which you pay upfront but can recover.

The arbitration threat (via the demand letter) is often enough. Actually filing is the nuclear option but it works ‚Äî I've seen Stripe settle quickly once an arbitration demand is filed because defending costs them more than releasing the funds.

4. Small Claims (Limited Option)

You could try small claims, but there are complications. Stripe will likely invoke the arbitration clause to dismiss. Also, $47K exceeds the limit in most states (California is $12,500, for example).

Happy to answer follow-ups. What state are you in?

Went through almost the exact same thing last year. $31K held for "risk review." Here's what worked for me:

1. CFPB complaint ‚Äî did nothing honestly
2. Demand letter ‚Äî got a slightly more detailed response but still no release
3. Actually filed for AAA arbitration ‚Äî funds released within 2 weeks of them receiving the arbitration demand

The filing is a pain but it's not rocket science. Cost me $200 and about 3 hours of my time. Worth it for $31K.

Pro tip: In your demand letter, specifically mention that you'll be seeking consequential damages for the business harm caused by the hold (the loan you had to take out, lost opportunities, etc.). That gets their attention.

Good discussion here. Just want to add a few points:

On the legal claims: The strongest claim in these situations is usually conversion ‚Äî Stripe is exercising dominion over your property (the funds) beyond what the contract allows. The SSA gives them the right to hold funds for "reasonable" periods to cover chargebacks, but 4 months with a 0.3% chargeback rate is hard to justify as reasonable.

On documenting damages: Mike, start documenting everything now ‚Äî the loan terms, any lost business opportunities, communications with Stripe. If this goes to arbitration, you'll want a clear damages calculation.

On the template: There's actually a demand letter generator on the main Terms.Law site specifically for this situation. Might save you some time: terms.law/stripe-demand-letter-generator
