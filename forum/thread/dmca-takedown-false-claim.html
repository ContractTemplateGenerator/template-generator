CopyrightLawyer Attorney Dec 8, 2025

Take a breath. This is infuriating but you have options.

Immediate step: File a DMCA counter-notification. The counter-notice must include:

1. Identification of the removed material and its location before removal
2. Statement under penalty of perjury that you have a good faith belief the material was removed by mistake
3. Your name, address, phone number
4. Consent to jurisdiction of federal district court
5. Your physical or electronic signature

Once you file, the platform must restore your content within 10-14 business days UNLESS the claimant files an actual lawsuit. Most fraudulent claimants won't go that far because they'd have to commit perjury in federal court.

Regarding suing them: Yes, 17 U.S.C. 512(f) allows you to sue for damages if someone "knowingly materially misrepresents" that content is infringing. The catch: you need to prove they KNEW the claim was false, not just that they were wrong. If you can connect them to your competitor and show bad faith, you have a case.

Been there, fought that, won.

Similar situation last year - competitor filed DMCA on 3 of my videos using a random LLC. I filed counter-notices on all of them. They didn't sue (obviously, because they had nothing). Content restored in 12 days.

Then I went on offense. Hired an attorney, sent them a demand letter citing 512(f), and they settled for $15k to make it go away. Not huge money but covered my legal fees and sent a message.

Key thing that helped: I had EVERYTHING documented. Original project files with metadata showing creation dates, Dropbox revision history, even slack messages discussing the video while I was making it. Gather all that now.

platform_mod_insight Moderator Dec 9, 2025

Former trust & safety at a major platform here. Let me explain how this works from the inside:

Platforms HAVE to act on DMCA notices quickly to maintain safe harbor protection. We can't investigate whether a claim is legit before taking content down - that's the law working as designed (poorly, imo).

But we DO track patterns. If someone files multiple bogus claims that get counter-noticed, they get flagged. Repeat abusers can lose their ability to file claims or have their account terminated entirely.

When you file your counter-notice, be thorough. Include a summary of evidence you have. We can't make legal determinations but we do note when counter-notices are well-documented vs. just "nuh uh wasn't me."

pro tip for everyone reading this: start documenting your creation process NOW before you need it

I use a combo of: 1) git commits for any code/scripts, 2) raw footage backups with original timestamps, 3) periodic screenshots of my editing timeline, 4) a quick voice memo when I start a new project saying "starting work on X video about Y topic on [date]"

Sounds paranoid but when someone comes at you with a bogus claim, having ironclad proof of creation makes everything easier. The counter-notice is way more convincing and if you do need to sue for 512(f) damages you've already got your evidence ready
