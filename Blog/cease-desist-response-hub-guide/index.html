When a C&D Lands in Your Inbox

A cease and desist letter is often the first warning shot before litigation. But not all C&D letters are created equal‚Äîand not all deserve the same response. Some represent legitimate legal claims requiring immediate compliance. Others are pure intimidation tactics from parties who know they'd lose in court but hope you'll back down anyway.

The challenge is distinguishing between them, and this hub gives you the tools to do exactly that.

‚ö†Ô∏è Response Deadlines Matter

Most C&D letters include a deadline‚Äîtypically 7 to 30 days. Missing this deadline doesn't automatically trigger a lawsuit, but it does signal to the sender that you're either unprepared or ignoring them. Either perception weakens your position.

Key Legal Concepts

The Anti-SLAPP Framework

In California and many other states, defendants in certain lawsuits can file an Anti-SLAPP motion (California Code of Civil Procedure ¬ß 425.16) to strike frivolous claims that target protected speech. The key is understanding when a pre-litigation C&D constitutes "speech" that triggers Anti-SLAPP protection‚Äîand when it crosses into unprotected conduct like extortion.

Key Anti-SLAPP Cases
  • Flatley v. Mauro ‚Äî Extortion exception to litigation privilege
  • Navellier v. Sletten ‚Äî Two-prong Anti-SLAPP test
  • FilmOn.com v. DoubleVerify ‚Äî Commercial speech limitations

Section 230 and Online Content

If you're being threatened over third-party content on your platform‚Äîuser reviews, forum posts, or aggregated data‚ÄîSection 230 of the Communications Decency Act (47 U.S.C. ¬ß 230) may provide immunity. But Section 230 has limits: it doesn't protect your own content, and recent case law has carved out exceptions for certain types of third-party liability.

The Consumer Review Fairness Act

The CRFA (15 U.S.C. ¬ß 45b) voids contract provisions that prohibit or restrict honest reviews. If you're receiving a C&D over a negative review and your contract had a "non-disparagement" clause, that clause may be unenforceable‚Äîand the sender may face FTC action.

Response Strategies

The hub covers six primary response approaches:

  1. Ignore ‚Äî Appropriate only when the threat is clearly baseless and sender lacks resources to litigate
  2. Deny and Defend ‚Äî Assert your legal position firmly; appropriate when you have strong defenses
  3. Seek Clarification ‚Äî Request specifics when the C&D is vague or overbroad
  4. Partial Compliance ‚Äî Address legitimate concerns while preserving your rights on other issues
  5. Negotiate ‚Äî Work toward a mutually acceptable resolution without admitting liability
  6. Counter-Offensive ‚Äî When the C&D itself is unlawful (extortion, abuse of process, tortious interference)

When to Get Legal Help

The hub provides substantial self-help resources, but certain C&D situations require attorney involvement:

  • The sender is a well-funded company or major law firm
  • The claims involve federal court jurisdiction (patent, copyright, Lanham Act)
  • Your potential exposure exceeds $50,000
  • You're facing a regulatory complaint or professional licensing threat
  • The deadline is less than 7 days away
  • You want to explore counterclaims or fee recovery
