What is an LLC Operating Agreement?

An Operating Agreement is the foundational internal document that governs how your Limited Liability Company functions. It works as a contract between the LLC members (owners) that establishes the financial and functional decisions of the business. While Articles of Organization officially create your LLC with the state, the Operating Agreement defines how it actually operates.

In California, an Operating Agreement can be written, oral, or implied. However, a well-drafted written agreement provides significantly more protection and clarity. Without a written agreement, your LLC will default to California’s statutory provisions, which may not align with your business goals or member relationships.

Why California LLCs Need an Operating Agreement

While California doesn’t legally require LLCs to have Operating Agreements (unlike states such as New York), creating one offers several crucial benefits:

  1. Preserves Limited Liability Protection: A proper Operating Agreement helps maintain the separation between you personally and your business entity, strengthening your liability shield.
  2. Prevents Default State Rules: Without an Operating Agreement, your LLC is governed by California’s default rules under the Revised Uniform Limited Liability Company Act (RULLCA), which may not reflect your intentions.
  3. Resolves Member Disputes: The agreement provides a clear framework for resolving disagreements before they escalate into costly litigation.
  4. Facilitates Business Operations: Banks, investors, and other businesses often require an Operating Agreement before doing business with your LLC.
  5. Customizes Management Structure: You can choose between member-managed or manager-managed structures based on your business needs.

Essential Components of a California LLC Operating Agreement

A comprehensive California LLC Operating Agreement should include the following key elements:

1. Basic Company Information

The agreement should begin with fundamental details about your LLC:

  • Company Name: Must match exactly what’s on your Articles of Organization
  • Principal Address: Your main business location in California
  • Formation Date: When your Articles of Organization were filed
  • Business Purpose: A description of your LLC’s activities (can be general or specific)
  • Duration: Typically “perpetual” unless you have a specific end date in mind

California requires that your LLC name include “Limited Liability Company,” “LLC,” or “L.L.C.” Your Operating Agreement should use the exact same name that appears on your filing with the Secretary of State to maintain consistency across all legal documents.

2. Membership Structure and Capital Contributions

This section details who owns the LLC and their financial involvement:

  • Member Information: Names, addresses, and contact information
  • Capital Contributions: What each member contributed to start the business (money, property, services)
  • Ownership Percentages: Each member’s percentage of the company
  • Voting Rights: How voting power is allocated (usually proportional to ownership)
  • Process for Admitting New Members: Requirements and procedures for adding owners

In California, single-member LLCs should be particularly thorough in this section to reinforce the separation between personal and business finances, which strengthens liability protection.

3. Management Structure

California LLCs can choose between two management structures:

Member-Managed Structure

In this common arrangement, all owners participate in day-to-day business decisions. This works well for:

  • Small businesses where all owners are actively involved
  • Simple business models with few members
  • Startups where founders want direct control

Your Operating Agreement should specify decision-making processes, including what constitutes a quorum and whether majority or unanimous votes are required for different types of decisions.

Manager-Managed Structure

This structure designates specific individuals (who may or may not be members) to run the business. It’s appropriate for:

  • LLCs with passive investors who don’t want to participate in daily operations
  • Larger LLCs with many members
  • Businesses requiring specialized management expertise

If choosing this structure, your agreement should clearly define manager powers, limitations, compensation, and removal procedures.

4. Capital Accounts and Allocations

This section outlines how the LLC handles financial matters:

  • Capital Accounts: How member investments are tracked
  • Additional Capital Requirements: Whether and how members can be required to make additional contributions
  • Profit and Loss Allocations: How profits and losses are divided (typically based on ownership percentages)
  • Tax Treatment: Usually specifies pass-through taxation, but can address special allocations

California LLCs should be aware of the state’s $800 annual minimum franchise tax, regardless of whether the business is profitable. Your Operating Agreement should acknowledge this obligation to ensure members understand this recurring expense.

5. Distributions

The distributions section covers how and when profits are distributed to members:

  • Distribution Schedule: Frequency of distributions (quarterly, annually, etc.)
  • Distribution Method: Usually proportional to ownership but can be customized
  • Limitations: Restrictions on distributions that would impair the LLC’s ability to pay debts
  • Tax Distributions: Provisions for distributions specifically to cover tax liabilities

California law prohibits distributions that would render the LLC unable to pay its debts as they come due, so your agreement should include this limitation to comply with state law.

6. Membership Changes and Transfers

This section governs changes in ownership:

  • Transfer Restrictions: Limitations on members’ ability to sell or transfer interests
  • Right of First Refusal: Giving existing members first opportunity to buy interests
  • Buy-Sell Provisions: Rules for handling member death, disability, divorce, or retirement
  • Valuation Methods: How to determine the value of a membership interest

Transfer restrictions are particularly important for small businesses in California to prevent unwanted third parties from becoming members and to maintain control over who has decision-making authority.

7. Dissolution and Liquidation

This section addresses business termination:

  • Dissolution Events: Circumstances that trigger dissolution (e.g., member vote, regulatory action)
  • Winding Up Process: Steps for concluding business affairs
  • Asset Distribution: How remaining assets will be distributed after paying creditors
  • Certificate of Dissolution: Requirements for formally dissolving with the California Secretary of State

California has specific dissolution filing requirements, including filing a Certificate of Dissolution (Form LLC-3) and a Certificate of Cancellation (Form LLC-4/7).

8. Amendments and Dispute Resolution

Finally, the agreement should include:

  • Amendment Procedures: How the Operating Agreement can be changed
  • Dispute Resolution Methods: Mediation, arbitration, or litigation provisions
  • Governing Law: Confirmation that California law governs the agreement

Many California businesses opt for mediation followed by binding arbitration to resolve disputes, which is often faster and less expensive than court litigation.

California-Specific Requirements and Considerations

California LLCs have several state-specific considerations to address in their Operating Agreements:

California Revised Uniform Limited Liability Company Act (RULLCA)

Since 2014, California LLCs have been governed by the RULLCA (California Corporations Code Sections 17701.01-17713.13). Your Operating Agreement should acknowledge this law and specifically override any default provisions that don’t align with your business goals.

California Tax Requirements

California imposes several tax obligations on LLCs:

  • $800 Annual Minimum Franchise Tax: Due even if your LLC isn’t profitable
  • LLC Fee: Additional annual fee for LLCs with gross revenues over $250,000
  • Sales and Use Tax: If selling tangible goods
  • Employment Taxes: If hiring employees

Your Operating Agreement should address these obligations and clarify member responsibilities regarding them.

Professional LLCs

California has special restrictions for professional LLCs (PLLCs) offering services that require professional licenses (e.g., lawyers, doctors, accountants). If applicable, your Operating Agreement should include:

  • Licensing Requirements: Confirmation that all members/managers hold required licenses
  • Limitation on Services: Restricting services to the licensed profession
  • Professional Liability: Clarification that members remain personally liable for professional malpractice

Series LLCs

Unlike some states, California does not recognize “series LLCs” (structures that allow multiple protected “cells” within one LLC). If you’re considering this structure, be aware that California law doesn’t provide the internal liability shields that make series LLCs attractive in other states.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Using Generic Templates Without Customization

Generic templates often miss crucial California-specific provisions and don’t address your unique business needs. Always customize your Operating Agreement to your specific situation.

2. Inadequate Voting and Management Provisions

Failing to clearly define voting requirements for different decisions can lead to deadlocks and disputes. Specify which decisions require majority votes versus unanimous consent.

3. Omitting Buy-Sell Provisions

Without clear buy-sell provisions, member exits, deaths, or divorces can create significant disruption. Include detailed procedures for these scenarios.

4. Inconsistency with Other Documents

Ensure your Operating Agreement aligns with your Articles of Organization and other business documents to avoid contradictions that could undermine your liability protection.

5. Failing to Update the Agreement

As your business evolves, your Operating Agreement should too. Regular reviews ensure it remains relevant and effective.

Executing Your California LLC Operating Agreement

After drafting your Operating Agreement:

  1. Review with All Members: Ensure everyone understands and agrees to the terms.
  2. Sign and Date: All members should sign and date the agreement.
  3. Store Securely: Keep the original in your business records and provide copies to all members.
  4. Implement Consistently: Follow the procedures established in your agreement consistently.
  5. Review Periodically: Revisit the agreement annually or when significant business changes occur.

Unlike Articles of Organization, you do not file your Operating Agreement with the California Secretary of State—it’s an internal document.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is an Operating Agreement legally required in California?

No, California law doesn’t legally require an LLC to have an Operating Agreement. However, operating without one means your LLC defaults to California’s statutory provisions, which may not align with your intentions.

Can I change my Operating Agreement after creating it?

Yes, most Operating Agreements include an amendment provision detailing how changes can be made. Typically, this requires some form of member vote (majority, supermajority, or unanimous, depending on what your agreement specifies).

Can a single-member LLC benefit from an Operating Agreement?

Absolutely. A single-member Operating Agreement helps maintain the separation between personal and business assets, strengthening liability protection. It also provides operating guidelines and can make your business appear more professional to banks and other stakeholders.

What happens if members disagree about interpreting the Operating Agreement?

Your Operating Agreement should include dispute resolution provisions that outline the process for resolving disagreements, whether through mediation, arbitration, or litigation. Without these provisions, disputes might lead to costly court battles.

Does my Operating Agreement need to be notarized?

California doesn’t require notarization of LLC Operating Agreements. However, having the document notarized provides an additional layer of authentication that can be beneficial in case of disputes.

How does California tax LLCs?

California LLCs pay an $800 annual minimum franchise tax regardless of profitability. LLCs with California gross income over $250,000 pay an additional annual fee, ranging from $900 to $11,790 depending on income level. For federal tax purposes, LLCs typically use pass-through taxation, but can elect corporate taxation.

Can my California LLC Operating Agreement include non-compete clauses?

Non-compete clauses in California are generally unenforceable except in very limited circumstances, such as the sale of a business. While you can include them in your Operating Agreement, be aware that California courts strongly favor employee mobility and typically won’t enforce restrictions on members’ ability to work in similar businesses.

What’s the difference between Articles of Organization and an Operating Agreement?

Articles of Organization officially create your LLC with the state and contain basic information like your LLC name, address, and registered agent. The Operating Agreement is a more comprehensive internal document that governs how your LLC operates. Articles are filed with the state; Operating Agreements are not.
