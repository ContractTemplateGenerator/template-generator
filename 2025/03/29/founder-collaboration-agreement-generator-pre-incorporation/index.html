Critical Risk Areas

Equity Allocation

High Risk: Improper equity allocation is the #1 source of founder disputes. The agreement must clearly define initial ownership percentages that total exactly 100%.

Recommendation: Consider contributions beyond just time – expertise, capital, and existing assets all factor into fair equity splits.
Intellectual Property Assignment

High Risk: Without proper IP assignment, founders might claim ownership of critical business assets if they leave. This can devastate fundraising efforts and valuation.

Recommendation: Clearly identify any pre-existing IP a founder is bringing to the venture but not transferring.
Vesting Schedules

High Risk: Without vesting, a founder could leave after a month but keep their full equity stake. Industry-standard 4-year vesting with a 1-year cliff protects all parties.

Recommendation: Vesting protects founders who stay committed; it’s not about distrust but aligning incentives.
Roles & Responsibilities

Medium Risk: Undefined or overlapping responsibilities create operational inefficiency and personal friction among founders.

Recommendation: Be specific – “Product Development” is too vague; list specific responsibilities and authority boundaries.
Decision Making

Medium Risk: Lack of clear decision-making protocols leads to deadlock, especially with equal equity splits. Specify which decisions require what level of approval.

Recommendation: Consider different voting requirements for different decision types (day-to-day vs. strategic).

Frequently Asked Questions

When is the right time to create a founder collaboration agreement?

The best time is as early as possible – ideally before significant work has been done on the project. The second best time is now. Many co-founders start collaborating informally, but once you’re committed to building something together, formalizing the relationship protects everyone involved. Don’t wait until conflict arises to establish ground rules.

How is this different from incorporation documents?

A founder collaboration agreement governs your relationship before formal incorporation. It’s a bridge document that establishes equity splits, roles, IP ownership, and other essential elements while you’re still developing your concept. When you formally incorporate (typically as an LLC or C-Corporation), you’ll create official corporate documents like bylaws, operating agreements, and stock purchase agreements. The terms in your founder collaboration agreement will help guide those formal documents.

How do we determine fair equity splits?

Equity splits should reflect both past contributions and expected future contributions. Consider factors like: idea origination, technical expertise, industry connections, capital contributions, full-time commitment, opportunity cost, and risk taken. Equal splits (50/50) are common for two founders who are both fully committed, but don’t default to equal splits without discussion. A dynamic split calculator or “equity pie” discussion is often valuable for teams of 3+ founders. Remember that equity without vesting is dangerous – a founder who leaves early should not retain their full stake.

Why is vesting so important in a founder agreement?

Vesting is crucial because it aligns incentives and protects committed founders. Without vesting, a founder could leave after a short period but retain their full equity stake – creating a “dead equity” problem that can cripple future funding rounds and demotivate remaining founders. The standard 4-year vesting schedule with a 1-year cliff means that founders earn their equity over time, demonstrating sustained commitment. After one year, 25% of equity vests (the “cliff”), and the remaining 75% vests monthly over the following three years. If a founder leaves before the cliff, they typically retain no equity.

Are non-compete provisions enforceable?

Non-compete enforceability varies significantly by jurisdiction. In California, for example, non-compete provisions are generally unenforceable except in very limited circumstances like the sale of a business. Other states enforce reasonable non-competes, typically those limited in geographic scope, duration (usually 1-2 years maximum), and scope of prohibited activities. If you include non-compete provisions, make them as narrowly tailored as possible. Non-solicitation provisions (preventing founders from poaching customers or employees) are generally more enforceable across jurisdictions than broad competitive restrictions.
