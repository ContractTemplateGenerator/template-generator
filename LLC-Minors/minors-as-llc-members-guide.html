Minors As LLC Members In The United States

Can a 14-year-old own part of an LLC? The short answer: almost always yes. The real question: how do you structure it safely?

üìä The Big Picture: Minors as LLC Members

‚úÖ Most States Allow Minor Members
‚ö†Ô∏è 5-6 States Require Adult Organizers
üè¶ Banks Prefer Adult Signatories
üìã Contract Law Real Risk Area
üéØ Key Takeaway

From a statutory standpoint, minors can be LLC members in virtually all U.S. states. From a risk-management standpoint, you should structure ownership through custodians, trusts, or manager-managed arrangements to avoid contract capacity problems.

üîç Three Critical Distinctions

üë§ Organizer

Who: Person who signs and files articles of organization with the state

Age Rules: Some states require 18+ (CO, IL, MN, OR, TX)

After Formation: Doesn't need to remain involved

üíº Member

Who: Owner of economic and governance rights

Age Rules: Almost no states set minimum age

Key Issue: Contract capacityMinors can typically void contracts they sign until reaching majority, creating risk for LLCs if signing agreements

‚öôÔ∏è Manager

Who: Person authorized to bind the LLC in contracts

Age Rules: Some states require 18+ for officers/agents

Best Practice: Always appoint an adult manager when minor is member

üí° The Real Problem Isn't Ownership‚ÄîIt's Signing Authority

A minor can own an LLC interest in most states without issue. The problem arises when that minor tries to sign an operating agreement, lease, vendor contract, or bank signature card. Under general contract law, those agreements may be voidable by the minor, creating uncertainty for everyone involved.

üíº Practical Use Cases

üé¨ Teen Influencer

16-year-old YouTuber with sponsorship income. Parent forms LLC as manager, holds membership via UTMA. Contracts signed by parent manager.

üè† Family Real Estate

Parents gift LLC interests in rental property to children (ages 10-17) via UTMA custodianships for estate planning. Parents remain managers.

üíª Student Startup

17-year-old co-founds tech company. Adult co-founder serves as manager; teen is member. At 18, teen signs operating agreement ratification.

üó∫Ô∏è State-by-State Guide: Focus States

Most states don't address age in their LLC member definitions. The variations appear in organizer requirements and practical considerations. Here are the key jurisdictions:

üìç California PERMISSIVE

Statute: Corp. Code ¬ß 17701.02 defines "member" as "a person" with no age limit.

Organizers: No age requirement

Practice: UTMA custodial structures very common; practitioners warn against direct minor membership due to capacity risks

üìç Delaware PERMISSIVE

Statute: 6 Del. C. ch. 18 ‚Äì "person" includes individuals, no age term

Organizers: No age requirement

Practice: Flexible; trusts and custodial arrangements frequently used for asset protection

üìç Texas ORGANIZER 18+

Statute: BOC ¬ß 101.102 ‚Äì person may be member "unless the person lacks capacity"

Organizers: Must be 18+ or entity (SOS guidance)

Practice: Minor can be member if adult files formation documents; manager-managed recommended

üìç Florida PERMISSIVE

Statute: Ch. 605 ‚Äì no age limit on members

Organizers: No age requirement

Practice: Florida UTMA (Ch. 710) explicitly used for LLC interests; "custodian for [Minor] under FUTMA" common structure

üìç New York PERMISSIVE

Statute: LLC Law ¬ß 102(q) ‚Äì "member" is a "person," no age specified

Organizers: At least one must be 18+ (practice guidance)

Practice: Guardian or custodian structures standard when minor involved

üìç Wyoming PERMISSIVE

Statute: RULLCA adoption ‚Äì "member" is a "person," silent on age

Organizers: No age requirement

Practice: Popular for asset protection; trusts and custodial structures common

üìç Colorado ORGANIZER 18+

Statute: ¬ß 7-80-203 ‚Äì organizer must be 18+; need not be member

Members: No explicit age limit in member definition

Note: Some sources incorrectly state minors can't be members; statute only restricts organizers

üìç Illinois ORGANIZER 18+

Statute: 805 ILCS 180/5-1(a) ‚Äì organizer cannot be "natural person under 18"

Members: Separate definition; no age restriction stated

Practice: Adult files formation; minor or custodian admitted as member afterward

üìç Nevada PERMISSIVE

Statute: NRS Ch. 86 ‚Äì "member" defined as "person" with membership interest; no age specified

Organizers: No age requirement

Practice: Popular for asset protection and privacy; minor ownership via trusts and custodial structures common

üìç Pennsylvania ORGANIZER 18+

Statute: 15 Pa.C.S. ¬ß 8821 ‚Äì organizer must be "individual of full age" (18+) or entity

Members: No age limit in member definition

Practice: Adult organizer required for formation; minor may hold membership interest thereafter

üìç Arizona PERMISSIVE

Statute: A.R.S. Title 29, Ch. 18 (RULLCA adoption) ‚Äì "member" is "person"; no age term

Organizers: No explicit age requirement

Practice: Manager-managed structures with adult managers recommended when minor involved

üìç Washington PERMISSIVE

Statute: RCW 25.15 ‚Äì "member" defined broadly; no minimum age specified

Organizers: No age requirement in statute

Practice: UTMA custodial arrangements and family trusts frequently used

üìç Massachusetts PERMISSIVE

Statute: M.G.L. Ch. 156C ‚Äì "member" is any "person"; no age restriction

Organizers: No age requirement stated

Practice: Conservative banking environment; custodial structures strongly preferred

üìç Georgia PERMISSIVE

Statute: O.C.G.A. ¬ß 14-11 ‚Äì "member" includes any "person" admitted; no age term

Organizers: No age requirement

Practice: Family business LLCs commonly use UTMA or trust structures for minor children

üìç New Jersey PERMISSIVE

Statute: N.J.S.A. 42:2B ‚Äì "member" defined as "person" with ownership interest; no age specified

Organizers: No age requirement

Practice: Custodial and trust ownership common; manager-managed structure recommended

üìã Other States: Oregon & Minnesota
‚ñº
Oregon

ORS 63.044 requires organizers to be "18 years of age or older" but doesn't restrict member age. Structure: adult organizer forms LLC, then admits minor member.

Minnesota

Ch. 322C (RULLCA) ‚Äì practice forms assume "natural person of full age" for organizers; member age not restricted by statute.

‚úÖ Summary Across All 50 States

Looking across the LLC statutes themselves, the pattern is surprisingly uniform. Where age is mentioned at all, it is almost always in provisions that require an individual organizer (the person signing the formation document) to be at least 18 years old in that state (for example, Colorado, Illinois, Oregon, Pennsylvania, Texas and a few others).

The operative definitions of "member" are framed simply in terms of a "person" and do not include a minimum age. There is generally no rule preventing a minor from being an LLC member. When secondary sources talk about "exceptions," they are really describing organizer-age requirements and the practical issue that minors have limited contract capacity, not a clear statutory ban on a minor owning a membership interest.

üèóÔ∏è How to Structure Minor Ownership Safely

The goal: give the minor economic participation while keeping contracts, banking, and governance stable. Here are the four main approaches:

üè¶ UTMA/UGMA Custodial

Structure: Adult custodian holds interest "for [Minor] under [State] UTMA"

Pros: Simple, widely recognized, custodian signs all docs

Cons: Auto-transfer at age 18/21/25 (can't defer control)

Best For: Gifts to children, family businesses

üìú Trust Ownership

Structure: Trust holds membership interest; trustee manages

Pros: Flexible transfer timing, conditions, creditor protection

Cons: More complex, requires trust document

Best For: Estate planning, staged vesting, high-value interests

‚öôÔ∏è Manager-Managed LLC

Structure: Minor is member; adult(s) are designated managers

Pros: Separates ownership from signing authority

Cons: Minor still signs operating agreement unless using custodian

Best For: Teen entrepreneurs with adult co-founders

‚öñÔ∏è Court-Appointed Guardian

Structure: Guardian of estate holds and manages interest

Pros: Court oversight, mandatory for some settlements

Cons: Expensive, requires ongoing court supervision

Best For: Litigation settlements, inheritances

üìö Understanding UTMA/UGMA Custodial Accounts

üéì What is UTMA/UGMA? (Click to expand)
‚ñº

UGMA (Uniform Gifts to Minors Act): Older statute allowing adults to transfer cash and securities to minors via a custodian. The custodian manages assets until the minor reaches the statutory age (often 18 or 21).

UTMA (Uniform Transfers to Minors Act): Successor in most states. Allows "any kind of property" to be held custodially, including:

  • LLC membership interests
  • Real estate
  • Partnership interests
  • Intellectual property

How it works for LLCs:

  1. Membership interest is issued to "Anna M√ºller, as custodian for Max M√ºller under California UTMA"
  2. Anna (the custodian) manages, votes, and signs documents
  3. Max (the minor) is the beneficial owner
  4. When Max reaches the statutory age, Anna must transfer legal title to Max

Key difference from trusts: UTMA requires outright transfer at a fixed age; trusts can defer or condition transfer.

‚úÖ Recommended Structural Approach

üéØ Conservative Best Practice
  1. Form LLC in mainstream jurisdiction (DE, WY, CA, NY, FL, TX) with adult organizer
  2. Make LLC manager-managed with adult manager(s) having exclusive authority to bind
  3. Admit minor as member via UTMA custodian or trust ‚Äì adult fiduciary signs operating agreement
  4. Reserve all signature authority for adults (banking, leases, vendor contracts)
  5. If minor is entrepreneurial, give them internal "founder" title but keep agency powers with adults

This preserves economic and educational benefits for the minor while substantially reducing contract capacity risk.

  • Court issues Letters of Guardianship

üí¨ Frequently Asked Questions

‚ùì Can a minor be the sole member and manager of an LLC?
‚ñº

Technically: In most states, the LLC statute doesn't explicitly forbid this structure.

Practically: This is almost always unworkable:

  • Banks typically refuse to open accounts for minor-only signatories
  • Landlords and vendors may reject contracts signed by minors
  • Even if accepted, the minor can later disaffirm those contracts
  • Payment processors and KYC/AML compliance become difficult

Recommendation: Structure with adult manager even if minor is sole economic owner.

‚ùì Does a minor need a Social Security Number to be a member?
‚ñº

For membership itself: LLC statutes don't require an SSN as a condition of membership.

For tax reporting: If the LLC issues a Schedule K-1 to the minor (pass-through taxation), you'll need:

  • Social Security Number (for U.S. citizen/resident minors)
  • ITIN (Individual Taxpayer Identification Number) for some non-U.S. minors with U.S. filing obligations

For banking: Banks and brokers will require identifying numbers under KYC/AML rules. When using a custodian or trustee, the adult fiduciary's information is typically provided.

‚ùì How is income allocated to a minor member for tax purposes?
‚ñº

For U.S. tax purposes, a minor who is a member of a pass-through LLC is generally taxed on their share of income, regardless of whether cash is distributed.

Kiddie Tax: Certain investment income of children may be taxed at the parent's marginal rate above thresholds (IRC ¬ß 1(g)).

UTMA/Trust structures: Tax reporting becomes more complex; the custodian/trustee typically handles filing.

Important: Using a minor as a member is NOT a tax-avoidance strategy. In many cases, family income is effectively consolidated.

‚ùì What happens when the minor turns 18 (or 21)?
‚ñº

The answer depends on how the interest was held:

Direct membership: Nothing necessarily changes at majority; they become an adult member and lose the ability to disaffirm on the basis of minority (subject to grace periods).

UTMA/UGMA: Custodian is required by statute to transfer legal title to the now-adult beneficiary at the statutory age. Update membership ledger and consider having the young adult ratify the operating agreement.

Trust: The trust instrument controls when and how control passes‚Äîmay be age 25, 30, or tied to conditions (graduation, employment, etc.).

Best Practice: Operating agreements should anticipate this transition with buy-sell mechanisms, staged vesting, or purchase options.

‚ùì Can a non-U.S. minor be a member of a U.S. LLC?
‚ñº

Yes. U.S. LLC statutes generally don't distinguish between U.S. and foreign individuals for membership purposes.

Complications:

  • Tax: Cross-border ownership may create U.S. filing obligations for the minor or custodial structure
  • Home country rules: The minor's country may have restrictions on children owning foreign business interests
  • KYC/AML: U.S. banks may require more extensive due diligence for foreign beneficial owners

These issues don't prevent membership but should be addressed in structuring.

‚ùì What if the parents disagree about putting LLC interest in minor's name?
‚ñº

When parents align: Gifting or allocating membership interests to a minor is straightforward (via UTMA or trust).

When parents disagree: The situation becomes more delicate:

  • In many states, one parent acting alone may make a valid UTMA gift or create a trust for the child
  • However, family-law considerations (divorce, custody, community property) can significantly impact this
  • In property division proceedings, the membership interest itself may become subject to claims

Mitigation: Carefully drafted operating agreements with transfer restrictions and buy-sell provisions can help, but cannot completely insulate against family-law disputes.

üìñ Understanding Minors as LLC Members: Detailed Analysis

‚öñÔ∏è

Parents, family businesses, and teen founders often ask a deceptively simple question: can a 15-year-old be a member of a U.S. LLC?

From a statutory standpoint, the answer is almost always yes. From a risk-management and drafting standpoint, the answer is: proceed carefully and structure it correctly.

üîç How U.S. LLC Law Looks at Minors as Members

Across U.S. jurisdictions, LLC statutes generally define three distinct roles:

  • The organizer: Signs and files formation documents with the Secretary of State
  • The members: Own the LLC (economic and governance rights)
  • The managers/managing members: Run the business and sign contracts on its behalf

Most LLC acts define "member"Person who has been admitted as a member and holds ownership interest in the LLC broadly as a "person" who has been admitted under the statute and operating agreement. "Person" includes natural persons and entities. Very few LLC statutes mention age at all.

üéØ The Key Insight

Age questions show up in two places: (1) some states require organizers to be 18+, and (2) general contract law says minors lack full capacity to contract. But membership itself is almost never restricted by age.

‚ö†Ô∏è Contract Capacity: The Real Risk

Under general U.S. contract law, a contract with a minor is typically enforceable against the adult party but "voidable" by the minor. This doesn't mean every contract is automatically invalid‚Äîit means the minor has a special right (usually until shortly after reaching majority) to avoid or disaffirm the contract.

When this matters:

üìù Operating Agreement

If signed directly by a minor, may be challenged as voidable. This creates uncertainty about capital commitments, buy-sell provisions, and dispute resolution clauses.

üè¢ Commercial Contracts

Leases, vendor agreements, or IP licenses executed by a minor "managing member" may be subject to challenge, creating risk for counterparties.

üè¶ Banking & Payments

Banks and payment processors are conservative‚Äîthey typically push back when a minor is the only member and manager, preferring adult signatories with full capacity.

Because of this risk, practitioners who are comfortable with minors as members still recommend keeping actual decision-making and signing authority with adults.

üó∫Ô∏è State-Level Variations That Matter

The broad pattern across all 50 states:

  • Most states: No express age limit in "member" definition
  • Several states: Explicit 18+ requirement for organizers (CO, IL, MN, OR, TX)
  • All states: Minor contract capacity governed by general state contract law
üéØ Practical Takeaway for All States

Delaware, California, New York, Florida, Wyoming, and most popular LLC jurisdictions do not set a minimum age for members. A handful require organizers to be 18+, but this doesn't prevent minors from being admitted as members after formation.

For your blog or client materials: "In most U.S. states, minors can be LLC members, but some states require at least one adult to file formation documents, and banks/counterparties will insist on adult managers."

üè¶ UTMA/UGMA: The American Custodial System

For international readers (especially German clients), it's worth understanding the two standard "custodial" regimes used for minors' assets in the U.S.:

üìä UGMA (Gifts)

Uniform Gifts to Minors Act: Allows adults to make gifts of cash or securities to minors, managed by custodian until specified age (usually 18 or 21).

üìã UTMA (Transfers)

Uniform Transfers to Minors Act: Expanded version allowing "any kind of property"‚Äîincluding LLC interests, real estate, and other assets‚Äîto be held custodially.

How UTMA works for LLCs:

  1. Membership ledger records member as: "Anna M√ºller, as custodian for Max M√ºller under the California Uniform Transfers to Minors Act"
  2. LLC treats the custodian as the person who can sign, vote, and receive notices
  3. Economically, the interest belongs to the minor beneficiary
  4. When child reaches statutory age, custodian must transfer control
‚úÖ Why Practitioners Love UTMA for LLCs

UTMA custodianships are simple, widely recognized, and comfortable for banks. Every document is signed by an adult with full capacity, while the minor retains beneficial ownership and tax treatment.

üéØ Recommended Structural Approach

If the goal is to give a minor economic participation while keeping contracts, banking, and governance stable, use this pattern:

  1. Formation: Form LLC in mainstream jurisdiction with adult organizer (or entity)
  2. Management: Make LLC manager-managed; appoint adult(s) as managers with exclusive authority to bind the LLC
  3. Membership: Admit minor via UTMA custodian or trust‚Äîoperating agreement reflects that adult fiduciary exercises all governance rights while beneficiary is underage
  4. Signing authority: Avoid placing minor in position to sign contracts on LLC's behalf
  5. If entrepreneurial minor: Consider internal "founder role" with title, but keep signature blocks and agency powers with adults

This preserves educational and economic benefits for the minor while substantially reducing capacity-related risk.
