Key clauses in an independent contractor agreement

1. Scope of work & deliverables

What this clause does: Defines exactly what work the contractor will perform, what deliverables are expected, and any deadlines or milestones.

Why it matters: Prevents scope creep and disputes about what was promised. Without clear deliverables, you can't enforce quality standards or timely completion.

What can go wrong: Vague descriptions like "consulting services" lead to disputes. Be specific: "Develop a responsive website with 5 pages, mobile-optimized, delivered by March 15."

When to get help: If the project is complex, involves multiple phases, or you're unsure how to define acceptance criteria.

2. Term & termination

What this clause does: Sets the duration of the relationship and how either party can end it.

Why it matters: Protects both sides. "At-will" termination gives flexibility; "for cause only" gives contractors more security but limits your flexibility.

What can go wrong: No termination clause means you're stuck until the work is done. A 90-day notice period may be too long if performance is poor.

When to get help: For long-term engagements or when contractor insists on "for cause only" termination.

3. Payment, invoicing & expenses

What this clause does: Specifies how much you'll pay (hourly, flat fee, milestone-based), when invoices are due, and who pays for expenses.

Why it matters: Prevents payment disputes. Clear payment terms also help demonstrate this is truly an independent contractor relationship (not employment).

What can go wrong: "Net 30" payment terms without late fees means contractors have no leverage for late payment. Vague expense policies lead to surprise bills.

When to get help: For large projects, consider milestone payments tied to deliverable acceptance to protect both sides.

4. IP ownership & license

What this clause does: Determines who owns the work product. Options: full assignment to client, license to client, or contractor retains ownership.

Why it matters: If you're paying for custom work, you usually want to own it. But "work made for hire" doesn't automatically apply to contractors under U.S. law‚Äîyou need explicit assignment language.

What can go wrong: Without clear assignment, contractor may own the work and only grant you a limited license. This is catastrophic for core business assets.

When to get help: Always, for any creative or technical work where IP matters. This is one of the most commonly litigated contract issues.

5. Confidentiality & non-disclosure

What this clause does: Protects your confidential business information, customer data, trade secrets, etc.

Why it matters: Contractors often see sensitive information. Without confidentiality obligations, they can share it with anyone‚Äîincluding competitors.

What can go wrong: Overly broad definitions ("all information") may be unenforceable. Too short a duration (1 year) doesn't protect long-term trade secrets.

When to get help: If contractor will access customer data, source code, financial information, or strategic plans.

6. Independent contractor status

What this clause does: Clarifies that contractor is not an employee‚Äîthey pay their own taxes, provide their own tools, control their own schedule.

Why it matters: Misclassifying an employee as a contractor triggers massive tax penalties, wage claims, and benefits liability.

What can go wrong: A clause alone doesn't make someone a contractor. If you control when/where/how they work, they may legally be an employee regardless of what the contract says.

When to get help: Before engaging anyone full-time, requiring set hours, or integrating them deeply into your team.

7. Liability, indemnity & limitations

What this clause does: Caps the contractor's liability (typically to fees paid) and allocates risk for third-party claims.

Why it matters: Without a cap, contractor faces unlimited liability for any mistake. Without indemnity, you bear all risk if contractor's work infringes IP or violates laws.

What can go wrong: Unlimited liability scares away good contractors. But excluding liability for gross negligence or IP infringement gives contractors too much protection.

When to get help: For high-risk work (public-facing software, regulated industries, high-value projects).

8. Governing law & dispute resolution

What this clause does: Chooses which state's law applies and whether disputes go to court or arbitration.

Why it matters: Avoids fighting over jurisdiction. Arbitration can be faster and cheaper than court, but you give up the right to appeal.

What can go wrong: Agreeing to contractor's home state may put you at a disadvantage. Mandatory arbitration with split costs may be expensive for small disputes.

When to get help: For out-of-state or international contractors, or when the project value justifies custom dispute resolution (mediation first, then arbitration).

Variations & negotiation angles

A. Common variations

  • Short-form vs. long-form: For quick projects under $5K, a 2-page agreement covering scope, payment, IP, and confidentiality may suffice. For ongoing engagements or complex projects, use the full template.
  • Retainer vs. project-based: Retainer agreements need auto-renewal clauses, monthly hour caps, and rollover/use-it-or-lose-it provisions. Project-based agreements should tie payment to deliverable acceptance.
  • U.S. vs. international contractors: For non-U.S. contractors, add tax withholding clauses (W-8 collection), clarify currency and payment method, and consider export control restrictions for technical data.
  • Client-friendly vs. contractor-friendly: This template is client-friendly (broad IP assignment, at-will termination, liability caps favoring client). A balanced version would allow contractor to retain ownership of pre-existing tools and limit non-solicitation scope.

B. If you are the contractor

When signing a client's contractor agreement, watch out for:

  • Overly broad IP assignment: Push back if client wants to own your pre-existing tools, templates, or frameworks. Carve those out and grant a license instead.
  • Unlimited liability: Insist on a liability cap (usually fees paid in the prior 6-12 months). Exception: you should remain liable for IP infringement and gross negligence.
  • Long non-compete or non-solicitation: 6-12 months is reasonable; 2+ years is not. Geographic and industry scope should be narrow.
  • Net 90+ payment terms: Push for Net 30 or milestone-based payments, especially for large projects. Add late fees (1.5% per month is standard).
  • Unfavorable termination: For long projects, push for "for cause only" termination or a notice period (30 days) plus payment for work completed.

C. Typical negotiation "give and take"

Usually negotiable:

  • Payment terms (Net 15 vs 30 vs 45)
  • Milestone structure and acceptance criteria
  • Liability cap amount
  • IP ownership of pre-existing materials (carve-outs)
  • Non-solicitation duration and scope

Rarely negotiable (red flags if client won't budge):

  • Reasonable confidentiality obligations
  • Assignment of work product created under the agreement
  • Indemnity for contractor's IP infringement or violations of law
  • Governing law (client's home state is standard)

FAQ & jurisdiction notes

Is this agreement valid in all U.S. states?

Yes, with caveats. Basic contract principles are consistent across U.S. states, but some states have specific rules:

  • California: Non-compete clauses are generally unenforceable (except in sale of business). Non-solicitation of employees may be OK if narrow. AB5 makes it much harder to classify workers as contractors.
  • New York: Freelance workers have special payment protections under the Freelance Isn't Free Act (contracts over $800 must be in writing, payment within 30 days).
  • Massachusetts: Independent contractor test is very strict (ABC test). Misclassification penalties are severe.

Always specify your state in the governing law clause and consult local counsel if you're unsure about classification.

Can I reuse this with multiple contractors?

Yes, but customize each time:

  • Update party names, addresses, and dates
  • Revise Exhibit A (scope of work) for each project‚Äînever reuse a prior scope
  • Adjust payment terms and IP provisions based on the specific engagement
  • Consider whether each contractor truly qualifies as independent (don't rubber-stamp)

What if the contractor is outside the U.S.?

Additional considerations:

  • Tax withholding: Collect Form W-8BEN (individual) or W-8BEN-E (entity) to claim treaty benefits and avoid 30% backup withholding on U.S.-source income.
  • Currency and payment method: Specify currency (USD) and payment method (wire, PayPal, Wise, etc.). Be aware of your bank's international wire fees.
  • Export controls: If contractor will access technical data or source code, check if it's export-controlled (ITAR, EAR). You may need an export license.
  • Data privacy: If contractor will access EU personal data, you may need a data processing agreement (DPA) and Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs).
  • Time zones and language: Clarify communication expectations and whether English is the governing language.

Do I need a separate NDA?

Not necessarily. This template includes a confidentiality clause that should cover most situations. Use a separate NDA if:

  • You're sharing confidential information before the contractor agreement is signed (e.g., during bidding or scoping)
  • The relationship is primarily about sharing information rather than performing services
  • You need mutual confidentiality (both sides sharing sensitive info)

What's the difference between this and an employment agreement?

Key differences:

Factor Employee Independent Contractor
Control Employer controls when, where, and how work is done Contractor controls methods; client controls result
Tools & equipment Employer provides Contractor provides own
Taxes Employer withholds income tax, pays payroll tax Contractor pays own (1099 reporting)
Benefits Eligible for health insurance, 401(k), etc. Not eligible
Relationship Ongoing, exclusive Project-based, can work for others

If in doubt, consult an employment attorney. Misclassification penalties are severe.

When should I get a custom version?

Consider hiring an attorney to draft or review if:

  • Contract value exceeds $50,000
  • Contractor will create mission-critical IP (core product, proprietary algorithms)
  • You're in a regulated industry (healthcare, finance, defense)
  • Contractor will access sensitive data (PII, PHI, financial records)
  • Contractor is international and you're unsure about tax or export compliance
  • You're hiring multiple contractors and need a scalable template + process
  • Contractor is pushing back on key terms and you need negotiation support
