ü§î Decision Guide: When to Choose Each Entity

üè¢ Choose C Corporation If...
‚ñº
  • You're raising venture capital or institutional funding
  • You plan to go public (IPO) eventually
  • You have or want foreign investors
  • You need multiple classes of stock (common, preferred, etc.)
  • You want to retain earnings in the business (lower 21% corporate rate vs. personal rates up to 37%)
  • You're a tech startup with significant R&D and losses (can carry forward losses)
  • You want maximum flexibility for equity compensation (ISOs, RSUs, etc.)

Example: Tech startup raising a seed round from accelerator and angel investors, planning Series A next year.

üíº Choose S Corporation If...
‚ñº
  • You're a profitable small business and want to save on self-employment tax
  • All owners are U.S. citizens/residents (no foreign shareholders)
  • You have 100 or fewer shareholders
  • You don't need multiple classes of stock
  • You want pass-through taxation (avoid double taxation)
  • You prefer corporate structure formalities over LLC flexibility
  • You're a professional service business (consulting, medical, legal, etc.)

Tax Savings Example: If your business makes $150K profit, as LLC you'd pay ~$21K in SE tax. As S Corp paying yourself $80K salary + $70K distribution, you save ~$10K in SE tax on the distribution portion.

Example: Consulting firm with 3 U.S.-based partners, profitable, no plans to raise outside capital.

üõ°Ô∏è Choose Wyoming LLC If...
‚ñº
  • Asset protection is your primary goal (real estate, investments)
  • You want maximum operational flexibility (no board meetings, minutes, etc.)
  • You're a single owner or have few partners
  • You don't plan to raise institutional capital
  • You want privacy (members not publicly disclosed)
  • You're holding passive investments (real estate, crypto, securities)

Note: Wyoming LLC can elect S Corp or C Corp taxation, giving you LLC asset protection + corporate tax treatment.

Example: Real estate investor holding multiple rental properties in separate LLCs for liability isolation.

üè¢ Wyoming C Corporation: Detailed Information

üìò What is a C Corporation?

A C Corporation is a separate legal entity owned by shareholders. It pays corporate income tax on profits (21% federal, 0% Wyoming), and shareholders pay tax on dividends received (double taxation). However, C Corps offer maximum flexibility for ownership, stock classes, and raising capital‚Äîmaking them the standard choice for venture-backed startups and companies planning to go public.

‚úÖ Advantages of Wyoming C Corporation

üöÄ Best for Raising Venture Capital
‚ñº

Why VCs prefer C Corps:

  • Can issue preferred stock with liquidation preferences, anti-dilution, etc.
  • Clean cap table management with multiple share classes
  • Standard legal structure VCs are familiar with
  • No ownership restrictions (unlike S Corp's 100-shareholder limit)
  • Can have corporate and foreign investors

Conversion path: If you start as LLC or S Corp, you can convert to C Corp later‚Äîbut it's cleaner to start as C Corp if VC funding is planned.

üí∞ Lower Tax Rate on Retained Earnings
‚ñº

21% federal corporate tax (vs. up to 37% personal income tax) makes C Corps attractive if you plan to reinvest profits in the business rather than distribute them.

Wyoming bonus: 0% state corporate income tax, so you only pay the 21% federal rate.

Strategy: Growth companies can retain earnings for R&D, expansion, acquisitions at lower tax rate. Founders take modest salary + stock options (taxed only when exercised/sold).

üìà Equity Compensation Flexibility
‚ñº

C Corps can offer:

  • Incentive Stock Options (ISOs): Favorable tax treatment for employees (taxed at capital gains rates if held long enough)
  • Non-Qualified Stock Options (NSOs): More flexible grant terms
  • Restricted Stock Units (RSUs): Common in tech companies
  • Stock Appreciation Rights (SARs)

S Corps can only offer NSOs (not ISOs). LLCs have complex profit interest structures.

üåç No Ownership Restrictions
‚ñº

C Corps can have:

  • Unlimited shareholders
  • Foreign (non-U.S.) shareholders
  • Corporate shareholders (e.g., holding company structure)
  • Trust shareholders
  • Partnership shareholders

S Corp restrictions: Max 100 shareholders, U.S. citizens/residents only, no corporate shareholders.

‚ö†Ô∏è Disadvantages & Considerations

üí∏ Double Taxation
‚ñº

How it works:

  • Corporation pays 21% federal tax on profits
  • Dividends to shareholders taxed at 0%-20% (qualified dividends) plus 3.8% NIIT
  • Total effective rate: ~36%-40% on distributed profits

Mitigation strategies:

  • Pay yourself reasonable salary (deductible by corporation, taxed once to you)
  • Retain earnings in corporation for growth (taxed once at 21%)
  • Defer dividends until qualified small business stock (QSBS) exclusion applies (up to $10M tax-free if held 5+ years)
üìã More Compliance Requirements
‚ñº

C Corps must maintain:

  • Annual shareholder meeting + minutes
  • Board of directors meeting(s) + minutes
  • Corporate bylaws and resolutions
  • Stock ledger and cap table
  • Separate corporate bank account and books
  • Annual reports to Wyoming ($62/year)
  • Federal corporate tax return (Form 1120)

Failure to maintain formalities can lead to "piercing the corporate veil" (losing liability protection).

üìã Wyoming C Corp Formation Process

  1. Choose corporate name (must include "Corporation," "Corp.," "Incorporated," or "Inc.")
  2. File Articles of Incorporation with Wyoming Secretary of State ($102 fee)
  3. Appoint registered agent with Wyoming street address
  4. Draft corporate bylaws (internal governance rules)
  5. Hold organizational board meeting (appoint officers, adopt bylaws, issue stock)
  6. Issue stock certificates to initial shareholders
  7. Obtain EIN from IRS (required for corporations)
  8. File Form 2553 if electing S Corp (within 2.5 months of formation or by March 15 of next year)

üíº Wyoming S Corporation: Detailed Information

üìò What is an S Corporation?

An S Corporation is a tax election, not a separate entity type. You form a Wyoming Corporation (C Corp by default), then elect S Corp status with the IRS by filing Form 2553. S Corps are "pass-through" entities: profits and losses flow through to shareholders' personal tax returns, avoiding double taxation. The key benefit: you can pay yourself a reasonable salary (subject to payroll taxes) and take additional profits as distributions (not subject to 15.3% self-employment tax), potentially saving thousands per year.

‚úÖ Advantages of S Corporation

üí∞ Self-Employment Tax Savings (Primary Benefit)
‚ñº

How it works:

  • LLC/Sole Prop: All profits subject to 15.3% SE tax (12.4% Social Security + 2.9% Medicare)
  • S Corp: Only salary subject to payroll taxes; distributions are NOT subject to SE tax

Example calculation:

  • Business profit: $150,000
  • As LLC: $150K √ó 15.3% = $22,950 in SE tax
  • As S Corp: Salary $80K (15.3% on this = $12,240), Distribution $70K (0% SE tax), Total payroll tax: $12,240
  • Savings: $10,710 per year

Important: Salary must be "reasonable" for your role/industry. IRS challenges unreasonably low salaries ($30K salary + $120K distribution would be flagged).

üö´ No Double Taxation
‚ñº

Unlike C Corps, S Corps have pass-through taxation:

  • Corporate income/losses flow to your personal tax return (Schedule K-1)
  • No corporate-level tax
  • Profits taxed once at your personal income tax rate (0%-37% federal + state)

Wyoming bonus: 0% state income tax, so you only pay federal income tax on your share of S Corp profits.

üõ°Ô∏è Liability Protection
‚ñº

As a corporation, S Corps provide:

  • Limited liability protection (shareholders not personally liable for business debts/lawsuits)
  • Corporate veil protection (if formalities maintained)
  • Creditor protection (creditors cannot seize corporate assets for personal debts)

Note: Wyoming LLCs have stronger asset protection (charging order protection), but S Corps still provide solid liability shielding.

üìä Easy Conversion from LLC
‚ñº

If you already have a Wyoming LLC, you can elect S Corp taxation without changing your entity:

  • Keep your LLC structure, operating agreement, and flexibility
  • File Form 2553 with IRS to elect S Corp tax treatment
  • Get pass-through taxation + SE tax savings
  • Maintain LLC asset protection (best of both worlds)

This is often the best choice for single-owner profitable businesses: LLC simplicity + S Corp tax savings.

‚ö†Ô∏è S Corporation Requirements & Restrictions

üë• Shareholder Restrictions (Critical)
‚ñº

To qualify for S Corp status, you must meet ALL of these requirements:

  • Max 100 shareholders (spouses count as one)
  • U.S. citizens or residents only (no foreign shareholders)
  • No corporate or partnership shareholders (individuals, estates, certain trusts only)
  • One class of stock (can have voting/non-voting, but same economic rights)

Violation consequences: If you breach any requirement, S Corp election is automatically terminated. Corporation reverts to C Corp taxation (double taxation) retroactively.

üíº Reasonable Salary Requirement
‚ñº

IRS requires S Corp shareholder-employees to pay themselves "reasonable compensation" for services performed:

  • Cannot pay $0 salary + $150K distribution (IRS will recharacterize distribution as salary + penalties)
  • Reasonable = market rate for similar work in your industry/geography
  • Safe harbor (no official rule, but practitioners suggest): 40-60% of profits as salary, remainder as distribution

IRS audit risk: Low salary + high distribution is red flag. I recommend working with a CPA to document reasonable salary determination.

üìã Additional Compliance Requirements
‚ñº

S Corps must:

  • File Form 2553 within 2 months 15 days of formation, or by March 15 to elect for current year
  • File Form 1120-S (S Corp tax return) annually, even if no income
  • Issue K-1s to all shareholders showing their share of income/loss/deductions
  • Run payroll for shareholder-employees (W-2s, quarterly 941s, unemployment tax, etc.)
  • Maintain corporate formalities (board meetings, minutes, bylaws) to avoid S Corp termination

Payroll costs: Budget $500-1,500/year for payroll processing service + CPA for 1120-S filing.

üö´ When S Corp Doesn't Make Sense
‚ñº

Avoid S Corp if:

  • You're not profitable yet (no tax savings if no income; added payroll/compliance costs not worth it)
  • Profit < ~$60K/year (reasonable salary would be most of profit; SE tax savings too small to justify added complexity)
  • You have foreign investors (disqualifies S Corp status)
  • You need multiple stock classes (for VC funding, liquidation preferences, etc.)
  • You plan to raise institutional capital (VCs/PE funds prefer C Corps)

Rule of thumb: S Corp tax savings typically outweigh added costs when profit exceeds $60-80K and you're a U.S.-based small business not raising outside capital.

‚è∞ When to Elect S Corporation Status

üìÖ Critical Deadline: Form 2553 Filing

To elect S Corp status for current year, you must file Form 2553 by the earlier of: (1) 2 months and 15 days after start of tax year you want election to take effect, or (2) 2 months and 15 days after forming your corporation. For calendar-year corporations: March 15 deadline. Miss this deadline? Election applies to following year. We can handle Form 2553 filing for you with our S Corp Conversion service.
