Bank Compliance for Russian Investors

Navigate OFAC sanctions, compliant transfer methods, and US banking under current restrictions

Critical Information

IMPORTANT: This page discusses legal compliance for non-sanctioned Russian nationals. Being Russian does not automatically mean you are sanctioned, but enhanced due diligence applies.

Bank Options

Since 2022, Russian nationals face the most challenging banking environment of any major investor group. Direct transfers from Russian banks are largely blocked, and US banks apply maximum scrutiny to Russian-connected transactions. However, legal pathways remain for non-sanctioned individuals with properly documented, compliant capital.

Current Sanctions Landscape

â–¼

US sanctions on Russia are extensive but NOT comprehensive. Understanding what is and is not sanctioned is critical.

What IS Sanctioned

  • SDN-listed individuals and entities
  • Most major Russian banks (Sberbank, VTB, etc.)
  • Russian Central Bank transactions
  • Certain sectors (defense, technology)
  • Occupied Ukrainian regions
  • Oligarchs on sanctions lists

What is NOT Sanctioned

  • Ordinary Russian citizens
  • Some smaller Russian banks
  • Funds held outside Russia (legally)
  • Personal savings and investments
  • Russian passport holders abroad
  • Non-SDN business owners

Key Sanctions Programs

  • Executive Order 14024 (Russia harmful activities)
  • Sectoral Sanctions (SSI)
  • Correspondent banking prohibitions
  • SWIFT disconnections
  • Export controls (EAR)

Self-Check Resources

  • OFAC SDN Search Tool
  • Consolidated Sanctions List
  • EU sanctions database
  • UK sanctions list
  • Professional sanctions screening
Sanctions change frequently. Always verify current status before any transaction.

OFAC Screening Process

â–¼

Every transaction involving Russian nationals undergoes intensive OFAC screening. Here is what banks check.

Name Screening

  • SDN (Specially Designated Nationals) list
  • Sectoral Sanctions Identifications
  • Non-SDN Menu-Based Sanctions
  • Fuzzy matching for name variants
  • Cyrillic-Latin transliteration checks

Address and Entity Screening

  • Russian address verification
  • Business entity ownership
  • Beneficial ownership disclosure
  • 50% ownership rule for entities
  • Associated persons and companies
Russian names have multiple transliterations. Banks check all variants (e.g., Sergei/Sergey, -ov/-off).

Account Opening Challenges

Common Rejection Reasons

  • Funds originating from sanctioned banks
  • Incomplete beneficial ownership disclosure
  • Connection to sanctioned individuals
  • Insufficient source of funds documentation

How to Improve Chances

  • Use funds held outside Russia
  • Provide comprehensive documentation
  • Work with an attorney
  • Consider private banking

Documentation Requirements

Enhanced Due Diligence Documents

â–¼

Russian nationals should expect to provide more documentation than investors from other countries. Prepare these in advance.

Identity and Background

  • Valid passport (Russian and/or foreign)
  • Secondary ID documents
  • Proof of current residence
  • Employment history
  • Professional background

Source of Wealth

  • Employment income documentation
  • Business ownership records
  • Investment history
  • Real estate sale documentation
  • Inheritance records

Source of Funds (Specific)

  • Bank statements showing accumulation
  • Tax returns (translated)
  • Sale contracts and receipts
  • Wire transfer history
  • Intermediary bank documentation

Negative Confirmations

  • No SDN list presence (self-certification)
  • No connection to sanctioned entities
  • No government/military affiliation
  • No sanctioned bank origin
  • Attorney opinion letter (helpful)
An attorney opinion letter confirming sanctions compliance significantly helps bank acceptance.

Compliant Transfer Methods

How to Move Funds Legally

â–¼

Direct transfers from Russian banks to US banks are effectively blocked. Compliant alternatives exist for legitimately-sourced funds.

Permitted Routes

  • Transfer from non-sanctioned third-country accounts
  • Funds already held outside Russia
  • Income earned outside Russia
  • Investment returns from non-Russian sources
  • Sale of non-Russian assets

Third-Country Banking

  • UAE (Dubai) - still accessible
  • Georgia - Russian-friendly
  • Armenia - accessible
  • Turkey - case by case
  • Note: Still requires clean funds

What to Avoid

  • Transfers from sanctioned banks
  • Correspondent banking through Russia
  • Crypto used to evade sanctions
  • False documentation
  • Nominee/straw person arrangements

Process Timeline

  • Account opening: 2-4 weeks typical
  • Wire processing: 3-10 business days
  • Compliance review: additional 1-2 weeks
  • Full clearance: 4-8 weeks realistic
Attempting to evade sanctions is a federal crime. Only use compliant methods with legitimate funds.

Frequently Asked Questions

Am I automatically sanctioned because I am Russian?

â–¼

No. Being a Russian citizen does not automatically make you sanctioned. Sanctions target specific individuals, entities, and sectors. You can verify your status using OFAC's SDN search tool. However, banks will apply enhanced due diligence to Russian nationals regardless of sanctions status.

Can I transfer money from Sberbank or VTB?

â–¼

No. Sberbank and VTB are under full blocking sanctions. Direct transfers from these banks will be blocked. You would need to move funds to a non-sanctioned bank first (which may also be difficult from within Russia), and even then, US banks will scrutinize the ultimate origin.

What if I have dual citizenship?

â–¼

Dual citizenship with a non-Russian country (especially a Western country) can help, but banks will still screen for Russian connections. Use your non-Russian passport for account opening, but be prepared to disclose Russian citizenship if asked. Never hide it - that creates far bigger problems if discovered.

Can I use cryptocurrency to transfer funds?

â–¼

Using cryptocurrency specifically to evade sanctions is illegal under OFAC regulations. If you convert sanctioned funds to crypto and then to USD, you are violating sanctions. US exchanges and banks will ask about the source of crypto-derived funds. Legitimate crypto holdings are fine, but not as a sanctions evasion mechanism.

What about funds I had outside Russia before 2022?

â–¼

Funds legitimately held outside Russia before the sanctions escalation are generally the easiest to use. You will need documentation showing when and how the funds were moved, and that they were not sourced from now-sanctioned entities. Account statements from before 2022 are very helpful.

Should I work with a lawyer for banking issues?

â–¼

For Russian nationals, I strongly recommend working with an attorney experienced in sanctions compliance. An attorney can provide an opinion letter, help prepare documentation, and communicate with banks' compliance departments. The cost is modest compared to the cost of failed transactions or account closures.
