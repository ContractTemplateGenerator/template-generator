Unreasonable Insurance Claim Delays

When your insurance company stalls, stonewalls, or drags out your claim indefinitely, they may be acting in bad faith. Learn the deadlines insurers must follow and how to hold them accountable.

Why Insurers Delay Claims

Insurance companies profit from holding onto premium dollars. Every day they delay paying your claim, they earn interest on that money - and you suffer the consequences. Some delays are legitimate (complex claims require investigation), but many are deliberate bad faith tactics designed to:

  • Pressure you to accept less: Financial desperation leads to accepting lowball offers
  • Hope you give up: Many policyholders abandon claims out of frustration
  • Run out the clock: Delays may push you past statutes of limitations
  • Earn investment income: Insurers profit from holding your money
  • Wait for circumstances to change: Your situation may weaken your claim

The good news: most states have regulations requiring insurers to process claims within specific timeframes. Violating these deadlines can constitute bad faith.

State-Mandated Claim Timelines

Every state has regulations governing how quickly insurers must respond to claims. While specific deadlines vary, here are common requirements:

Action Required Typical Deadline Notes
Acknowledge claim receipt 10-15 days Must be in writing
Begin investigation Immediately Cannot wait for documentation
Request additional information 15-30 days Must specify what's needed
Accept or deny claim 30-45 days From receipt of complete information
Issue payment after acceptance 5-30 days Some states require immediate payment
Respond to communications 15-30 days Cannot ignore policyholder inquiries
🐻 California Claim Timelines (Cal. Code Regs. tit. 10, Section 2695.7)

California has some of the strictest claim handling deadlines in the nation:

  • 15 days: Acknowledge receipt of claim in writing
  • 15 days: Provide necessary claim forms and instructions
  • 40 days: Accept or deny claim (from receipt of proof of claim)
  • 30 days: Issue payment after acceptance
  • Every 30 days: If more time needed, send written status update with reasons

Failure to meet these deadlines can result in Department of Insurance penalties and support a bad faith lawsuit.

Common Delay Tactics and How to Respond

🔍 The "Ongoing Investigation" Excuse

The insurer claims they're "still investigating" without explaining what they're investigating or providing a timeline. Months go by with no progress.

Your Response: Send a written demand asking for: (1) specific aspects still being investigated, (2) what additional information they need from you, (3) a timeline for completion. Cite your state's claim handling regulations.

📝 Endless Documentation Requests

The insurer keeps requesting more documents, often asking for the same things repeatedly or requesting information that's irrelevant to your claim.

Your Response: Provide a comprehensive written response listing every document you've already submitted with dates. Ask for a single, complete list of everything still needed. Most states require insurers to request all needed documentation at once, not piecemeal.

👤 The Disappearing Adjuster

Your assigned adjuster doesn't return calls or emails. When you finally reach someone, they say your adjuster is "no longer with the company" and your file is being reassigned.

Your Response: Request the name and direct contact information for your new adjuster in writing. Ask for written confirmation that all previous communications and documentation remain in your file. Send a summary of your claim status to the new adjuster.

📁 "We Never Received That"

The insurer claims they never received documents you've already sent, forcing you to resubmit and restart the clock.

Your Response: Always send important documents via certified mail, email with read receipts, or fax with confirmation. Keep copies of everything. When they claim non-receipt, provide your proof of delivery and demand immediate processing.

🏥 Waiting for Medical Records Forever

For injury claims, the insurer claims they're waiting on medical records - even when you've provided authorizations and records have been sent.

Your Response: Contact your medical providers directly to confirm records were sent. Obtain copies yourself and submit them directly. Ask the insurer to specify exactly which records they're missing and from which providers.

📊 Expert Reviews That Never End

The insurer sends your claim for "independent" review by engineers, doctors, or other experts, then claims they're waiting on the report - indefinitely.

Your Response: Request the name and credentials of any expert reviewing your claim. Ask for a timeline for completion. If excessive time passes, you may need to hire your own expert and demand the insurer's expert report be produced.
Pro Tip: The Paper Trail is Everything

Document every interaction with your insurer. After phone calls, send an email: "Per our conversation today at [time] with [representative], you stated [summary]. Please confirm this is accurate." This creates evidence for a bad faith claim.

When Delays Become Bad Faith

Not every delay is bad faith - some claims genuinely take time to investigate. However, delays cross into bad faith territory when:

  • The insurer misses regulatory deadlines without explanation
  • Delays appear designed to pressure you into accepting less
  • The insurer provides false or misleading reasons for delays
  • Requests for information are unreasonable or duplicative
  • The insurer fails to respond to your communications
  • Delays continue after you've provided all requested information
  • The insurer ignores evidence supporting your claim

The Financial Impact of Delays

When proving bad faith, I help clients document the real cost of delays:

  • Interest on loans taken to cover the loss
  • Rental costs while waiting for property repairs
  • Medical bills going to collections
  • Credit score damage
  • Lost business income
  • Emotional distress from prolonged uncertainty
Warning: Don't Let Delays Run Out Your Clock

Statutes of limitations continue running while insurers delay. If you've been waiting months for a response, consult an attorney to protect your rights before deadlines expire.

How to Fight Unreasonable Delays

Step 1: Send a Time-of-the-Essence Letter

Put the insurer on notice in writing. Cite the relevant state regulation, document how long you've been waiting, and demand a response within a specific timeframe (typically 10-15 days).

Step 2: File a Department of Insurance Complaint

Every state has an insurance department that accepts consumer complaints. While they can't force payment, their inquiry often gets results - and creates a regulatory record of the insurer's conduct.

🐻 California Department of Insurance Complaints

California DOI complaints are particularly effective. The Department has authority to fine insurers for claims handling violations and maintain public records of complaint history. File online at www.insurance.ca.gov.

Step 3: Escalate Within the Company

Request your claim be escalated to a supervisor or manager. Ask for their direct contact information in writing. Sometimes going above a stalling adjuster gets results.

Step 4: Send a Bad Faith Demand Letter

A well-crafted demand letter from an attorney puts the insurer on notice that their delay tactics are creating liability. This often prompts immediate action.

Step 5: Consider Legal Action

If delays continue despite your efforts, you may need to file suit. In many states, you can recover additional damages for bad faith delay beyond your original claim amount.
