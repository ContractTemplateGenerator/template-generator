What Is Insurance Bad Faith?

Bad faith occurs when an insurance company fails to honor its obligations to you - the policyholder - without a reasonable basis. Insurance is a contract built on mutual good faith: you pay premiums, and when you have a covered loss, the insurer pays your claim fairly and promptly.

When insurers break this promise through unreasonable denials, excessive delays, lowball offers, or failure to properly investigate, they're acting in "bad faith." This gives you the right to sue not just for your policy benefits, but for additional damages - sometimes far exceeding your policy limits.

Bad faith claims are powerful because they transform a simple contract dispute into a tort (wrongful conduct) case. This opens the door to emotional distress damages, punitive damages, and in California, attorney's fees under the Brandt doctrine.

California Bad Faith Law

Special protections for California policyholders

California has some of the strongest policyholder protections in the nation. The Fair Claims Settlement Practices Regulations create specific duties for insurers, and California courts have developed robust case law on bad faith claims. California also allows recovery of attorney's fees through the Brandt doctrine - a significant advantage for policyholders.
