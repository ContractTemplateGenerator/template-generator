When to Use This Guide

Use this guide if your employer owes you money for:

  • Regular wages - Salary, hourly pay, or day rate not paid
  • Overtime - Time-and-a-half or double-time not paid
  • Final paycheck - Wages due upon termination or resignation
  • Commissions & bonuses - Earned but unpaid incentive pay
  • Vacation/PTO payout - Accrued time not paid out

Legal Basis

California statutes and case law supporting your claim

Key California Labor Code Sections

Labor Code ¬ß 201
If you are discharged, your employer must pay all wages due immediately at the time of discharge.
Labor Code ¬ß 202
If you quit with at least 72 hours notice, wages are due on your last day. If you quit without notice, wages are due within 72 hours.
Labor Code ¬ß 203
If an employer willfully fails to pay wages due upon termination, the employee's wages continue as a penalty for up to 30 days at the same daily rate.
Labor Code ¬ß 226
Employers must provide itemized wage statements. Violations can result in penalties of $50-$4,000 per employee.
Labor Code ¬ß 1194
Employees can recover unpaid minimum wages or overtime, plus interest, plus reasonable attorney's fees and costs.

Under California Civil Code ¬ß 3289, you're entitled to interest at 10% per year on all unpaid amounts from the date they were due.

Sample Language

Copy-paste snippets for your demand letter

Opening Paragraph
I am writing to formally demand payment of wages owed to me for work performed during my employment with [COMPANY NAME]. As of [DATE], you have failed to pay me $[AMOUNT] in earned wages, in violation of California Labor Code.
Employment Background
I was employed by [COMPANY NAME] as a [JOB TITLE] from [START DATE] to [END DATE]. My agreed compensation was $[RATE] per [hour/week/month].
Legal Basis
Under California Labor Code Section 203, when an employer willfully fails to pay wages due upon termination, the employee's wages continue as a penalty for up to 30 days at the same daily rate. Additionally, under Labor Code Section 1194, I am entitled to recover unpaid wages plus interest at 10% per annum and reasonable attorney's fees.
Demand Paragraph
I hereby demand payment of $[TOTAL AMOUNT], representing unpaid wages of $[WAGES], plus waiting time penalties of $[PENALTY AMOUNT], plus interest. Payment must be received within 14 days of the date of this letter.
Consequences Warning
If I do not receive full payment by this deadline, I will pursue all available legal remedies, including filing a complaint with the California Labor Commissioner (DLSE), filing a lawsuit in civil court, and seeking recovery of the full amount owed plus penalties, interest, and attorney fees as provided by law.

Next Steps

What to do after sending your demand letter

Standard Response Deadline: Give 10-14 days for response. This provides enough time for payroll review while maintaining urgency.

If They Pay

Great! Make sure the payment includes:

  • All unpaid wages
  • Applicable waiting time penalties
  • Interest on late amounts

Get payment via certified check or direct deposit. Document everything.

If They Don't Pay or Dispute

Option 1
File with Labor Commissioner (DLSE)

Free process, no lawyer needed. DLSE investigates and can hold a hearing. Takes 6-12 months typically.

Option 2
Small Claims Court

Fast and cheap ($30-75 filing fee). Limit is $12,500 for individuals. No lawyers allowed in court.

Option 3
Civil Lawsuit

For larger amounts or when you want attorney fees. Can recover more in penalties. Lawyers often take wage cases on contingency.
