In re Marriage of Haines (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 277

California Court of Appeal, First District

Holding: In interspousal transactions where one spouse gains an advantage, a presumption of undue influence arises due to the fiduciary relationship under Fam. Code Â§721.

Facts: Husband induced wife to sign quitclaim deed transferring community real property to husband's separate property without adequate consideration.

Rule: Spouse claiming benefit of transaction must prove it was obtained through full disclosure, adequate consideration, and without undue influence.

Practice Tip: Cite Haines in demand letters alleging self-dealing transactions (e.g., spouse buying business asset from LLC at below-market price, or transferring community business interest to separate holding company).

In re Marriage of Brewer & Federici (2001) 93 Cal.App.4th 1334

California Court of Appeal, Second District

Holding: Managing spouse has an affirmative duty to acquire and disclose information concerning the value and character of community assets, including complex assets like pensions and business interests.

Facts: Husband failed to disclose full value of pension benefits during dissolution proceedings.

Rule: It is not enough for managing spouse to passively answer questionsâ€”they have a sua sponte (self-initiated) duty to investigate, obtain accurate information, and disclose it to the other spouse.

Practice Tip: Brewer & Federici is the anchor case for demand letters by non-managing spouses. Even if spouse doesn't ask for business financials, managing spouse has duty to disclose.

In re Marriage of Margulis (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 1252

California Court of Appeal, Second District

Holding: Managing spouse's sua sponte duty to furnish information extends to disposition and valuation of community assets, citing Brewer & Federici with approval.

Facts: Husband failed to disclose sale of business interest and disposition of proceeds during marriage.

Rule: Managing spouse cannot claim "she never asked" as defense to non-disclosure; duty is affirmative and ongoing.

Practice Tip: Combine Brewer and Margulis in demand letters: "You have an affirmative, sua sponte duty under Brewer and Margulis to disclose all business transactions, valuations, and dispositionsâ€”regardless of whether I specifically requested them."

In re Marriage of Duffy (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 923

California Court of Appeal, Fourth District

Holding: Fiduciary duty of spouse managing community assets includes a duty of care in investment choices, and expert testimony can be used to show investments were imprudent or excessively risky.

Facts: Husband made speculative real estate investments with community funds that resulted in significant losses; wife's expert testified investments violated prudent-investor standards.

Implication: Business decisions by managing spouse are not insulated by business-judgment ruleâ€”can be scrutinized under fiduciary duty of care standards.

Practice Tip: Use Duffy when challenging managing spouse's risky business ventures, undercapitalized expansions, or speculative investments with community funds.

In re Marriage of Walker (2006) 138 Cal.App.4th 1408

California Court of Appeal, Fourth District

Holding: Managing spouse who handles community assets has ongoing duty to account and provide transparent financial information.

Practice Tip: Frequently cited alongside Brewer to establish that managing spouse must maintain and produce accurate business records.

ğŸ¢ Corporate Governance & Shareholder Rights Cases

Jones v. H.F. Ahmanson & Co. (1969) 1 Cal.3d 93

California Supreme Court

Holding: Majority shareholders owe fiduciary duties to minority shareholders regarding use of control over the corporation; they must act in a "fair, just, and equitable" manner and may not use control to disadvantage minority.

Facts: Controlling shareholders created holding company and transferred their shares, leaving minority frozen out of liquidity opportunity.

Rule: Controlling shareholders' fiduciary duty runs directly to minority shareholders (not just to corporation) when they engage in conduct that harms minority.

Practice Tip: Cite Jones when managing spouse is majority shareholder and engages in freeze-out tactics (withholding dividends, refusing board seats, blocking access to information). Establishes that minority spouse-shareholder has direct claim, not just derivative.

Jara v. Suprema Meats, Inc. (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 1238

California Court of Appeal, Second District

Holding: Excessive officer compensation paid by controlling shareholders to themselves can support both derivative and direct claims by minority shareholders; court awarded attorney fees to minority shareholder for successfully enforcing inspection rights.

Facts: Controlling shareholders paid themselves salaries far exceeding market rates while refusing dividends to minority shareholders.

Rule: When controlling shareholders use corporate form to enrich themselves at expense of minority, courts will scrutinize compensation as potential breach of fiduciary duty.

Practice Tip: Use Jara in demand letters alleging managing spouse takes excessive salary/bonuses while refusing to distribute profits to minority spouse-shareholder. Also supports fee awards for inspection litigation.

Havlicek v. Coast-to-Coast Analytical Services, Inc. (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 1844

California Court of Appeal, First District

Holding: Trial court erred in denying shareholder inspection rights under Corp. Code Â§1601; court improperly applied Delaware law and failed to recognize breadth of California statutory inspection rights.

Facts: Minority shareholder sought to inspect corporate books and records; corporation resisted, claiming shareholder had improper motive.

Rule: California Â§1601 inspection rights are very broad and cannot be denied based on speculation about shareholder's motives, as long as purpose is "reasonably related to shareholder's interests."

Practice Tip: Havlicek is critical for California corporation inspection demands. Courts cannot deny inspection based on vague concerns; burden is on corporation to show request is clearly unrelated to shareholder interests.

Stephenson v. Drever (1997) 16 Cal.4th 1167

California Supreme Court

Holding: In closely held corporation with buy-sell agreement, valuation disputes and majority-minority dynamics are resolved by interpreting shareholder agreements and applying fiduciary principles.

Facts: Buy-sell agreement required valuation of departing shareholder's interest; majority and minority disputed valuation method.

Implication: Shareholder agreements are enforceable but will be interpreted against backdrop of fiduciary duties; courts won't allow controlling shareholders to manipulate valuation to minority's disadvantage.

Practice Tip: Useful when demand letter challenges valuation of business for buyout or dissolution purposes.
