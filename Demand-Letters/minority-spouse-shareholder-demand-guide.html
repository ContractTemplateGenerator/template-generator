üìö Legal Framework: The Foundation for Your Demand Letter

A minority spouse-shareholder demand letter draws authority from three distinct bodies of law: California Family Code (community property and marital fiduciary duties), California/Delaware corporate law (shareholder inspection and fiduciary duties), and common-law principles governing closely held businesses. Understanding where these overlap‚Äîand where they conflict‚Äîis critical to drafting a letter that judges and opposing counsel will take seriously.

üèõÔ∏è Part 1: California Family Code ‚Äî Marital Fiduciary Foundation

üìú Family Code ¬ß 760 ‚Äì Community Property Presumption

Rule: All property acquired by a married person during marriage while domiciled in California is community property, except as provided by statute (separate property).

Application to Business Interests: Shares, membership interests, and business equity acquired or grown during marriage are presumptively community assets, regardless of whose name is on the stock certificates.

Demand Letter Impact: You can assert standing as a community co-owner of the business, even if you only hold a minority of the record shares. The growth in value during marriage is community property subject to marital fiduciary duties.

üìú Family Code ¬ß 721 ‚Äì Fiduciary Relationship Between Spouses

Rule: Spouses are subject to the general rules governing fiduciary relationships that control the actions of persons occupying confidential relations with each other. This confidential relationship imposes a duty of the highest good faith and fair dealing on each spouse, subject to the same rights and duties of nonmarital business partners under Corp. Code ¬ß¬ß 16403‚Äì16404 (Uniform Partnership Act).

Specific Duties:

  • Provide full disclosure of all material facts and information regarding community assets
  • Account to the other spouse for any benefit or profit derived from transactions affecting the community estate
  • Not take unfair advantage of the other spouse in transactions between them

Demand Letter Impact: ¬ß721 lets you frame requests for business information as enforcement of ongoing statutory duties, not as favors or courtesy. The controlling spouse's refusal to disclose is itself a breach.

üìú Family Code ¬ß 1100 ‚Äì Management of Community Personal Property

¬ß1100(a)‚Äì(c): Either spouse has management and control of community personal property, but cannot make gifts or sell substantially all community personal property without written consent.

¬ß1100(d) ‚Äî Community Business Transactions: If a business constitutes all or substantially all of the community estate's personal property, the spouse operating the business has primary management and control, but must give the other spouse prior written notice of any:

  • Sale, lease, or exchange
  • Grant of a security interest
  • Other disposition of all or substantially all personal property used in the operation of the business

Violation Consequences: Transaction may be set aside by the non-managing spouse, and the managing spouse may be liable for breach of fiduciary duty under ¬ß1101.

Demand Letter Impact: You can cite ¬ß1100(d) to challenge major transactions done without notice‚Äîasset sales, large loans, pledges, or equity dilution.

üìú Family Code ¬ß 1101 ‚Äì Remedies for Breach of Fiduciary Duty

Cause of Action: A spouse has a claim for breach of fiduciary duty when the other spouse's breach results in impairment of the claiming spouse's one-half interest in the community estate.

Remedies:

  • ¬ß1101(g): Award to the injured spouse 50% of any undisclosed or misappropriated asset, plus attorney fees and costs
  • ¬ß1101(h): If breach involved fraud, oppression, or malice under Civ. Code ¬ß3294, court shall award 100% of the asset to the injured spouse, plus attorney fees and costs
  • ¬ß1101(a): Court may also order accounting, set aside transactions, and impose constructive trusts

Demand Letter Impact: These remedies create powerful settlement leverage. You can reference them as potential consequences if disclosure and corrective action are refused.

üí° Practice Tip: Schnabel v. Superior Court (1993)

In Schnabel, the California Supreme Court held that a non-record spouse in a dissolution proceeding could obtain broad discovery of a close corporation's business records where the shares were community property. The court emphasized the spouse's need for detailed financial information to protect their community interest.

Takeaway: Demand letters can cite Schnabel as authority that spouse-level information rights are real and recognized, not just theoretical.

üè¢ Part 2: California Corporate and LLC Law ‚Äî Inspection Rights

üìú Corporations Code ¬ß 1601 ‚Äì Shareholder Inspection Rights (CA Corporations)

Who Has Rights: Any shareholder of record.

What Can Be Inspected:

  • Accounting books and records
  • Minutes of proceedings of shareholders
  • Minutes of proceedings of the board of directors and committees
  • Records of any subsidiary corporation

Procedure: Written demand stating a purpose reasonably related to the shareholder's interests as a shareholder.

Timing: Inspection at any reasonable time during usual business hours, at the corporation's principal office.

Non-Waivable Right: ¬ß1601 rights cannot be limited by articles, bylaws, or shareholder agreements‚Äîthey are substantive statutory rights.

Demand Letter Impact: If you are a record shareholder (even a small minority), you have an absolute right to this inspection. Refusal sets up a summary enforcement action in Superior Court.

üìú Corporations Code ¬ß 1602 ‚Äì Director Inspection Rights

Who Has Rights: Any director.

Scope: Virtually absolute right to inspect and copy all corporate books, records, documents, and physical properties at any reasonable time.

Demand Letter Impact: If you are or were on the board, ¬ß1602 gives you even broader rights than ¬ß1601. Courts treat director inspection rights as essential to fiduciary oversight.

üìú Corporations Code ¬ß 17704.10 ‚Äì LLC Member Information Rights

Who Has Rights: Any member of a California LLC.

What Members Can Access:

  • List of members' names and addresses
  • Copy of operating agreement
  • Copy of the LLC's tax returns for the past three years
  • Copy of financial statements
  • Other information regarding the affairs of the LLC as is just and reasonable

Procedure: Written demand with proper purpose.

Penalties for Noncompliance: LLC may be liable for per-day penalties.

Demand Letter Impact: LLC member inspection rights are broader and less restricted than corporate shareholder rights‚Äîeasier to enforce.

ü¶Ö Part 3: Delaware Law ‚Äî For DE Corporations and LLCs

Many California family businesses are actually Delaware entities (for tax or investor reasons). The internal affairs doctrine means Delaware law governs corporate governance‚Äîincluding inspection rights‚Äîeven for California resident shareholders.

üìú DGCL ¬ß 220 ‚Äì Stockholder Inspection of Books and Records (DE Corporations)

Who Has Rights: Any stockholder (no minimum ownership threshold).

What Can Be Inspected:

  • Stock ledger
  • List of stockholders
  • Other books and records of the corporation

Procedure:

  • Written demand under oath
  • State a proper purpose with reasonable particularity
  • Describe the documents sought with reasonable particularity

Proper Purposes (Recognized by DE Courts):

  • Investigating mismanagement or waste
  • Valuing shares
  • Investigating potential wrongdoing by officers/directors
  • Determining whether to pursue derivative claims

Litigation Process: If corporation refuses, stockholder files ¬ß220 action in Delaware Chancery Court. Cases typically resolved in 60‚Äì90 days‚Äîmuch faster than plenary litigation.

Fee-Shifting: Losing party often pays winner's attorney fees.

Demand Letter Impact: Delaware courts expect stockholders to use ¬ß220 "tools at hand" before filing derivative suits. A well-crafted ¬ß220 demand letter shows you're following proper procedure.

üìú Delaware LLC Act ¬ß 18-305 ‚Äì LLC Member Inspection Rights

Default Rule: Members have inspection rights to books and records for purposes reasonably related to the member's interest as a member.

Flexibility: Unlike ¬ß220 for corporations, Delaware LLC operating agreements can modify or eliminate default inspection rights.

Demand Letter Impact: Always read the operating agreement first. If it restricts inspection rights, you may need to rely more heavily on California Family Code ¬ß721 duties rather than corporate-law inspection rights.

üó∫Ô∏è Dual Analysis for Delaware Entities Owned by California Spouses

When a Delaware corporation or LLC is owned by California spouses, you must analyze rights under both jurisdictions:

  • Delaware law governs internal corporate affairs: inspection rights, derivative vs. direct claims, fiduciary duties of directors and officers
  • California Family Code governs ownership characterization (community vs. separate property) and marital fiduciary duties between spouses
  • Demand letters should invoke both: DGCL ¬ß220 / DE LLC ¬ß18-305 for corporate inspection AND Fam. Code ¬ß¬ß721/1101 for marital disclosure and accountability

‚öñÔ∏è Part 4: Controlling Shareholder Fiduciary Duties ‚Äî Jones and Jara

Jones v. H.F. Ahmanson & Co. (1969) 1 Cal.3d 93

California Supreme Court ‚Äî Foundational case on majority-minority fiduciary duties

Holding: Controlling shareholders owe a fiduciary duty to minority shareholders and may not use their control to benefit themselves to the detriment of the minority.

Standard: Majority shareholders must act in a manner that is "fair, just, and equitable" toward the minority.

Examples of Breach:

  • Creating a holding company and issuing shares to themselves while excluding minority
  • Self-dealing transactions that shift value away from the corporation
  • Excessive compensation to controlling shareholders
  • Freeze-out tactics designed to force minority to sell at unfair prices

Demand Letter Impact: Cite Jones when complaining about self-dealing compensation, free use of corporate assets, or transactions structured to benefit the controlling spouse at the expense of the business and minority.

Jara v. Suprema Meats, Inc. (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 1238

California Court of Appeal ‚Äî Individual vs. derivative claims for fiduciary breach

Holding: Minority shareholder may bring an individual (direct) action for breach of fiduciary duty where controlling insiders allegedly paid themselves excessive compensation and diverted corporate opportunities.

Significance: Not all fiduciary-duty claims must be brought derivatively. Where the harm falls disproportionately on the minority (e.g., majority gets excess pay while minority gets no distributions), the claim is direct.

Demand Letter Impact: Frame concerns like excessive compensation, related-party deals, and unequal distributions as direct fiduciary breaches, which allows individual recovery rather than just corporate recovery.

‚úçÔ∏è Drafting the Demand Letter: Component by Component

A well-drafted minority spouse-shareholder demand letter follows a structured progression from establishing authority ‚Üí stating facts ‚Üí making demands ‚Üí warning of consequences ‚Üí offering resolution. Each section has a specific legal function.

üìù Component 1: Opening & Identification of Capacities

Purpose

Establish your dual standing: as a corporate stakeholder with statutory rights AND as a spouse with marital fiduciary rights.

Key Elements
  • Your name and relationship to recipient (spouse)
  • Your shareholder/member/director status with ownership percentage
  • Community property interest under Fam. Code ¬ß760
  • Entity name, state of incorporation, nature of business
Sample Language

"I write in my dual capacity as: (1) a record holder of [X]% of the common stock of [Company Name], a [Delaware/California] corporation; and (2) as your spouse, with a community-property interest in [Company Name] pursuant to California Family Code ¬ß 760. This letter constitutes a formal demand under (a) [Corporations Code ¬ß 1601 / DGCL ¬ß 220], (b) Family Code ¬ß¬ß 721 and 1101, and (c) the common-law fiduciary duties owed by controlling shareholders to minority shareholders under Jones v. H.F. Ahmanson & Co. and related authority."

üìù Component 2: Statement of Legal Framework

Purpose

Educate the recipient (and their attorney) about the legal authorities you're invoking. This is not a courtesy request‚Äîit's enforcement of statutory and fiduciary duties.

Subsections

A. Marital Fiduciary Duties (Fam. Code ¬ß721): Cite the "highest good faith and fair dealing" standard and the duty to provide full disclosure.

B. Community Business Transactions (Fam. Code ¬ß1100(d)): Explain the prior-written-notice requirement for major business transactions.

C. Shareholder Inspection Rights (¬ß1601 or ¬ß220): Quote the statute showing you have a right to inspect for proper purposes.

D. Controlling Shareholder Duties (Jones, Jara): Cite case law prohibiting self-dealing and freeze-outs.

Why This Matters

Many demand letters fail because they read like angry venting. Citing statutes and case law immediately signals: "I have a lawyer, I understand my rights, and I'm prepared to enforce them."

üìù Component 3: Statement of Proper Purposes

Purpose

Both ¬ß1601 and DGCL ¬ß220 require a "proper purpose" for inspection. You must state it explicitly and tie it to recognized categories.

Recognized Proper Purposes
  • Investigating potential mismanagement or waste
  • Investigating breaches of fiduciary duty by officers, directors, or controlling shareholders
  • Valuing shares for potential sale, buyout, or dissolution
  • Determining whether to initiate derivative litigation
  • Protecting my community-property interest under California Family Code
Sample Language

"My purposes for this inspection demand are proper and reasonably related to my interests as a shareholder and spouse, specifically: (1) investigating potential mismanagement, waste, self-dealing, and breaches of fiduciary duty; (2) evaluating the financial condition of [Company] and the propriety of compensation and related-party transactions; (3) determining whether grounds exist for derivative or direct claims under California and Delaware law; and (4) protecting my one-half community-property interest in [Company] under Family Code ¬ß¬ß 760, 1100, and 1101."

üìù Component 4: Factual Background (Without Defamation Landmines)

Purpose

Provide specific factual basis for your concerns, demonstrating this is not a fishing expedition.

What to Include
  • Timeline of marriage, business formation, your acquisition of shares
  • Your historical involvement (or exclusion) from governance
  • Specific transactions or patterns that concern you:
    • Compensation levels vs. distributions
    • Major asset sales, loans, or pledges done without notice
    • Related-party transactions
    • Denial of access to financial information
  • Any prior requests for information and how they were handled
What NOT to Include

Avoid: Conclusory accusations of fraud, embezzlement, or criminal conduct unless you have hard evidence. Describe patterns and facts, not motives.

Example: Good vs. Bad
‚ùå Bad (Risky) ‚úÖ Good (Safe)
"You are stealing from the company and committing tax fraud." "Company tax returns show your salary increased from $120K to $420K over three years, while shareholder distributions ceased entirely. I seek documentation to understand this disparity."
"You're hiding assets in offshore accounts." "I have been unable to locate documentation for several wire transfers listed on bank statements. I request copies of all inter-company and related-party transaction agreements."

üìù Component 5: Document and Information Requests

Purpose

Specify the categories of documents you're demanding, tied to your stated purposes and statutory inspection rights.

Standard Categories (Tailor to Your Situation)
  • Corporate Formation & Governance: Articles/certificate of incorporation, bylaws/operating agreement, current cap table, shareholder/member list
  • Meeting Records: Minutes of all board, committee, and shareholder/member meetings for past [X] years
  • Financial Records: Audited and unaudited financial statements, balance sheets, income statements, cash flow statements, bank statements, general ledger
  • Tax Records: Federal and state tax returns (corporate and personal if relevant to ¬ß1101 claims) for past [X] years
  • Compensation & Benefits: Employment agreements, bonus plans, deferred compensation agreements, benefits documentation for all officers and directors
  • Related-Party Transactions: All contracts, leases, loans, or other agreements between [Company] and you, your affiliates, or entities you control
  • Major Transactions: Documentation for any sale, lease, pledge, or encumbrance of business assets exceeding $[threshold] in past [X] years, including ¬ß1100(d) written notices (if applicable)
Format & Production

Request production in native electronic format where applicable (e.g., Excel files, PDFs with searchable text). Specify a reasonable time frame: 21‚Äì30 days for production, 10‚Äì15 days for written response confirming compliance or stating objections.

üìù Component 6: Notice of Potential Claims and Remedies

Purpose

Signal the consequences of non-compliance without making empty threats. This is where ¬ß1101 remedies, fee-shifting, and fiduciary-breach damages come in.

Tone & Language

Frame as "potential consequences" rather than certainties. You're not filing suit yet‚Äîyou're explaining what the law allows if your concerns are validated and if disclosure is refused.

Sample Language

"Please be advised that refusal to comply with this inspection demand, or any attempt to conceal or misrepresent information, may give rise to claims under Family Code ¬ß 1101, which provides for remedies including 50% of any undisclosed or misappropriated asset (¬ß 1101(g)), or 100% of such asset if the breach involved fraud, oppression, or malice (¬ß 1101(h)), plus attorney fees and costs. Additionally, unjustified refusal to permit inspection under [¬ß 1601 / DGCL ¬ß 220] may result in a summary enforcement action with fee-shifting to the prevailing party. I also reserve all rights to pursue direct and derivative claims for breach of fiduciary duty under Jones v. H.F. Ahmanson & Co., Jara v. Suprema Meats, and related authority."

üìù Component 7: Preservation of Evidence Notice

Purpose

Trigger litigation-hold obligations and create a record for spoliation sanctions if documents are destroyed or altered.

Key Elements

Explicitly state that litigation is reasonably foreseeable, and that all relevant documents and electronically stored information (ESI) must be preserved.

Sample Language

"This letter constitutes formal notice that litigation is reasonably foreseeable concerning the matters discussed herein. You and [Company Name] are therefore under a legal duty to preserve all documents, records, electronically stored information, and tangible things relating to: [list topics‚Äîe.g., compensation, related-party transactions, board decisions, financial records]. This includes, but is not limited to, emails, text messages, financial records, corporate minutes, contracts, and any other materials in your possession, custody, or control. Any destruction, alteration, or concealment of such materials may result in spoliation sanctions, adverse-inference instructions, and independent tort claims under California law."

üìù Component 8: Demand for Response and Meet-and-Confer

Purpose

Set clear deadlines and offer an opportunity to resolve informally, which strengthens your fee-shifting and good-faith arguments later.

Sample Language

"Please provide a written response to this letter within 15 days, confirming your intent to comply with this inspection demand and stating any objections with specificity. Full document production should be completed within 30 days. I am willing to meet and confer in good faith to address any reasonable concerns about timing, format, or scope, and to discuss potential resolution of the underlying issues. However, failure to respond substantively, or unjustified refusal to permit inspection, will leave me no choice but to pursue all available legal remedies without further notice."

üìù Component 9: Delivery Method

üí° How to Send the Letter for Maximum Seriousness

To ensure the letter is taken seriously and to create an unimpeachable record:

  • Email (with read receipt) to recipient and, if applicable, to the company's registered agent
  • Certified mail, return receipt requested to recipient's home address and company's principal office
  • If the entity is a Delaware corporation/LLC, consider also sending to the Delaware registered agent

Why dual delivery: Email shows immediate notice; certified mail creates formal proof of delivery. If they later claim "I never received it," you have receipts.

üèõÔ∏è California Supreme Court: Jones v. H.F. Ahmanson & Co.

Jones v. H.F. Ahmanson & Co. (1969) 1 Cal.3d 93

California Supreme Court ‚Äî Landmark case establishing controlling shareholder fiduciary duties

Facts: Majority shareholders of a savings and loan association created a holding company and sold their shares into the public holding company at a premium, while refusing to allow minority shareholders the same opportunity. Minority shareholders sued for breach of fiduciary duty.

Holding: Controlling shareholders owe a fiduciary duty to minority shareholders and may not use their power to benefit themselves at the minority's expense. The duty is to act in a manner "fair, just, and equitable" toward minority shareholders.

Key Quote: "The comprehensive rule of good faith and inherent fairness to the minority in any transaction where control of the corporation is material properly governs controlling shareholders in this state."

Application to Demand Letters: Cite Jones when alleging:

  • Self-dealing transactions structured to benefit controlling shareholder
  • Opportunities diverted from corporation to controlling shareholder's other entities
  • Excessive compensation to majority while minority receives no distributions
  • Freeze-out tactics or denial of governance participation

üèõÔ∏è California Court of Appeal: Jara v. Suprema Meats, Inc.

Jara v. Suprema Meats, Inc. (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 1238

California Court of Appeal ‚Äî Direct vs. derivative claims for fiduciary breach

Facts: Minority shareholder alleged that controlling shareholders paid themselves excessive salaries and diverted corporate opportunities, while minority received no return on investment.

Holding: Minority shareholder could bring an individual (direct) action for breach of fiduciary duty, not just a derivative claim, where the harm fell disproportionately on the plaintiff as a minority shareholder.

Significance: Overturns the assumption that all fiduciary-duty claims in closely held corporations are derivative. Where controlling shareholders structure transactions to harm the minority as minority shareholders (not just as part of the general body of shareholders), the claim is direct.

Application to Demand Letters: Use Jara to argue that:

  • Excessive compensation to majority + zero distributions to minority = direct harm
  • You're not seeking recovery "for the corporation"‚Äîyou're seeking individual relief for breach of duties owed specifically to you
  • This allows you to recover personally, not just benefit the corporate treasury

üèõÔ∏è California Supreme Court: Schnabel v. Superior Court

Schnabel v. Superior Court (1993) 5 Cal.4th 704

California Supreme Court ‚Äî Spousal discovery rights in community-property business disputes

Facts: In a marital dissolution, non-managing spouse sought broad discovery of closely held corporation's financial records. Corporation resisted, arguing spousal status doesn't override corporate privileges.

Holding: Non-record spouse in dissolution proceeding is entitled to extensive discovery of corporate records where the business is community property, because the spouse has a direct ownership interest via community property law.

Key Principle: Corporate formalities and separate legal existence do not shield the managing spouse from marital disclosure obligations under Family Code.

Application to Demand Letters: Cite Schnabel for the proposition that:

  • "The corporation is a legal fiction; it does not transform the managing spouse's obligations to the marital community."
  • Even if you're not on corporate documents, you have discovery rights as a community co-owner.
  • Refusal to disclose corporate information to spouse is a breach of marital fiduciary duty.

‚öñÔ∏è Family Code ¬ß 1101 Breach Cases

In re Marriage of Haines (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 277

California Court of Appeal ‚Äî Undue influence presumption in interspousal transactions

Holding: Where one spouse gains an advantage in an interspousal transaction, a presumption of undue influence arises due to the confidential relationship under Family Code ¬ß 721.

Application: If the managing spouse caused you to sign documents (e.g., dilutive stock issuances, waivers of inspection rights, below-market buyouts) without full disclosure, you can argue the transaction is voidable under Haines + ¬ß 721.

In re Marriage of Brewer & Federici (2001) 93 Cal.App.4th 1334

California Court of Appeal ‚Äî Affirmative duty to disclose community assets

Holding: Managing spouse has an affirmative, sua sponte duty to obtain and disclose information concerning the existence and valuation of community assets‚Äînot just respond when asked.

Application: Even if you never explicitly asked for financial information, the managing spouse was required under ¬ß 721 to provide it. Failure to do so is a breach supporting ¬ß 1101 remedies.

ü¶Ö Delaware ¬ß 220 Inspection Cases

Delaware Chancery Court ¬ß 220 Practice

Numerous Delaware Chancery Court decisions emphasize "tools at hand" and proper-purpose requirements

Key Principles from Delaware Courts:

  • "Tools at hand": Stockholders are expected to use ¬ß 220 inspection before filing derivative or plenary suits. Failure to do so can result in dismissal.
  • Proper purpose broadly construed: Investigating mismanagement, valuing shares, and determining whether to sue are all proper purposes.
  • Reasonable particularity: You don't need to prove wrongdoing to get inspection‚Äîyou just need a credible basis to investigate.
  • Fee-shifting: Losing party (corporation or stockholder) often pays the winner's fees‚Äîso unjustified resistance is expensive.
  • Speed: ¬ß 220 cases typically resolve in 60‚Äì90 days, much faster than full litigation.

Application to Demand Letters: For Delaware entities, emphasize that your demand letter is not optional‚Äîit's the procedural prerequisite Delaware courts require before you can sue.

üéØ Strategic Takeaway: Stacking Remedies

  • Direct fiduciary-duty damages under Jones and Jara
